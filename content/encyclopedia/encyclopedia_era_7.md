# Era 7: First Democratic Republic (1918-1921)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The First Democratic Republic of Georgia (1918-1921) was a brief but extraordinary experiment in democracy and national self-determination. Born from the chaos of the Russian Revolution and World War I, the Georgian Republic lasted only three years before being crushed by the Red Army, but in that short time it achieved a remarkable record of democratic governance, social reform, and international recognition. For Georgian nationalists, this period represents both a source of pride — proof that Georgians are capable of building a modern democratic state — and a source of sorrow — a reminder of the independence that was lost.

The republic emerged from the collapse of the Russian Empire. In the wake of the February Revolution of 1917, Georgia (along with Armenia and Azerbaijan) formed the Transcaucasian Democratic Federative Republic in April 1918. This fragile union quickly fell apart due to irreconcilable differences between the three national groups, and on May 26, 1918, Georgia declared its independence. The new republic faced almost unimaginable challenges: it was surrounded by hostile powers (the Bolsheviks to the north, the Ottoman Turks to the south, and the Armenians and Azerbaijanis, with whom Georgia had territorial disputes, to the east and west); its economy was devastated by war; and its territory was occupied by Turkish and German troops.

Despite these challenges, the Georgian Republic made remarkable progress. Its government, led by the Social Democratic (Menshevik) Party, established a multi-party parliamentary democracy with universal suffrage (including women's suffrage — making Georgia one of the first countries in Europe to grant women the vote), land reform, labor protections, and an independent judiciary. The republic was recognized by the major Allied powers and was admitted to the League of Nations. Its leaders — Noe Zhordania, Noe Ramishvili, Evgeni Gegechkori, and others — were committed social democrats who believed in building a just society through democratic means.

The republic's downfall came in February-March 1921, when the Red Army invaded Georgia in violation of the 1920 Soviet-Georgian peace treaty. The Georgian army, weakened by months of fighting against Armenian and Bolshevik forces, was overwhelmed by the superior numbers and equipment of the Soviet forces. The Georgian government fled into exile, and Georgia became a Soviet republic. The three-year experiment in democracy was over — but its memory would endure as a powerful symbol of what Georgia could be and as a source of legitimacy for the independence movement that would emerge seventy years later.

## KEY FIGURES

### Noe Zhordania (1868-1953)

Noe Zhordania was the leading figure of the First Democratic Republic and served as its prime minister from 1918 to 1921. A committed social democrat, Zhordania had been a leader of the Georgian Menshevik movement since the 1890s. Unlike the Bolsheviks, the Mensheviks believed in achieving socialism through democratic means rather than violent revolution, and Zhordania's government attempted to implement social reforms while maintaining a multi-party democratic system.

Zhordania's government faced impossible challenges — foreign invasions, economic collapse, territorial disputes — and while it made significant achievements, it was ultimately unable to secure the republic's survival. After the Bolshevik conquest in 1921, Zhordania and other government leaders fled to France, where they established a government-in-exile that continued to advocate for Georgian independence until the 1950s. Zhordania died in exile in 1953, never having returned to his homeland. His legacy is complex: he was a committed democrat who built a remarkable (if brief) democratic state, but his government's inability to form effective military alliances ultimately doomed the republic.

### Noe Ramishvili (1881-1930)

Noe Ramishvili was the first prime minister of independent Georgia, serving from May to July 1918 before handing the premiership to Zhordania. A founding member of the Georgian Social Democratic Party, Ramishvili was a key organizer and strategist of the independence movement. He later served as minister of the interior, where he was responsible for internal security during the republic's difficult early years.

Ramishvili was assassinated in 1930 in Paris by a Soviet agent, one of many victims of the Soviet campaign against Georgian political exiles. His murder underscored the danger that the Soviet regime posed to anyone who challenged its authority, even in exile. Ramishvili is remembered as one of the founding fathers of Georgian democratic statehood and as a martyr to the cause of Georgian independence.

### Ekaterine (Kato) Meskhi-Makashidze (1871-1956)

Kato Meskhi was a pioneering Georgian feminist and social activist who played a significant role in the social reforms of the First Republic. She was one of the leaders of the Georgian women's movement, which successfully campaigned for women's suffrage and equal rights in the new republic. Georgia's 1918 declaration of independence and subsequent constitution granted women the right to vote and to hold public office, making Georgia one of the first countries in Europe to do so.

Kato Meskhi's activism encompassed education reform, workers' rights, and the promotion of Georgian culture. She represented a generation of Georgian women who combined a commitment to national independence with a progressive social vision. Her work demonstrated that the Georgian national movement was not solely a male-dominated enterprise and that Georgian women were active participants in the struggle for democracy and self-determination.

### General Giorgi Kvinitadze (1874-1970)

General Giorgi Kvinitadze was the commander-in-chief of the Georgian armed forces during the First Republic and one of the most capable Georgian military leaders of the 20th century. A professional soldier who had served in the Imperial Russian Army, Kvinitadze organized the Georgian National Guard in 1917 and led Georgian forces through the multiple conflicts of the independence period — against Ottoman and German forces, against Armenian and Azerbaijani rivals, and finally against the Red Army.

Kvinitadze was a skilled and courageous commander, but he was hampered by inadequate resources, internal political divisions, and the reluctance of Georgia's Western allies to provide military assistance. He led the desperate defense against the Bolshevik invasion in February 1921, conducting a fighting retreat as the Red Army advanced on Tbilisi. After the republic's collapse, Kvinitadze went into exile in France, where he lived until his death in 1970. His memoirs, published posthumously, provide a detailed account of the military history of the First Republic.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Declaration of Independence (May 26, 1918)

On May 26, 1918, the National Council of Georgia declared the independence of the Democratic Republic of Georgia. The declaration came after the collapse of the Transcaucasian Federation and amid the advance of Ottoman Turkish forces into the Caucasus. The Act of Independence proclaimed Georgia's right to self-determination and committed the new republic to democratic governance, the rule of law, and the protection of minority rights.

The declaration was a moment of extraordinary significance — the first time Georgia had been an independent state since the Russian annexation of 1801, and the first time in modern history that Georgia declared itself a democratic republic. The challenges facing the new state were immense: foreign troops occupied parts of Georgian territory, the economy was in ruins, and the population was exhausted by years of war. But the declaration also expressed enormous hope and determination — a belief that Georgia could build a modern, democratic, European-oriented state.

### The Treaty of Moscow (1920)

The Treaty of Moscow, signed on May 7, 1920, was a peace agreement between the Georgian Democratic Republic and Soviet Russia. Under the treaty, Soviet Russia recognized Georgia's independence and renounced claims to Georgian territory. In return, Georgia agreed to allow Bolshevik political activity within its borders and to refrain from hosting anti-Soviet forces. The treaty was hailed as a diplomatic triumph for Georgia, as it appeared to secure Soviet recognition of Georgian sovereignty.

In reality, the treaty was a tactical maneuver by the Bolsheviks, who were not yet ready to invade Georgia but had no intention of respecting Georgian independence in the long term. The Bolshevik government continued to support revolutionary activity within Georgia, and within less than a year, the Red Army would invade in violation of the treaty's terms. The Treaty of Moscow thus serves as a cautionary tale about the limitations of paper agreements when the parties have fundamentally different intentions.

### The Soviet-Georgian War and the Battle of Tbilisi (February-March 1921)

The Red Army invaded Georgia on February 11, 1921, crossing the border from Azerbaijan and advancing rapidly toward Tbilisi. The Georgian army, under General Kvinitadze, fought a series of desperate rearguard actions but was overwhelmed by the superior Soviet forces, which enjoyed overwhelming numerical and material superiority. The decisive battle for Tbilisi took place on February 24-25, 1921, when Soviet forces broke through Georgian defenses and entered the capital.

The Georgian government, led by Noe Zhordania, evacuated to Batumi on the Black Sea coast and then went into exile. The last Georgian forces in Batumi were evacuated by sea in March 1921, ending the First Republic's existence as a de facto state. The Soviet-Georgian War was a tragedy for Georgian democracy and a turning point in Georgian history, beginning the seventy-year Soviet period. The brief, valiant defense of Georgian independence against overwhelming odds would become a powerful part of Georgian national memory.

### Admission to the League of Nations (1921)

Despite its short existence, the Democratic Republic of Georgia achieved international recognition that would later be cited by independence advocates. On January 27, 1921 — just weeks before the Bolshevik invasion — Georgia was admitted to the League of Nations as the 39th member state. This recognition by the international community affirmed Georgia's status as a sovereign state and represented a significant diplomatic achievement for a small, newly independent country in a hostile geopolitical environment. Georgia's admission to the League of Nations was later invoked by Georgian independence activists in the 1980s-1990s as evidence that Georgia had a legitimate right to restored sovereignty.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### Democratic Constitution and Governance

The First Republic's most significant achievement was the establishment of a functioning democratic state. The constitution, adopted in February 1921 (just days before the Bolshevik conquest), established a parliamentary republic with separation of powers, an independent judiciary, universal suffrage (including for women), proportional representation, and protections for civil liberties and minority rights. The republic held free elections, maintained a multi-party system, and respected the autonomy of local governments. This record of democratic governance, however brief, provided a powerful model for future Georgian state-building.

### Land Reform

The Social Democratic government implemented a sweeping land reform that redistributed land from large estates to peasant farmers. The reform was one of the most comprehensive land reforms in Europe at the time and addressed one of the most pressing social issues in Georgian society. The redistribution of land to the peasantry gave millions of Georgians a stake in the new state and demonstrated the government's commitment to social justice. However, the reform also created economic disruptions and was still being implemented when the Bolsheviks seized power.

### Educational and Cultural Reform

The First Republic made significant investments in education and culture. The Tbilisi State University, founded in 1918, became a center of Georgian academic life and produced generations of scholars and intellectuals. The republic promoted Georgian-language education, supported the arts, and encouraged the development of a modern Georgian national culture. The freedom of the press and of intellectual expression during this period — however constrained by economic conditions and external threats — was unprecedented in Georgian history and stood in sharp contrast to the censorship that would follow under Soviet rule.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### Tbilisi State University Building

Tbilisi State University (TSU) was founded on February 8, 1918, making it the oldest university in the Caucasus region and one of the most important cultural institutions in Georgia. The university was established through the efforts of a group of Georgian intellectuals led by the historian Ivane Javakhishvili, who became its first rector. The university's main building, a neoclassical structure on Chavchavadze Avenue (now Rustaveli Avenue), became a symbol of Georgian intellectual aspirations and the nation's commitment to higher education. TSU has produced many of Georgia's most important scholars, writers, and political leaders, including the Soviet leader Joseph Stalin (who studied there briefly), the writer Nodar Dumbadze, and the dissident Zviad Gamsakhurdia.

### The Parliament Building (Former Noble Gymnasium)

The Georgian National Council and later the Constituent Assembly of the First Republic met in various locations in Tbilisi, but one of the most significant was the building of the former Noble Gymnasium on present-day Rustaveli Avenue. This building, an elegant neoclassical structure, was the site of the debates and decisions that shaped the short-lived republic. It was here that the Act of Independence was discussed and where the constitution was drafted. The building later served various functions during the Soviet period and remains an important landmark associated with Georgia's democratic heritage.

### Batumi Boulevard

The Batumi Boulevard, a waterfront promenade along the Black Sea coast, was developed during the late Russian period and the First Republic into one of the most elegant public spaces in the Caucasus. Lined with exotic plants, ornamental gardens, cafes, and recreational facilities, the boulevard reflected the cosmopolitan character of Batumi, which was Georgia's most important port and a major international trading center during this period. The boulevard was expanded and beautified during the First Republic as part of the government's efforts to develop Batumi as a modern European-style port city.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### The Georgian National Guard

The Georgian National Guard, established in 1917 under the command of General Kvinitadze, was the core of the First Republic's armed forces. Initially a militia force composed of volunteers, the National Guard was gradually professionalized and expanded. By 1921, it numbered approximately 20,000-30,000 men, organized into infantry, cavalry, and artillery units. The Guard was equipped with a mixture of weapons inherited from the former Imperial Russian Army, captured from enemy forces, and purchased from abroad (including some aircraft).

### Artillery and Small Arms

The Georgian army was primarily equipped with weapons from the Imperial Russian arsenal: Mosin-Nagant rifles, Maxim machine guns, and various calibers of artillery. These weapons were adequate for the conflicts of the period but were increasingly outmatched by the more modern equipment of the Red Army. The Georgians also had a small air force with a handful of reconnaissance aircraft. Ammunition and spare parts were chronic problems, as the republic lacked the industrial capacity to manufacture its own weapons and was dependent on foreign purchases that were often blocked by the surrounding hostile powers.

### Fortifications and Defensive Positions

During the brief existence of the republic, Georgian military engineers constructed defensive positions along the borders with Soviet Russia and in the approaches to Tbilisi. These included trenches, barbed wire entanglements, machine gun nests, and artillery emplacements. The defensive lines were designed to slow a Soviet advance and allow time for diplomatic intervention by the Western powers — a strategy that ultimately failed when the Red Army's rapid advance overwhelmed the Georgian defenses before international pressure could be brought to bear.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

The brief period of the First Republic did not significantly change Georgian clothing styles, but it did see the emergence of a new urban professional class whose dress reflected European influences. Men in the cities increasingly wore suits and ties for business and government work, while the chokha was reserved for ceremonial occasions. Women's fashion became more Western, with shorter hemlines and simpler silhouettes reflecting the modernizing spirit of the era. In rural areas, traditional dress remained the norm.

### Food and Daily Life

Daily life in the First Republic was dominated by the economic hardships of the postwar period. Food shortages were common, and many families struggled to obtain basic necessities. The government implemented rationing and price controls to manage the crisis. Despite the hardships, the urban cultural life of Tbilisi and other cities remained vibrant — cafes, theaters, and newspapers continued to operate, and the intellectual and artistic life of the country showed remarkable resilience. The Georgian tradition of hospitality and communal dining helped sustain social bonds during this difficult period.

### Social Structure

The First Republic introduced significant changes in Georgian social structure. The land reform created a class of small peasant proprietors where large estates had previously dominated. Universal suffrage and political representation gave women and previously marginalized groups a voice in public life for the first time. The urban middle class of professionals, bureaucrats, and small business owners grew, though it remained a small proportion of the total population. The multiethnic character of Georgian society — with substantial Armenian, Azerbaijani, Russian, Greek, and Jewish communities — was formally recognized in the republic's commitment to minority rights.

### Political Culture

The political culture of the First Republic was characterized by vigorous debate, free elections, and a commitment to democratic norms. The Social Democratic Party (Mensheviks) was the dominant political force, but opposition parties — including the National Democrats, the Federalists, and the Socialist Federalists — operated freely and participated in parliamentary politics. The press was free, and newspapers represented a wide range of political views. This open political culture stood in sharp contrast to both the authoritarian Tsarist regime that preceded the republic and the totalitarian Soviet regime that followed it.

## GLOSSARY

**Sakartvelos Demokratiuli Respublika** — The Democratic Republic of Georgia (1918-1921), Georgia's first modern independent state.

**Mensheviki** — The Georgian Social Democratic (Menshevik) Party, which governed the First Republic. Unlike the Russian Bolsheviks, the Georgian Mensheviks were committed to democratic socialism.

**Amtkhverebi** — "Representatives." The members of the Georgian National Council and Constituent Assembly during the First Republic.

**Tbilisis Universiteti** — Tbilisi State University, founded in 1918 as the first university in the Caucasus.

**Eris Mshvenieria** — "Popular representation." The concept of democratic governance that the First Republic sought to establish.

**Sabchota Kavkasi** — "Soviet Caucasus." The name of the Bolshevik newspaper that was the mouthpiece of the underground communist movement in Georgia.

**Gamgeoba** — Local government or administration. The First Republic devolved significant powers to local governments.

**Sakartvelos Dasisi** — The National Council of Georgia, which declared independence in 1918.

**Ganis Mkurnali** — "Member of Parliament." The elected representatives who served in the Constituent Assembly of the First Republic.

**Mimokhilva** — "Independence." The central aspiration and achievement of the First Republic.
