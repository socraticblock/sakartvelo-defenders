# Era 8: Soviet Century (1921-1991)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The Soviet period (1921-1991) was the longest and most transformative era in modern Georgian history. For seventy years, Georgia existed as a constituent republic of the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics, undergoing radical changes in its political system, economy, social structure, and culture. The Soviet era brought industrialization, mass education, and the creation of a modern infrastructure — but it also brought political repression, censorship, the suppression of religious freedom, and the systematic effort to remake Georgian identity within the framework of Soviet ideology. It was also the era during which Joseph Stalin (born Ioseb Jughashvili in the Georgian town of Gori) rose to become the absolute ruler of the Soviet Union, a fact that gives the Georgian experience of the Soviet period a unique and deeply ambivalent character.

The early Soviet period (1921-1930s) was characterized by the consolidation of Bolshevik power through often brutal means. The Red Army's conquest of Georgia in 1921 was followed by a series of uprisings against Soviet rule — the most serious being the 1924 August Uprising, which was crushed with enormous violence, resulting in thousands of executions and deportations. The collectivization of agriculture in the early 1930s devastated the Georgian countryside, and the Great Purge of 1937-1938 decimated the Georgian Communist Party and intelligentsia. These were years of terror and suffering, and they left deep scars on Georgian society.

The middle decades of the Soviet period (1940s-1960s) saw the Soviet Union's victory in World War II (the Great Patriotic War, in Soviet terminology), which was a defining experience for the entire Soviet Union and for Georgia in particular. Hundreds of thousands of Georgians fought and died in the war, and Georgia's economy was mobilized for the war effort. The postwar period brought reconstruction, further industrialization, and — during the Khrushchev "thaw" of the late 1950s and early 1960s — a degree of cultural relaxation. The death of Stalin in 1953 was a watershed moment, and the subsequent denunciation of Stalin's crimes by Khrushchev created a complex situation for Georgians, many of whom had viewed Stalin with pride as a Georgian who had ruled the world's largest country.

The late Soviet period (1970s-1980s) saw growing tensions between Georgian national consciousness and Soviet ideology. Economic stagnation, corruption, and the perception of cultural Russification fueled nationalist sentiment. The April 9, 1989 tragedy — when Soviet troops violently dispersed a peaceful pro-independence demonstration in Tbilisi, killing 21 people — was the pivotal event that galvanized the Georgian independence movement and led directly to the declaration of independence in April 1991. The Soviet century thus ended as it had begun, with Georgian aspirations for freedom met with Russian violence.

This era must be presented factually. Stalin's rise from Georgian origins to absolute power is a historical reality. The purges he orchestrated killed many thousands of Georgians — including Old Bolsheviks who had helped bring the Soviets to power, Georgian intellectuals, clergy, and ordinary citizens. His policies of collectivization and industrialization brought both development and suffering. The Georgian experience of the Soviet period is one of genuine achievement alongside genuine tragedy, and both must be acknowledged.

## KEY FIGURES

### Joseph Stalin (Ioseb Jughashvili) (1878-1953)

Joseph Stalin, born Ioseb Besarionis dze Jughashvili in Gori, Georgia, was the General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union from 1922 until his death in 1953 and the de facto dictator of the Soviet Union from the late 1920s onward. His policies of rapid industrialization, agricultural collectivization, political purges, and victory in World War II shaped the course of the 20th century and caused the deaths of millions of people.

Stalin's relationship with Georgia was complex. He was Georgian by birth and culture — he spoke Georgian with his family throughout his life and retained a fondness for Georgian wine, food, and poetry. Some Georgians took pride in a Georgian ruling the Soviet Union. However, Stalin's policies devastated Georgia as they did the rest of the Soviet Union. The collectivization campaigns of the early 1930s caused famines and social upheaval in the Georgian countryside. The Great Purge of 1937-1938 killed thousands of Georgian party members, intellectuals, and ordinary citizens. Georgian churches and monasteries were destroyed, clergy were arrested and executed, and religious practice was driven underground. Stalin's rule was totalitarian, and no part of the Soviet Union — including his native Georgia — was spared its horrors.

### Noe Jordania (in exile) and the National Resistance

While the official history of Soviet Georgia focuses on the Communist Party, it is important to remember that significant resistance to Soviet rule existed throughout the period. The 1924 August Uprising, led by the Social Democratic opposition, was the largest armed revolt against Soviet rule in the Transcaucasus. The uprising was brutally suppressed — estimates of the number killed range from 3,000 to over 10,000 — and was followed by mass arrests, deportations, and executions that effectively destroyed the organized political opposition in Georgia for a generation. The government-in-exile, led by Noe Jordania in Paris, continued to advocate for Georgian independence until the 1950s.

### Zviad Gamsakhurdia (1939-1993)

Zviad Gamsakhurdia was a Georgian writer, scholar, and dissident who became the leading figure of the Georgian national movement in the late Soviet period and the first president of independent Georgia. The son of the famous Georgian writer Konstantine Gamsakhurdia, Zviad was a philologist and literary scholar who became involved in the human rights movement in the 1970s. He was a founding member of the Georgian Helsinki Group, which monitored Soviet compliance with human rights agreements, and was repeatedly arrested, imprisoned, and harassed by the KGB for his dissident activities.

Gamsakhurdia's significance lies in his role as the person who translated Georgian national sentiment into organized political action. His advocacy for Georgian independence, the Georgian language, and the Georgian Orthodox Church resonated deeply with a population increasingly disillusioned with Soviet rule. However, his uncompromising nationalism and authoritarian tendencies also contributed to the political instability that plagued Georgia after independence. His complex legacy — both as the father of Georgian independence and as a deeply flawed political leader — reflects the contradictions of the late Soviet and early post-Soviet periods.

### Merab Kostava (1946-1989)

Merab Kostava was a Georgian dissident, musicologist, and national movement leader who worked closely with Zviad Gamsakhurdia in the 1970s and 1980s. A classically trained musician, Kostava was imprisoned multiple times by the Soviet authorities for his human rights and national rights activism. He was one of the founding members of the Georgian Helsinki Group and a key organizer of the pro-independence movement in the late 1980s.

Kostava was killed in a car accident on October 13, 1989, under circumstances that many Georgians believe were suspicious. His death was a devastating blow to the national movement, as he was widely regarded as a more moderate and pragmatic leader than Gamsakhurdia. Kostava's commitment to nonviolent resistance and his ability to build coalitions across different segments of Georgian society made him a uniquely effective leader. His memory is honored in Georgia as a martyr to the cause of independence.

### Eduard Shevardnadze (1928-2014)

Eduard Shevardnadze was a Georgian Communist Party official who rose to become the Soviet Union's Minister of Foreign Affairs under Mikhail Gorbachev (1985-1990) and later the second president of Georgia (1995-2003). Shevardnadze's career spanned the entire Soviet period and the transition to independence. As Soviet foreign minister, he played a key role in the end of the Cold War, helping negotiate the reunification of Germany, the withdrawal of Soviet troops from Eastern Europe, and arms reduction agreements with the United States.

As leader of Georgia in the 1990s and early 2000s, Shevardnadze's legacy was more mixed. He was credited with ending the civil wars of the early 1990s and bringing a measure of stability to Georgia, but his government was widely perceived as corrupt and ineffective. The Rose Revolution of 2003, which toppled Shevardnadze's government, reflected public frustration with his rule. Shevardnadze's career illustrates the complex relationship between Georgia and the Soviet/Russian system — he was both a product of that system and a leader who helped dismantle it.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Red Army Invasion and Establishment of Soviet Power (February-March 1921)

The Sovietization of Georgia began with the Red Army invasion of February 1921, which overthrew the democratic government and established a Bolshevik regime. The Georgian Communist Party, which had only a small base of popular support, governed through the power of the Soviet military and security apparatus. The new regime faced immediate resistance from the Georgian population, leading to the 1924 August Uprising — a large-scale armed revolt that was brutally suppressed. The establishment of Soviet power in Georgia was accomplished through military force rather than popular revolution, and the regime's legitimacy was always contested by significant segments of the Georgian population.

### The Great Purge in Georgia (1937-1938)

The Great Purge — Stalin's campaign of political terror against real and imagined opponents — hit Georgia with particular severity. Lavrenti Beria, the head of the Georgian NKVD (secret police), oversaw a purge that virtually eliminated the entire leadership of the Georgian Communist Party that had been in power since 1921. Thousands of party members, government officials, intellectuals, clergy, and ordinary citizens were arrested, tortured, and executed. The purge also targeted Georgian cultural institutions — theaters, universities, newspapers — and many of Georgia's finest writers, artists, and scholars were killed or imprisoned.

The impact of the purge on Georgian society was profound and lasting. It destroyed a generation of Georgian political and intellectual leaders, created a culture of fear and self-censorship that persisted for decades, and deepened the alienation between the Georgian population and the Soviet regime. Even after Stalin's death and Khrushchev's "de-Stalinization" campaign, the trauma of the purges remained a powerful undercurrent in Georgian society.

### World War II and the Georgian War Effort (1941-1945)

World War II — known in the Soviet Union as the Great Patriotic War — was a defining experience for Soviet Georgia. Approximately 700,000 Georgians served in the Red Army during the war, and an estimated 300,000 of them were killed. Georgian soldiers fought in every major campaign of the war, from the defense of Moscow to the Battle of Stalingrad to the capture of Berlin. Georgia's economy was mobilized for the war effort, producing weapons, ammunition, food, and raw materials. Georgian factories manufactured aircraft parts, and Georgian agriculture fed the army.

The war also had darker dimensions for Georgia. In 1944, Stalin ordered the deportation of several ethnic groups from their homelands in the North Caucasus to Central Asia, including the Meskhetian Turks (a Muslim population from southern Georgia). This forced deportation, carried out with great brutality, was one of the war's most terrible legacies for Georgia. The Meskhetian Turks were not allowed to return to Georgia for decades, and the issue of their repatriation remains politically sensitive.

### The April 9 Tragedy (1989)

On April 9, 1989, Soviet troops violently dispersed a peaceful pro-independence demonstration in front of the Government House in Tbilisi. The demonstrators, including women, elderly people, and teenagers, were attacked with spades, clubs, and (according to some accounts) toxic gas. Twenty-one people were killed, mostly young women, and hundreds were injured. The brutality of the attack shocked the Soviet Union and the world, and it became a turning point in Georgian history.

The April 9 tragedy transformed the Georgian independence movement from a relatively small dissident activity into a mass popular movement. The victims of April 9 became national martyrs, and the event is commemorated annually in Georgia as a day of national unity and remembrance. The incident also severely discredited the Soviet leadership in Georgia and accelerated the process that led to the declaration of independence in April 1991.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### Soviet Georgian Cinema

The Soviet period produced one of Georgia's most significant cultural achievements: its cinema. Georgian filmmakers — including Otar Ioseliani, Tengiz Abuladze, Eldar and Giorgi Shengelaia, and Georgi Danelia — created films that gained international acclaim for their artistic quality and their distinctive blend of poetic realism, humor, and social commentary. Abuladze's "Repentance" (1984), a surrealist allegory of Stalinist terror, was a landmark film that contributed to the glasnost era's reckoning with the Soviet past. Georgian cinema of this period is widely regarded as one of the finest national cinemas in the world.

### Soviet Georgian Literature

Georgian literature flourished during the Soviet period, despite the constraints of censorship. Writers like Konstantine Gamsakhurdia (author of "The Hand of the Great Master," a historical novel about the 12th-century Georgian architect), Nodar Dumbadze, Chabua Amirejibi, and Otar Chiladze produced works of lasting literary value. Some writers, like the dissident poet Titsian Tabidze (who was executed in the purges), used their work to express subtle resistance to Soviet ideology. The "thaw" period of the 1960s saw a particular flowering of literary creativity, as censorship was temporarily relaxed and writers explored themes of individual freedom, national identity, and moral responsibility.

### Georgian Polyphonic Music

Georgian polyphonic singing — a unique musical tradition in which songs are performed in three-part harmony without instrumental accompaniment — was preserved, studied, and promoted during the Soviet period. The Rustavi Choir, founded in 1968, became internationally famous for its performances of traditional Georgian polyphony and played a major role in introducing this remarkable musical tradition to audiences around the world. UNESCO recognized Georgian polyphonic singing as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity in 2001, but the preservation and development of the tradition during the Soviet period laid the groundwork for this recognition.

### Architecture and Urban Planning

The Soviet period brought significant changes to Georgia's built environment. New cities and industrial centers were built, including Rustavi (a major steel-producing city near Tbilisi), and existing cities were expanded and modernized. Soviet architecture in Georgia ranged from the monumental neoclassicism of the Stalin era (exemplified by the massive government buildings in Tbilisi's Republic Square) to the functionalist concrete-block housing estates of the 1960s-1980s. While many Soviet-era buildings are aesthetically undistinguished, some — like the Ministry of Highways building in Tbilisi, a striking example of Soviet brutalist architecture — have gained recognition as significant works of 20th-century architecture.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### Sameba Cathedral (Holy Trinity Cathedral), Tbilisi

The construction of the Sameba (Holy Trinity) Cathedral, begun in 1995 and completed in 2004, was one of the most significant architectural projects of the post-Soviet period and a powerful symbol of the revival of Georgian Orthodox Christianity after decades of Soviet suppression. While technically built after the Soviet period, Sameba represents the cultural revival that the Soviet era's religious persecution ultimately failed to prevent. The cathedral, one of the largest Orthodox churches in the world, dominates the Tbilisi skyline and serves as the seat of the Catholicos-Patriarch of All Georgia. Its construction on the site of a former Soviet-era cemetery and its massive scale reflect the Georgian Church's determination to reassert its central place in national life after seven decades of Soviet hostility.

### The Old Tbilisi Historic District

The historic district of Old Tbilisi, with its narrow winding streets, carved wooden balconies, sulfur baths, and eclectic mix of architectural styles — Georgian, Persian, Russian, and European — survived the Soviet period (though many of its historic buildings were destroyed to make way for Soviet-style construction). The district represents the living architectural heritage of centuries of Georgian history, including the Persian, Ottoman, and Russian periods. The Soviet-era destruction of parts of Old Tbilisi and the post-Soviet restoration efforts are themselves part of the story of Georgia's evolving relationship with its past.

### The Stalin Museum, Gori

The Stalin Museum in Gori, Stalin's birthplace, is one of the most unusual museums in the world. Founded in 1957 (four years after Stalin's death), the museum presents a largely uncritical celebration of Stalin's life and career, as it was originally conceived during the period when Stalin's legacy was still officially revered in the Soviet Union. The museum contains personal belongings, photographs, gifts from foreign leaders, and a death mask of Stalin, all displayed in an elaborate Stalinist-era building. The museum's existence and its unreconstructed presentation of Stalin's legacy reflect the complex and often contradictory Georgian relationship with the Soviet past. The museum remains controversial, with some calling for its closure and others arguing that it should be preserved as a historical document.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### Georgian Troops in the Red Army

During the Soviet period, Georgians served in the Soviet armed forces as part of the universal conscription system. Georgian soldiers fought in all the major conflicts of the Soviet era, including World War II, the Soviet-Afghan War (1979-1989), and various internal security operations. The Transcaucasian Military District, headquartered in Tbilisi, was one of the major military commands of the Soviet Union, and Georgia hosted significant military installations including air bases, training grounds, and weapons storage facilities. Georgian military officers served throughout the Soviet military structure, and some reached senior ranks.

### Industrial Military Production

Georgia contributed to Soviet military-industrial production, particularly in the areas of aviation components, electronics, and chemicals. The Tbilisi Aircraft Manufacturing Plant produced components for military aircraft, and Georgian chemical plants manufactured materials used in weapons production. However, Georgia was not a major center of Soviet military industry, and the republic's industrial base was relatively small compared to the major industrial centers of Russia and Ukraine. This meant that when Georgia gained independence, it inherited relatively limited military-industrial capabilities.

### The Soviet Border with Turkey and Iran

Georgia's southern border with Turkey and Iran was a heavily fortified segment of the Soviet Union's external frontier. The border was guarded by Soviet Border Troops (a separate service branch of the Soviet military) and was equipped with watchtowers, electronic surveillance systems, minefields, and barbed-wire barriers. The fortified border cut off Georgia's historic connections with its southern neighbors and restricted trade and movement. The opening of this border after Georgian independence was a significant change in the region's geopolitics.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

Soviet Georgian dress reflected both the standardizing tendencies of Soviet culture and the persistence of Georgian distinctive traditions. In the cities, Soviet-style clothing — standardized, functional, and often drab — was the norm. However, Georgians maintained their distinctive traditions of dress for festive occasions, and the chokha continued to be worn at weddings, national celebrations, and cultural events. Georgian women maintained a reputation for elegance and style, often adapting Soviet fashions with Georgian flair. The contrast between the uniformity of Soviet public dress and the vibrancy of Georgian traditional and festive clothing was a visible expression of the tension between Soviet identity and Georgian identity.

### Food and the Soviet Experience

The Soviet period brought both positive and negative changes to Georgian food culture. On the positive side, Soviet urbanization created a restaurant culture in Tbilisi and other cities that made Georgian cuisine widely known throughout the Soviet Union. Georgian restaurants — serving dishes like khachapuri, khinkali, badrijani, and pkhali — were among the most popular and prestigious in the Soviet Union, and Georgian wine was celebrated throughout the country. Georgian food became a symbol of hospitality and cultural sophistication within the Soviet system.

On the negative side, the collectivization of agriculture disrupted traditional farming patterns and food production practices. Chronic shortages of basic goods were a feature of Soviet life in Georgia, as in other Soviet republics, and the informal economy of barter, connections, and black-market transactions that developed to cope with these shortages was a defining feature of daily life. The Soviet-era phenomenon of "deficit" — chronic shortages of quality food and consumer goods — shaped the daily experience of generations of Georgians.

### Education and Soviet Culture

The Soviet period brought mass education to Georgia, dramatically expanding literacy and educational attainment. Soviet schools taught in Georgian (at least at the primary level), and Georgia developed a substantial higher education system. However, Soviet education was also an instrument of ideological indoctrination, and the curriculum emphasized Marxist-Leninist ideology and Russian language and culture. Georgian history was taught through a Soviet lens that downplayed periods of independent statehood and emphasized Georgia's "voluntary union" with Russia. The tension between genuine educational achievement and ideological manipulation was a defining feature of Soviet Georgian intellectual life.

### Religion Under Soviet Rule

The Georgian Orthodox Church survived the Soviet period but under severe pressure. Churches were closed, destroyed, or converted to secular uses. Clergy were arrested, imprisoned, and executed — particularly during the worst periods of repression in the 1920s-1930s. Religious education was banned, and open religious practice was discouraged and sometimes punished. Despite this persecution, the Church persisted as an underground institution, maintaining a network of priests and believers who kept the faith alive. The resilience of the Georgian Church under Soviet persecution became a powerful symbol of Georgian cultural endurance, and the Church's revival after the fall of the Soviet Union was one of the most dramatic religious revivals in post-Communist Europe.

## GLOSSARY

**Sakartvelos SSR** — The Georgian Soviet Socialist Republic, Georgia's official designation within the Soviet Union (1921-1991).

**Kavkasiis Msignobareba** — "Caucasian Autonomy." The Soviet policy of granting formal autonomy to minority regions within the Georgian republic, which created the autonomous republics of Abkhazia and Adjara and the autonomous oblast of South Ossetia.

**Gulagi** — The Soviet forced labor camp system. Many Georgians were imprisoned in the Gulag during the purges and other periods of repression.

**Perestroika** — "Restructuring." Mikhail Gorbachev's program of political and economic reforms (1985-1991) that ultimately led to the collapse of the Soviet Union.

**Glasnost** — "Openness." Gorbachev's policy of allowing greater freedom of expression and discussion of previously taboo topics, including Stalin's crimes.

**Tbilisis Universiteti** — Tbilisi State University, which remained the premier educational institution in Georgia throughout the Soviet period.

**Sakartvelos Sapatriarko** — The Georgian Patriarchate. The Georgian Orthodox Church, which maintained its institutional existence (though under severe pressure) throughout the Soviet period.

**Shesvlis Ts'reba** — "Night of the murder." A Georgian term referring to the April 9, 1989 tragedy.

**Sakartvelos Demokratiuli Aliance** — Various democratic alliances and movements that emerged during the late Soviet period to advocate for Georgian independence.

**Didi Mghvimeoba** — "The Great Terror." The Stalinist purges of 1937-1938, which devastated Georgia's political and intellectual elite.
