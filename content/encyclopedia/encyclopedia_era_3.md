# Era 3: Georgian Golden Age (~1089 - ~1225 AD)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The Georgian Golden Age is the period when Georgia, against all odds, became one of the most powerful and culturally brilliant states in the medieval world. Beginning with the reign of King David IV "the Builder" in 1089 and reaching its zenith under Queen Tamar in the late 12th century, this era saw Georgia transformed from a battered, Seljuk-besieged kingdom on the verge of extinction into the dominant power of the South Caucasus and eastern Anatolia. It is the period that Georgian historians and patriots look back to with the greatest pride, and for good reason — the achievements of this era in military conquest, political organization, literature, architecture, and art are genuinely extraordinary.

The Golden Age was built on the reforms of David the Builder, who inherited a kingdom reduced to a handful of mountain fortresses and transformed it into a military powerhouse. David reorganized the army, replacing the unreliable feudal levy with a professional standing force (the "monaspa"), reformed the tax system, reclaimed fertile lowland territories from the Seljuk Turks, and — most dramatically — captured Tbilisi in 1122, ending nearly four centuries of Arab rule. His successors, particularly Demetre I, Giorgi III, and above all Queen Tamar, continued and expanded his conquests, pushing Georgian borders to their greatest historical extent and establishing a trans-Caucasian empire that stretched from the Black Sea to the Caspian and from the Caucasus Mountains deep into northern Iran and eastern Anatolia.

Culturally, the Golden Age was equally spectacular. This was the age of Shota Rustaveli, author of "The Knight in the Panther's Skin" — Georgia's national epic and one of the masterpieces of world literature. It was the age of the great cathedral builders, who created monuments like the monasteries of Gelati, Vardzia, and David Gareja that still inspire awe today. Georgian art, particularly manuscript illumination, cloisonné enamelwork, and ecclesiastical metalwork, reached levels of sophistication comparable to the finest Byzantine and Islamic work of the period. The Georgian Church developed a rich theological and philosophical tradition, and the Gelati Academy became a renowned center of learning.

The Golden Age also had its complexities and contradictions. Georgia's empire was built on the subjugation of neighboring peoples — Armenians, Azerbaijanis, Circassians, and others — and the Georgian aristocracy's treatment of conquered populations could be harsh. The court politics of the era were frequently vicious, with rival noble factions scheming and murdering their way to power. And the very military successes that made Georgia powerful also sowed the seeds of future disaster, as the expansion of Georgian territory created overextended borders that would prove impossible to defend against the Mongol storm that arrived in the 1220s.

Nevertheless, the Golden Age represents the high-water mark of Georgian civilization and the period that most clearly demonstrated what Georgians could achieve when united under strong leadership. Its legacy — in art, literature, architecture, and national consciousness — has shaped Georgian identity down to the present day.

## KEY FIGURES

### King David IV "the Builder" (1089-1125)

David IV is arguably the greatest king in Georgian history. He inherited the throne at the age of sixteen, when Georgia was at its lowest ebb — Seljuk Turks controlled the fertile lowlands, the royal treasury was empty, and the great nobles were more powerful than the crown. Over a reign of thirty-six years, David rebuilt the kingdom from the ground up. His military reforms created the monaspa, a professional standing army loyal directly to the king, supplemented by 40,000 Kipchak Turk mercenaries settled in Georgia. He reformed the administration, weakened the power of the great nobles, reformed the Church (replacing the Georgian Church's Byzantine-aligned hierarchy with loyal Georgian prelates), and rebuilt the economy.

David's military campaigns were spectacularly successful. In a series of brilliant campaigns between 1099 and 1121, he drove the Seljuk Turks out of the Georgian heartland, reclaiming the cities and fertile agricultural lands that had been lost for decades. His crowning achievement was the Battle of Didgori in 1121, where his heavily outnumbered army routed a massive Seljuk coalition, and the subsequent capture of Tbilisi in 1122, ending the Arab Emirate that had ruled the city for nearly four centuries. David moved his capital to Tbilisi and embarked on an ambitious building program. He was canonized as a saint by the Georgian Orthodox Church, and his feast day is still celebrated as one of Georgia's most important national holidays.

### King Demetre I (1125-1156)

Demetre I, son of David the Builder, inherited a powerful kingdom and — with mixed success — worked to maintain and consolidate his father's conquests. A complex and sometimes controversial figure, Demetre was both a skilled poet (several of his religious hymns are still sung in Georgian churches today) and a ruthless politician. He faced rebellions from powerful nobles who resented the centralized authority his father had established, and he dealt with them with a combination of military force and political cunning.

Demetre's most famous literary work is the hymn "Shen Khar Venakhi" ("Thou Art a Vineyard"), a beautiful religious poem addressed to the Virgin Mary that is considered one of the finest examples of medieval Georgian religious poetry. The hymn is still sung in Georgian churches and has been set to music by numerous Georgian composers. Demetre's reign, while less dramatically successful than his father's, was important for consolidating the gains of the previous generation and for developing the cultural and literary traditions that would flourish under Queen Tamar.

### Queen Tamar (1184-1213)

Queen Tamar is the most celebrated ruler in Georgian history and one of the most remarkable female monarchs of the medieval world. She inherited the throne from her father, Giorgi III, in 1184, and ruled for twenty-nine years during which Georgia reached the absolute peak of its power and cultural achievement. Tamar's reign is often described as the apex of the Georgian Golden Age, and with good reason.

Tamar's achievements were extraordinary across every domain. Militarily, her armies — commanded by brilliant generals like Zakaria and Ivane Mkhargrdzeli — conquered vast territories, pushing Georgian borders to their greatest historical extent. The Georgian empire under Tamar stretched from the Black Sea to the Caspian, from the Caucasus Mountains to the Armenian highlands and northern Iran. The Georgian victory at the Battle of Shamkor in 1195 and the campaign against the Seljuks at Basian in 1203 were among the greatest military achievements of the era. Culturally, Tamar's court patronized the greatest writers and artists of the age, most famously Shota Rustaveli, who dedicated his epic poem "The Knight in the Panther's Skin" to the queen. Tamar's reign saw a remarkable flowering of architecture, with the construction or renovation of churches and monasteries throughout the kingdom.

Tamar was canonized as a saint by the Georgian Orthodox Church, and her memory has been venerated throughout Georgian history. She is a central figure in Georgian national identity — a symbol of strength, wisdom, and the possibilities of Georgian greatness. For players of Sakartvelo Defenders, Queen Tamar represents the ultimate expression of Georgian royal power and cultural achievement.

### Shota Rustaveli (c. 1172-c. 1216)

Shota Rustaveli is Georgia's national poet and the author of "Vepkhistqaosani" ("The Knight in the Panther's Skin"), one of the great epic poems of world literature. Little is known with certainty about his life — even his dates are approximate — but tradition holds that he was a nobleman from Rustavi (or possibly Meskheti) who served as treasurer at Queen Tamar's court. His great poem, written in the 1190s and dedicated to Queen Tamar, is a chivalric romance that draws on Persian literary traditions while being thoroughly Georgian in spirit.

"The Knight in the Panther's Skin" tells the story of the adventures of the knight Tariel, his faithful companion Avtandil, and their beloved Nestan-Darejan, in a world of courtly love, heroic combat, and philosophical reflection. The poem is written in a distinctive 16-syllable verse form called the shairi, and its language is one of the peaks of Georgian literary achievement. Beyond its literary merits, the poem expresses a philosophy of chivalric friendship, romantic love, and heroic striving that has profoundly influenced Georgian culture. Its famous opening lines — "I sing of the lion-dressed man, who seized the panther's skin" — are known by heart by virtually every educated Georgian.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Battle of Didgori (1121)

The Battle of Didgori, fought on August 12, 1121, is the most celebrated military victory in Georgian history. King David the Builder, leading an army of approximately 56,000 men (including Georgian troops, Kipchak mercenaries, and Western European crusaders), confronted a massive Seljuk Turkish coalition estimated at 200,000-300,000 fighters near the mountain of Didgori, about 40 km west of Tbilisi. Despite being heavily outnumbered, David's well-disciplined professional army achieved a crushing victory, routing the Seljuk forces and opening the road to Tbilisi.

The battle's outcome was shaped by David's tactical skill: he used a feigned retreat to draw the Seljuk vanguard into a trap, then unleashed his heavy cavalry in a devastating charge. The Seljuk army collapsed into a rout, with enormous casualties. Georgian tradition calls it the "miraculous victory" (dzlevay sakvirveli), and August 12 is still celebrated as a national holiday in Georgia. The victory at Didgori broke Seljuk power in the Caucasus and made possible the reconquest of Tbilisi the following year.

### The Capture of Tbilisi (1122)

Following his victory at Didgori, King David the Builder marched on Tbilisi, which had been under Arab rule as the capital of the Emirate of Tbilisi since 736. The city fell in February 1122 after a siege, ending nearly four centuries of Muslim rule. David entered the city in triumph and established it as the capital of the Georgian kingdom, a position it has held ever since.

The capture of Tbilisi was both a military triumph and a symbolic watershed. It restored to Georgian control the most important city in the Caucasus, a major trading center at the crossroads of Europe and Asia. David embarked on an ambitious program of rebuilding and repopulating the city, inviting Armenian, Jewish, and Muslim merchants to return and reestablish trade. The liberation of Tbilisi completed the work of national unification and reconquest that David had begun, and it set the stage for the further expansion of Georgian power under his successors.

### The Battle of Shamkor (1195)

The Battle of Shamkor, fought in 1195 during the reign of Queen Tamar, was another decisive Georgian victory over the Seljuk Turks. The Georgian army, commanded by the Mkhargrdzeli brothers Zakaria and Ivane, routed the forces of Abu Bakr, the Seljuk atabeg of Azerbaijan, near the city of Shamkor in northwestern Iran. The battle was triggered by the Seljuk invasion of the Georgian client state of Shirvan, and its outcome established Georgian military dominance over much of the South Caucasus and northwestern Iran.

The victory at Shamkor was celebrated with elaborate festivities at Queen Tamar's court and was commemorated in Georgian chronicles and literature. It opened the way for Georgian expansion into the Armenian highlands and northern Iran, and it demonstrated the effectiveness of the Georgian military machine that Tamar and her generals had built. The battle cemented Georgia's reputation as the most powerful Christian state in the Near East.

### The Battle of Basian (1203)

The Battle of Basian, fought in 1203, was the last and perhaps the greatest of Queen Tamar's military victories. A massive Seljuk army, led by the sultan of Rum, Rukn al-Din, invaded Georgian territory, and the Georgian army under Zakaria Mkhargrdzeli marched to meet them near the fortress of Basian in northeastern Anatolia. The battle was hard-fought and nearly lost — the Georgian right wing was driven back — but a counterattack by the reserve forces turned the tide, and the Seljuks were decisively defeated.

The Battle of Basian was significant because it confirmed Georgian control over the important border regions between the Caucasus and Anatolia and because it demonstrated the continued military superiority of Georgia over its Seljuk rivals even at a great distance from the Georgian heartland. The victory was followed by further Georgian expansion into Anatolia, and it established a period of relative peace and prosperity that allowed the cultural achievements of Tamar's reign to reach their fullest flowering.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### "The Knight in the Panther's Skin"

Shota Rustaveli's epic poem, written in the 1190s, is the supreme achievement of Georgian literature and one of the great works of world literature. The poem, consisting of approximately 1,600 sixteen-syllable quatrains, tells a complex story of chivalric adventure, courtly love, and philosophical reflection. Its themes — the primacy of friendship, the power of love, the nobility of self-sacrifice, the quest for justice — have resonated with Georgian readers for eight centuries. The poem's language is rich and inventive, and its philosophical passages reflect deep engagement with both Georgian Christian tradition and the broader intellectual currents of the medieval world. It has been translated into dozens of languages and remains the defining work of Georgian literature.

### Gelati Academy and the Golden Age of Learning

The Gelati Academy, founded by David the Builder in 1106 as part of the Gelati Monastery complex near Kutaisi, was the premier center of learning in medieval Georgia. The academy attracted scholars from throughout the Georgian world and beyond, and its curriculum encompassed theology, philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, music, and rhetoric. Scholars at Gelati produced translations of Greek philosophical and scientific works into Georgian, as well as original compositions in theology, law, and history. The academy's library was one of the largest in the Caucasus. The tradition of scholarship at Gelati helped make the Georgian Golden Age truly golden in intellectual as well as military and artistic terms.

### Cloisonné Enamelwork

Georgian cloisonné enamelwork reached extraordinary heights during the Golden Age. This technique, which involves creating small compartments (cloisons) with thin metal strips and filling them with colored enamel paste before firing, was used to produce stunning religious icons, book covers, and decorative objects. The finest examples — such as the Khakhuli Triptych (a massive altarpiece now in the Georgian National Museum in Tbilisi) and the icons from the Gelati and Vardzia monasteries — display remarkable technical mastery and artistic beauty, combining brilliant colors with intricate geometric and figural designs. Georgian enamelwork was widely admired throughout the medieval world and influenced the decorative arts of Byzantium, Armenia, and the Slavic countries.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### Gelati Monastery Complex

Founded by David the Builder in 1106, the Gelati Monastery complex near Kutaisi was the religious and intellectual heart of the Georgian Golden Age. The complex includes the Cathedral of the Virgin (Natlismtsemeli), famous for its remarkable mosaic of the Virgin and Child — one of the finest medieval mosaics in the Caucasus — as well as the Church of Saint George and the Church of Saint Nicholas. The monastery's walls are adorned with exquisite stone carvings, and the complex includes the Gelati Academy building. David the Builder chose Gelati as his burial place, and his tomb in the cathedral's gateway has been a place of veneration for centuries. The entire complex is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

### Vardzia Cave City

Vardzia is one of the most extraordinary architectural monuments in the Caucasus — a vast cave city carved into the cliffs of the Erusheti Mountain on the left bank of the Mtkvari River in southern Georgia. Originally begun as a small cave church in the 12th century, Vardzia was expanded by Queen Tamar into a massive fortified complex with hundreds of rooms, including churches, chapels, living quarters, refectories, libraries, bakeries, and wine cellars, all carved into the volcanic rock cliff face. At its peak, Vardzia could house up to 50,000 people and served as both a monastery and a military fortress guarding Georgia's southern border. The complex extends for about 500 meters along the cliff face and rises to thirteen levels. Although partially destroyed by an earthquake in 1283, Vardzia remains one of the most impressive and atmospheric monuments of medieval Georgia.

### David Gareja Monastery Complex

The David Gareja Monastery Complex is a vast network of cave monasteries carved into the semi-desert hills of southeastern Georgia, along the border with Azerbaijan. Founded in the 6th century by Saint David Garejeli (one of the Thirteen Syrian Fathers who brought monasticism to Georgia), the complex was greatly expanded during the Golden Age, particularly under Queen Tamar. The complex includes dozens of monasteries, churches, and hermitages spread across a remote and strikingly beautiful landscape of barren hills and rocky gorges. The churches contain important medieval frescoes, some of which are among the finest examples of Georgian mural painting. The Udabno Monastery, perched on a clifftop overlooking the dramatic landscape, is particularly noted for its well-preserved 10th-13th century frescoes.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### The Monaspa

The monaspa was the professional standing army created by David the Builder, and it was the key to Georgia's military success during the Golden Age. Unlike the traditional feudal levy — which was unreliable, poorly trained, and available only for limited campaigns — the monaspa was a full-time, salaried military force loyal directly to the king. The monaspa included heavy cavalry, light cavalry, infantry, and support troops, and it was organized into well-defined units with clear chains of command. The professionalization of the Georgian military gave it a qualitative edge over the feudal armies of neighboring states and allowed Georgia to sustain long campaigns far from its borders.

### Kipchak Mercenaries

One of David the Builder's most innovative military reforms was the settlement of 40,000 Kipchak Turk families in Georgia in the early 12th century. The Kipchaks (also known as Cumans or Polovtsians) were nomadic Turkic warriors from the Eurasian steppe who had been displaced from their homeland. David settled them in Georgian territory and incorporated them into the Georgian military system. Each Kipchak family provided one warrior for the Georgian army, creating a large reserve of experienced horse archers. The Kipchaks were gradually Christianized and integrated into Georgian society, and they played a crucial role in David's military campaigns against the Seljuks.

### Siege Warfare

The Georgian military of the Golden Age was proficient in siege warfare, a capability that was essential for the conquest and defense of the numerous fortified cities and castles throughout the region. Georgian siege engines included catapults (both traction and counterweight trebuchets), siege towers, battering rams, and scaling ladders. Mining and sapping were also used to undermine fortress walls. The Georgians also developed sophisticated fortification techniques for their own castles and cities, incorporating concentric walls, moats, and carefully designed gate complexes.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

During the Golden Age, Georgian court dress reached new heights of elegance and sophistication. Elite men wore richly embroidered caftans over silk or linen undergarments, wide trousers, and ornate boots. Cloaks and mantles, often lined with fur, were worn for formal occasions. The nobility wore elaborate headdresses, and belts with jeweled clasps signified rank. Women's dress featured long, flowing robes of silk, velvet, or brocade, with wide sleeves and elaborate embroidery. Precious jewelry — gold rings with gemstones, pearl earrings, jeweled necklaces, and ornate belt buckles — completed the ensemble. Court dress reflected influences from both Byzantine and Persian fashion but maintained distinctive Georgian elements.

### Food and Feasting

The Golden Age was an era of culinary sophistication and elaborate feasting traditions that would become central to Georgian culture. The Georgian supra (feast) tradition, with its rituals of toasting, its sequence of dishes, and its emphasis on hospitality and conviviality, was fully developed during this period. The cuisine featured a rich variety of dishes: khachapuri (cheese bread), khinkali (dumplings), badrijani (eggplant dishes), roasted meats, and an enormous variety of vegetable, grain, and dairy preparations. Wine was the essential accompaniment to every meal, and Georgia's distinctive varieties — grown in the country's many microclimates and fermented in qvevri — were celebrated. Spices from the East — pepper, cinnamon, saffron, coriander — added exotic flavors. The tamada (toastmaster) tradition, in which a designated master of ceremonies guides the sequence of toasts through a feast, was already well established.

### Social Structure

Georgian society during the Golden Age was hierarchical but relatively dynamic. The king (mep'e) stood at the apex, supported by a court of ministers, generals, and nobles. Below the royal family were the great nobles (dideli aznauri) and the lesser nobility (aznauri), who held land in exchange for military service. The Church was another powerful institution, with bishops, abbots, and monks wielding significant social and economic power. Free commoners (mokalake) formed the majority of the population, working as farmers, craftsmen, and merchants. At the bottom were dependent peasants and slaves. One distinctive feature of Georgian society in this period was the presence of the kholopogebi — royal servants and soldiers who were personally dependent on the king and formed an important counterweight to the power of the great nobles.

### Religion

The Georgian Orthodox Church was at the height of its power and prestige during the Golden Age. The Church was a major landowner, a center of learning, and a powerful political institution. Church architecture reached its peak, with the construction of magnificent cathedrals and monasteries throughout the kingdom. Religious art — icons, frescoes, enamelwork, and illuminated manuscripts — achieved extraordinary beauty and sophistication. The Church also played an important role in state ceremonies, royal coronations, and the legitimation of royal power. The relationship between the Church and the crown was close but not without tension, as kings periodically sought to assert control over Church appointments and property. The Church's promotion of a distinctly Georgian form of Christianity — with its own liturgy, calendar of saints, and artistic traditions — was a powerful force for national cohesion.

## GLOSSARY

**Monaspa** — The professional standing army created by David the Builder. From Georgian "mona" (one who is in a group) and "spa" (troop).

**Mep'e** — "King." The title of the Georgian sovereign during the Golden Age and throughout the medieval period.

**Didgori** — The site of the most famous victory in Georgian history (1121), where David the Builder defeated a massive Seljuk coalition.

**Vepkhistqaosani** — "The Knight in the Panther's Skin," the national epic of Georgia by Shota Rustaveli.

**Tamada** — The toastmaster at a Georgian feast (supra). The tamada guides the sequence of toasts and sets the tone for the gathering.

**Supra** — A traditional Georgian feast, characterized by an elaborate sequence of dishes and toasts governed by a tamada.

**Shairi** — The 16-syllable verse form used by Rustaveli in "The Knight in the Panther's Skin." A distinctive Georgian poetic meter.

**Aznauri** — The Georgian nobility. During the Golden Age, the aznauri served as the officer corps of the Georgian army and the administrative class of the kingdom.

**Kipchaki** — Turkic nomadic warriors settled in Georgia by David the Builder. Also known as Cumans or Polovtsians.

**Sakdrisi-Karighe** — A Georgian term referring to a soldier or warrior, particularly one who serves in the standing army.
