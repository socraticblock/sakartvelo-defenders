# Era 5: Between Empires (~1500 - ~1801 AD)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The period from approximately 1500 to 1801 is one of the most complex and challenging in Georgian history. Georgia existed as a set of small, competing kingdoms and principalities — primarily Kartli (eastern Georgia), Kakheti (easternmost Georgia), and Imereti (western Georgia), along with the semi-independent principalities of Samegrelo, Guria, Abkhazia, and Samtskhe — sandwiched between three great empires: the Ottoman Empire to the west, Safavid and later Afsharid and Qajar Iran to the east, and the Russian Empire emerging to the north. Survival in this deadly geopolitical environment required extraordinary diplomatic skill, constant military readiness, and a willingness to accept humiliating degrees of foreign interference in internal affairs.

This era saw Georgia transformed from a set of medieval kingdoms into a frontier zone between the Ottoman and Iranian empires. The Ottomans conquered western Georgia's Black Sea coastline and much of western Georgia's lowland territory in the 16th century, while the Safavid Persians repeatedly invaded eastern Georgia, sacked its cities, and deported large portions of its population. The Georgian kingdoms became pawns in the Ottoman-Iranian rivalry, forced to shift allegiance between the two empires as circumstances dictated. The principality of Samtskhe was conquered by the Ottomans in the 16th century and lost to Georgian control entirely.

Yet this era was also one of remarkable cultural creativity and political experimentation. Georgian kings like David X of Kartli, Levan of Kakheti, and especially the great King Erekle II (Heraclius II) demonstrated that Georgia could still punch above its weight through clever diplomacy and military reform. The 17th and 18th centuries saw the construction of magnificent new churches, the development of a rich tradition of Georgian historical chronicles, the creation of elaborate urban caravansarais and bathhouses in Tbilisi, and the introduction of new artistic influences from Iran and the Ottoman world. Georgian warriors served as elite troops in both the Persian and Ottoman armies, gaining military experience and reputations as fierce fighters.

The defining dilemma of this era was whether Georgia could survive as an independent entity between the empires or whether it must seek the protection of a great power — which inevitably meant sacrificing some degree of independence. This dilemma was resolved, for better or worse, by King Erekle II's decision to seek a protective alliance with the Russian Empire in the Treaty of Georgievsk (1783). When Russia failed to protect Georgia from the Persian invasion of 1795, and when the resulting devastation demonstrated that Georgia could not survive alone, the Georgian kingdoms were formally annexed by Russia in 1801, beginning a new chapter in Georgian history.

## KEY FIGURES

### King David X of Kartli (1505-1525)

David X was one of the most capable kings of Kartli in the 16th century, ruling during a period of intense pressure from both the Ottoman and Safavid Persian empires. David's reign was marked by constant warfare, as he fought to maintain Kartli's independence against the expansionist ambitions of both empires while also attempting to assert control over the neighboring kingdom of Kakheti and the rebellious Georgian nobility. David was known for his military skill and his personal courage — he led his armies in battle and was wounded multiple times.

David's most significant decision was to align Kartli with the Safavid Persians against the Ottomans, a choice that shaped Georgian politics for the next two centuries. This alignment brought some measure of security against Ottoman expansion but at the cost of periodic Persian interference in Kartli's internal affairs and the requirement to provide troops for Persian military campaigns. David's reign illustrates the impossible choices facing Georgian rulers in this era: there was no option that did not involve significant compromises of sovereignty.

### King Rostom (1633-1658)

King Rostom of Kartli was one of the most controversial figures in Georgian history — a Georgian king who converted to Islam, served the Persian Shah, and ruled his kingdom as a Persian vassal. Born a Georgian Christian noble, Rostom spent much of his early career in the Persian court and converted to Islam to gain the shah's favor. He was installed as king of Kartli by the Safavids in 1633 and ruled for twenty-five years.

Despite his conversion and Persian allegiance, Rostom was a capable administrator who brought stability and relative prosperity to Kartli after decades of chaos. He rebuilt cities, promoted trade, and maintained a degree of internal peace. He was also a noted patron of the arts — his court produced some of the finest Georgian miniature paintings of the 17th century. However, his Islamization policies, including the encouragement of Georgian nobles to convert, were deeply resented by many of his subjects and by the Georgian Church. Rostom's reign illustrates the complex compromises that Georgian rulers were forced to make in this era and the painful tensions between political pragmatism and religious/national identity.

### King Erekle II (Heraclius II) (1744-1798)

King Erekle II is one of the most important and most debated figures in Georgian history. Born in 1720 (or 1722), Heraclius (Erekle in Georgian) was the son of Teimuraz II of Kakheti and inherited the throne of Kakheti in 1744. In 1762, upon the death of his father, he united the kingdoms of Kartli and Kakheti, becoming the first ruler of eastern Georgia since the medieval period to govern a single Georgian state. He is often referred to as "the Last King of Georgia."

Erekle's reign was marked by ambitious reform and desperate diplomacy. He modernized the Georgian military along European lines, established schools and printing presses, reformed the legal system, and sought to build alliances with European powers against Georgia's Muslim neighbors. His greatest diplomatic achievement was the Treaty of Georgievsk (1783), in which the Russian Empire recognized the independence of the Kartli-Kakheti Kingdom and promised military protection in exchange for Georgian alignment with Russian foreign policy. The treaty was celebrated as a triumph, but when the Persians invaded in 1795 and the Russians failed to send the promised help, Erekle's hopes were crushed. The Persian army under Agha Mohammad Khan sacked Tbilisi, killing thousands and devastating the city. Erekle died in 1798, a broken man, and his kingdom was annexed by Russia three years later. Despite the tragic end of his reign, Erekle's efforts to modernize Georgia and maintain its independence make him one of the most sympathetic figures in Georgian history.

### Queen Ketevan (c. 1560-1624)

Saint Ketevan is one of the most revered martyrs of the Georgian Orthodox Church. A noblewoman from the House of Mukhrani, she married King David I of Kakheti and became queen of Kakheti. After her husband's death, she served as regent for her young son, Constantine I, navigating the treacherous politics of the period with considerable skill. In 1614, the Safavid Persian Shah Abbas I invaded Kakheti and took Ketevan as a hostage to his capital at Isfahan.

Shah Abbas demanded that Ketevan renounce Christianity and convert to Islam. When she refused, she was subjected to horrific tortures over a period of days before being executed on September 13, 1624. Her courage and faith made her a legendary figure, and she was canonized as a saint by the Georgian Orthodox Church. Her relics, long thought lost, were partially rediscovered in 2017 at the Sainte-Catherine monastery in Sinai, Egypt. Ketevan's story is one of the most powerful examples of Georgian resistance to forced conversion and cultural assimilation.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Ottoman Conquest of Western Georgia (16th century)

The Ottoman Empire's expansion into the Caucasus in the 16th century led to the conquest of western Georgia's Black Sea coastline and much of western Georgia's lowland territory. The Ottoman capture of the fortress of Gonio (Apsaros) and the port of Batumi gave the empire control of the important eastern Black Sea coast. The Principality of Samtskhe was conquered in 1578 and incorporated into the Ottoman administrative system as the Eyalet of Childir (Çıldır). The Ottoman conquest cut western Georgia off from direct access to the Mediterranean trade network and brought large parts of the Georgian population under Muslim rule.

### The Treaty of Georgievsk (1783)

The Treaty of Georgievsk, signed on July 24, 1783, was a landmark agreement between the Kingdom of Kartli-Kakheti (eastern Georgia) and the Russian Empire. Under the treaty, King Erekle II accepted Russian protectorate status in exchange for Russian guarantees of Georgia's territorial integrity and military protection against Persian and Ottoman threats. The treaty also guaranteed the Georgian royal dynasty's right to rule and preserved the Georgian Church's autonomy.

The treaty was initially hailed as a diplomatic triumph, but its terms were ambiguous and it proved to be more beneficial to Russia than to Georgia. The Russians interpreted the treaty as granting them broad authority over Georgian foreign policy and eventually over internal affairs as well. When the Persian army of Agha Mohammad Khan invaded Georgia in 1795, the Russians did not send the promised military assistance, leaving Tbilisi to be sacked. The failure of the Georgievsk alliance disillusioned many Georgians and paved the way for the Russian annexation of 1801.

### The Persian Sack of Tbilisi (1795)

On September 11-12, 1795, the Persian army of Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar entered Tbilisi after the Georgian army was defeated at the Battle of Krtsanisi. The Persians sacked the city with appalling ferocity, burning buildings, killing thousands of civilians, and enslaving a large portion of the surviving population. The destruction was systematic and devastating — the great churches of Tbilisi were looted and damaged, the royal palace was burned, and the city's economic infrastructure was destroyed.

The sack of Tbilisi was the single most traumatic event in 18th-century Georgian history and effectively ended any realistic hope of independent Georgian statehood. King Erekle II, who had fought bravely but been overwhelmed, was forced to watch helplessly as his capital was destroyed. The catastrophe demonstrated conclusively that Georgia could not survive between the empires without effective great-power protection — a lesson that led directly to the Russian annexation of 1801. The memory of 1795 remained a powerful force in Georgian national consciousness, a symbol of the consequences of geopolitical vulnerability.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### Persian-Influenced Art and Architecture

This era saw the development of a distinctive hybrid artistic style in Georgia that blended indigenous Georgian traditions with strong Persian influences. Tbilisi was rebuilt in the 17th and 18th centuries with a distinctive urban architecture featuring caravansarais, bathhouses (the famous Tbilisi sulphur baths), bazaars, and private houses with carved wooden balconies (the "Tbilisi style"). Persian decorative motifs — floral patterns, geometric designs, calligraphic inscriptions — were widely adopted in Georgian art, architecture, and textile design, creating a rich syncretic aesthetic unique to Georgia.

### Georgian Historical Chronicles

The 17th and 18th centuries were a remarkably productive period for Georgian historical writing. Chroniclers like Beri Egnatashvili, Prince Vakhushti (whose comprehensive "Description of the Kingdom of Georgia" is an invaluable source for Georgian history and geography), and Catholicos Anton I produced detailed histories of Georgia that preserved and interpreted the Georgian past for contemporary and future readers. These chronicles combined historical narrative with geographical description, genealogical information, and cultural commentary, and they played an important role in maintaining Georgian national consciousness during a period of political fragmentation and foreign domination.

### The First Georgian Printing Press

In 1709, King Vakhtang VI of Kartli established the first printing press in Georgia, located initially in Tbilisi and later transferred to Moscow. The press produced religious texts, dictionaries, and educational works, and it represented a major step in the development of Georgian literacy and intellectual life. Vakhtang himself was a noted scholar and patron of learning who produced important translations of Persian literature into Georgian and compiled a comprehensive Georgian legal code. The establishment of a printing press, at a time when Georgia was under severe pressure from its Muslim neighbors, was an act of cultural defiance and a significant contribution to the preservation of Georgian literary traditions.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### Metekhi Church and Cliff

The Metekhi Church, perched on a dramatic cliff overlooking the Mtkvari River in the heart of Tbilisi, is one of the city's most iconic landmarks. The current church dates to the 13th century (with later modifications), but the site has been fortified since antiquity. According to tradition, King Vakhtang Gorgasali built a church and palace on this site in the 5th century. Metekhi served as a royal residence, a fortress, and a prison at various points in its history — the most famous prisoner was the great Georgian saint Shushanik in the 5th century, and in the 20th century, the Soviet secret police used the site as a prison and execution ground. The dramatic cliff-top setting, with the church silhouetted against the sky above the river, has made Metekhi one of the most photographed sites in Georgia.

### Gremi Fortress Complex

The Gremi Fortress Complex, located in the Kakheti region of eastern Georgia, was the capital of the Kingdom of Kakheti from the 15th to the 17th century. The complex includes a massive fortified citadel with high stone walls and towers, the Church of the Archangels (built in 1565 and decorated with fine 16th-century frescoes), a royal palace, and a trading quarter with caravansarais and baths. Gremi was an important center of trade connecting the Caucasus with the Persian world, and its architecture reflects both Georgian and Persian influences. The city was destroyed by the Persian Shah Abbas I in 1616, but the citadel and church survive as impressive ruins and are a UNESCO World Heritage Site candidate.

### Rabati Castle, Akhaltsikhe

Rabati Castle is a massive fortified complex in the city of Akhaltsikhe in southern Georgia. The site has been fortified since at least the 9th century, but the current structures primarily date to the 17th-18th centuries, when Akhaltsikhe was the capital of the Ottoman Eyalet of Childir (Çıldır). The castle reflects the complex cultural history of the region, with Georgian, Ottoman Turkish, and later Russian architectural elements layered on top of each other. The complex includes mosques, churches, a madrasa (Islamic school), a caravansarai, and extensive fortification walls with towers. Restored in the 2010s, Rabati Castle is a striking visual reminder of the multicultural world of the Ottoman-Iranian-Georgian frontier.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### Firearms and Gunpowder

The introduction of firearms transformed Georgian warfare during this period. Matchlock muskets, pistols, and cannon were gradually adopted by Georgian armies, though the adoption was slower in Georgia than in Europe due to economic constraints and limited access to manufacturing. Georgian warriors in Persian and Ottoman service gained experience with firearms, and returning soldiers brought this knowledge back to Georgia. King Erekle II's military reforms in the late 18th century included the establishment of regular infantry units equipped with muskets, modeled on European military organization.

### Cavalry Traditions

Georgian cavalry remained important throughout this period, combining indigenous horse-riding traditions with Persian and Ottoman influences. Georgian heavy cavalry, equipped with lances, swords, pistols, and increasingly with firearms, continued to be the elite striking force of Georgian armies. Georgian cavalrymen served in both Persian and Ottoman armies, where they were highly valued for their skill and bravery. The Georgian tradition of mounted warfare reached its final flowering in the 18th century, when King Erekle II's reformed cavalry was one of the most effective military forces in the Caucasus.

### Fortress Modernization

The demands of defending against Ottoman and Persian armies, both equipped with cannon, led to the modernization of Georgian fortifications. Walls were thickened and reinforced with earthworks to absorb cannon fire. Bastions and angle batteries were added to allow defensive cannon fire. The citadels of Gremi, Khertvisi, and other Georgian fortresses were rebuilt and strengthened during this period. However, Georgia's limited resources meant that its fortifications were often inferior to those of the Ottoman and Persian empires, and Georgian fortresses frequently fell to numerically superior besiegers with better artillery.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

Georgian dress in this period was strongly influenced by Persian fashion, particularly among the elite. Men wore the chokha (also called the cherkeska) — a long, fitted wool coat with decorative cartridge holders (gziani) across the chest — over a shirt and wide trousers. The chokha would become the quintessential Georgian national costume and is still worn on formal occasions today. Headgear included the papakha (a tall fur hat) and various styles of wrapped turbans for men. Women wore long dresses with fitted bodices and full skirts, often with richly embroidered vests and decorative belts. Jewelry continued to be important, with silver and gold rings, earrings, and necklaces displaying both Georgian and Persian design elements.

### Food and the Evolution of Georgian Cuisine

The interaction with Persian and Ottoman culinary traditions enriched Georgian cuisine during this period. New dishes and ingredients were introduced, including various rice pilafs, kebabs, and sweet pastries. The Georgian supra tradition continued to evolve, incorporating new dishes while maintaining its distinctive structure and rituals. The cuisine of eastern Georgia (Kartli and Kakheti) was more influenced by Persian traditions, while western Georgian cuisine (Imereti, Samegrelo, Guria) showed more Ottoman influence. Regional specialties emerged — the spicy walnut sauces of Samegrelo, the cheese breads of Imereti, the herb-filled dishes of Kakheti. Wine remained central to Georgian culture, with the distinctive qvevri winemaking tradition continuing in rural areas.

### Social Structure

Georgian society in this period was organized around the competing kingdoms and principalities, each with its own royal house and noble class. The king (mep'e) stood at the apex, supported by a court of ministers and a powerful aristocracy. Below the nobility were the free commoners — farmers, craftsmen, and merchants — and at the bottom were serfs and slaves. The Church remained a major landowner and social institution. One significant change in this period was the increasing importance of merchant communities — Armenian, Persian, and Indian merchants played major roles in Georgian trade, particularly in Tbilisi. The cities of Tbilisi, Gori, and Kutaisi were multiethnic, multireligious communities where Georgians, Armenians, Persians, Turks, Jews, and others lived side by side.

### Religion

Religious life in this period was shaped by the complex political and cultural interactions between Christianity and Islam. The Georgian Orthodox Church remained the dominant religious institution, but it faced enormous pressures. The Ottomans and Persians encouraged conversion to Islam, offering political and economic advantages to converts. Some Georgian nobles, like King Rostom, converted to Islam to advance their careers. The Church responded by strengthening its institutional structures, promoting the cult of martyrs (like Queen Ketevan), and maintaining its role as the guardian of Georgian cultural identity. At the same time, the Islamic communities of Georgia — both converts and immigrant Muslims — developed their own distinctive traditions, contributing to the multicultural character of Georgian society.

## GLOSSARY

**Chokha** — The traditional Georgian men's coat, fitted and woolen, with decorative cartridge holders (gziani) across the chest. Became the national costume of Georgia.

**Papakha** — A tall fur hat worn by Georgian men, part of the traditional costume. Also worn throughout the Caucasus region.

**Mep'e** — "King." The title used by the rulers of Kartli, Kakheti, and Imereti during this period.

**Batonishvili** — "Prince" or "royal son." A member of the royal family. During this period, the various branches of the Bagrationi dynasty used this title.

**Aznauri** — The Georgian noble class. During this period, the aznauri of the various kingdoms and principalities exercised significant local power.

**Tbilisi** — The capital of Georgia, rebuilt during this period with a distinctive blend of Georgian, Persian, and Ottoman architectural influences.

**Kartlis Tskhovreba** — "The Life of Kartli," the standard collection of Georgian medieval chronicles. Additional chronicles continued to be written during this period.

**Caravansarai** — A roadside inn with a central courtyard, providing accommodation for merchants and their animals. Numerous caravansarais were built in Georgian cities during this period.

**Eyalet** — An Ottoman administrative province. The Ottoman conquest of western Georgia created the Eyalet of Childir (Çıldır) in the Samtskhe region.

**Krtsanisi** — The site near Tbilisi where King Erekle II's army was defeated by the Persians in 1795, leading to the sack of the city.
