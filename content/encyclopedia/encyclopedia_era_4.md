# Era 4: Mongol Catastrophe (~1225 - ~1500 AD)

## ERA OVERVIEW

No catastrophe in Georgian history matches the devastation wrought by the Mongol invasions of the 13th century. In the space of just a few decades, the most powerful kingdom in the Caucasus was reduced to a shattered, tribute-paying vassal state, its cities sacked, its population decimated, its Golden Age achievements in ruins. The Mongol period, which lasted from the first invasions in the 1220s until the gradual disintegration of Mongol power in the late 14th and early 15th centuries, was the darkest chapter in medieval Georgian history — and it was followed by further devastations at the hands of Timur (Tamerlane) in the late 14th century. Yet through all this suffering, Georgian identity endured, sustained by the Orthodox Church, the mountain fortresses, and the unbreakable spirit of a people who refused to disappear.

The Mongol threat first appeared in 1220, when Mongol reconnaissance forces under Subutai and Jebe crossed the Caucasus during their famous "circumnavigation" of the Caspian Sea. The full-scale invasion came in 1236, when the Mongol armies of the Golden Horde, under the command of generals of the Great Khan Ögedei, swept through the Caucasus. Georgia's armies, still powerful but exhausted from recent conflicts, were overwhelmed by the Mongols' superior mobility, tactics, and military organization. King Rusudan, Queen Tamar's daughter, was forced to submit and accept Mongol suzerainty in 1243, beginning two centuries of foreign domination.

The Mongol period was not unrelievedly bleak. During periods of relative peace, Georgian princes were able to rebuild some of what had been destroyed, and cultural life continued, albeit at a reduced level. The Georgian Church maintained its institutional structure and continued to produce art and literature. Georgian warriors served in the Mongol armies, gaining military experience that would prove valuable in later centuries. And the political fragmentation that accompanied the decline of Mongol power led to the emergence of strong regional principalities — particularly in Imereti, Kakheti, and Samtskhe — that would become the nuclei of Georgian political life in the post-Mongol period.

The final blow of this catastrophic era came from Timur (Tamerlane), the Turco-Mongol conqueror who invaded Georgia no fewer than eight times between 1386 and 1403. Timur's campaigns were specifically targeted at Georgia's Christian identity: he destroyed churches, massacred clergy, and forcibly deported thousands of Georgians to Central Asia. The cities of Tbilisi, Kutaisi, and Gori were sacked and burned repeatedly. By the end of Timur's campaigns, Georgia was a wasteland, its population reduced by as much as two-thirds from its pre-Mongol peak.

Despite this devastation, the era also saw the emergence of a new kind of Georgian heroism — the resistance fighters who held out in mountain fortresses, the monks who preserved manuscripts and traditions in remote monasteries, and the kings who, in the darkest moments, found ways to keep the idea of Georgian independence alive. The struggle against overwhelming odds during this era became an enduring theme of Georgian culture and a source of national myth that would inspire future generations.

## KEY FIGURES

### Queen Rusudan (1223-1245)

Queen Rusudan, daughter of Queen Tamar and her consort David Soslan, inherited the Georgian throne at the worst possible moment. She was a young woman facing the full fury of the Mongol conquest. Rusudan attempted to organize resistance to the Mongol invasion, but the sheer military superiority of the Mongols made conventional defense impossible. After a series of devastating defeats, Rusudan was forced to accept Mongol suzerainty in 1243, agreeing to pay heavy tribute and provide troops for Mongol campaigns.

Rusudan's reign was marked by personal tragedy and political division. She sent her son David to the Mongol court as a hostage, where he was raised as a Muslim — a fact that caused enormous controversy in Christian Georgia. She also faced opposition from supporters of her nephew, another David, who claimed the throne. The resulting succession dispute after Rusudan's death in 1245 led to a period of dual rule ("David VI the Lesser" and "David VII Ulu"), which further weakened Georgia during a critical period. Despite these difficulties, Rusudan's perseverance in the face of impossible odds demonstrated the resilience that would characterize Georgian resistance throughout the Mongol period.

### King David VI Narin (1245-1293)

David VI, known as "Narin" (the Younger), was one of the two rival kings who ruled Georgia after the death of Queen Rusudan. While his cousin David VII Ulu controlled eastern Georgia from the Mongol-dominated capital of Tbilisi, David Narin established himself in the western region of Imereti, where the terrain was more defensible and Mongol control was weaker. Under David Narin's rule, Imereti became a center of Georgian cultural and political life, relatively sheltered from the worst excesses of Mongol domination.

David Narin's decision to establish a separate western Georgian kingdom was enormously consequential for Georgian history. It created the political division between eastern and western Georgia that would persist, with interruptions, for the next five centuries. But it also ensured that at least part of Georgia retained a degree of autonomy and cultural vitality during the darkest years of Mongol rule. David Narin patronized the Church and the arts, and his court in Kutaisi became a refuge for Georgian intellectuals and artists fleeing the Mongol-controlled east. His reign demonstrates that even in catastrophe, Georgians found ways to preserve their culture and institutions.

### King Demetre II "the Self-Sacrificer" (1270-1289)

Demetre II is one of the most tragic and heroic figures in Georgian history. He ruled eastern Georgia during a period of intense Mongol control and was caught between the demands of his Mongol overlords and the expectations of his Georgian subjects. When the Mongol Ilkhan Arghun suspected Demetre of plotting rebellion and demanded he present himself at the Mongol court, Demetre knew that going meant almost certain death. But he also knew that refusing would bring devastating Mongol retribution upon Georgia.

Demetre chose to go, accepting execution in 1289 to save his people from Mongol wrath. According to Georgian sources, he was offered his life if he converted to Islam, but refused, declaring "I was born a Christian and I will die a Christian." He was executed in the Mongol capital, and his body was later returned to Georgia for burial at Svetitskhoveli Cathedral in Mtskheta. The Georgian Orthodox Church canonized him as a martyr saint, and he is venerated as "Demetre the Self-Sacrificer" (Tavdadebuli). His story became one of the most powerful examples of personal sacrifice in Georgian national memory.

### King George V "the Brilliant" (1314-1346)

George V is the great exception in this era of decline — a king who actually managed to restore significant Georgian independence and prosperity during a temporary weakening of Mongol power. Known as "the Brilliant" (Mtsire) or "the Magnificent" (Gmertveli), George V skillfully exploited the fragmentation of the Mongol Empire and the conflicts between the Golden Horde and the Ilkhanate to reassert Georgian sovereignty. He stopped paying tribute, expelled Mongol garrisons from Georgian cities, and reasserted control over previously lost territories.

George V's reign saw a brief but significant cultural revival. He restored trade connections with the Mediterranean world, rebuilt damaged churches and fortresses, and brought a measure of stability and prosperity to his war-torn kingdom. He was also known for his diplomatic skill, maintaining good relations with both the Mongol states and the Byzantine Empire, and even establishing contacts with the rising powers of Western Europe. George V's reign showed that Georgia could still be strong when circumstances permitted, but his gains were fragile and would be swept away by the invasions of Timur a generation later.

### Timur (Tamerlane) (1336-1405)

Timur, the Turco-Mongol conqueror from Central Asia, was the most devastating enemy Georgia faced in the late medieval period. Between 1386 and 1403, Timur invaded Georgia eight times, each campaign bringing terrible destruction. Unlike the Mongols, who were primarily interested in extracting tribute and political submission, Timur seems to have been motivated in part by religious zeal: he specifically targeted Georgian churches and monasteries, destroyed Christian symbols, and massacred clergy. His campaigns depopulated entire regions, destroyed the agricultural base of the economy, and set back Georgian civilization by generations.

Timur's impact on Georgia was catastrophic. The population of the Georgian kingdoms declined dramatically, cities were reduced to ruins, and the political authority of the kings was shattered. The chronicler describing Timur's campaigns used language of apocalyptic horror. Yet even Timur could not destroy Georgia entirely. The mountain fortresses held out, the Church survived, and the Georgian people endured. The memory of Timur's invasions became a powerful part of Georgian historical consciousness, a symbol of the worst that could befall the nation — and of the possibility of survival even in the face of annihilation.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Mongol Invasion (1236-1243)

The Mongol conquest of Georgia was a prolonged and devastating process. Mongol forces first appeared in Georgia in the early 1220s, but the full-scale invasion began in 1236. The Mongol armies systematically conquered the Georgian lowlands, capturing the major cities and fortresses one by one. Georgian resistance was fierce but ultimately futile against the Mongols' superior numbers, mobility, and military organization. The decisive Battle near the fortress of Garni in 1239 ended effective Georgian military resistance, and Queen Rusudan's submission in 1243 formalized Mongol suzerainty. Georgia was required to pay heavy annual tribute and provide troops for Mongol military campaigns.

### The Division of the Kingdom (1290s)

The death of Queen Rusudan in 1245 triggered a succession crisis that led to the division of Georgia into two kingdoms: eastern Georgia (Kartli) under David VII Ulu, and western Georgia (Imereti) under David VI Narin. This division, initially caused by Mongol manipulation of the succession, became permanent, and for the next five centuries Georgia would be divided between rival eastern and western kingdoms. The fragmentation of Georgian political authority was one of the most damaging long-term consequences of the Mongol period, as it prevented the emergence of a single strong Georgian state capable of resisting external threats.

### Timur's Invasions of Georgia (1386-1403)

Timur invaded Georgia eight times between 1386 and 1403. His first invasion in 1386 captured Tbilisi after a fierce siege; the king, Bagrat V, was captured and forced to convert to Islam temporarily before escaping. Subsequent campaigns devastated the Georgian countryside, destroyed churches and monasteries, and forcibly deported thousands of Georgians. The most destructive campaigns were those of 1394-1396 and 1400-1403, which systematically devastated the regions of Kakheti, Hereti, and Samtskhe. By the end of Timur's campaigns, Georgia was a shadow of its former self, with its population drastically reduced, its economy in ruins, and its political institutions severely weakened.

### The Battle of Torgha (1444)

The Battle of Torgha, fought in 1444 near the Georgian-Black Sea coast, was a significant defeat for Georgian forces attempting to resist the expansion of the Ottoman Turks into western Georgia. King George VIII of Georgia led an army against the Ottoman forces of Sultan Murad II but was decisively defeated. The battle marked the beginning of sustained Ottoman pressure on western Georgia that would continue for centuries. Coming as it did on the heels of Timur's devastations, the defeat at Torgha underscored Georgia's inability to resist the great powers that surrounded it.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### Survival and Preservation of Manuscripts

Despite the devastations of the Mongol and Timurid periods, Georgian monks and scribes worked tirelessly to preserve the literary and artistic heritage of the Golden Age. Monasteries in remote areas — particularly in the mountains of Svaneti, Tusheti, and the western Georgian regions — became repositories of ancient manuscripts and works of art. The Svaneti region, with its isolated mountain villages and tower-fortresses, preserved an extraordinary collection of medieval Georgian manuscripts and icons that survived the catastrophes that destroyed similar collections in the lowlands. Many of the finest surviving examples of Georgian medieval art — icons, enamelwork, illuminated manuscripts — exist today because they were hidden away in remote mountain monasteries during the worst of the invasions.

### The Development of Georgian Tower Architecture

The defensive needs of this era stimulated the development of distinctive tower architecture, particularly in the mountainous regions of Svaneti, Tusheti, and Khevsureti. The Svan towers (koshki) — tall, narrow stone towers, often four or five stories high, built as family fortresses — are among the most iconic structures in the Caucasus. These towers served both as military defenses against raiders and as status symbols for the families that built them. The dense clusters of towers in villages like Ushguli, Mestia, and Chazhashi are among the most remarkable medieval landscapes in Europe and are recognized as UNESCO World Heritage Sites.

### Ecclesiastical Art in a Time of Crisis

Even in this era of destruction, Georgian ecclesiastical art continued to develop. Icons from this period show a distinctive style that combines the refined aesthetic of the Golden Age with a new expressiveness and emotional intensity, possibly reflecting the suffering of the era. The so-called "Palimpsest" manuscripts — medieval Georgian manuscripts whose original texts were scraped off and written over during the period of economic hardship, with the original texts later recovered through modern technology — are a poignant physical reminder of the material difficulties of the era and the determination of Georgian scribes to preserve their literary heritage.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### The Svan Towers of Upper Svaneti

The medieval tower villages of Upper Svaneti represent one of the most remarkable and distinctive architectural landscapes in Europe. Scattered across the dramatic mountain valleys of the Caucasus, these villages feature dense clusters of stone defensive towers (koshki) built by Svan families between the 9th and 13th centuries (with some dating to even earlier periods). The towers, typically 20-25 meters tall with four or five stories, were built of local stone without mortar and served as family fortresses, watchtowers, and storehouses. The village of Ushguli, at 2,200 meters one of the highest permanently inhabited settlements in Europe, contains nearly forty towers and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. These towers are the physical embodiment of the Georgian determination to survive against overwhelming odds.

### Ananuri Fortress Complex

Located on the Georgian Military Highway overlooking the Aragvi River valley, about 70 km north of Tbilisi, the Ananuri Fortress Complex is one of the best-preserved medieval fortifications in Georgia. The complex includes two churches (the Church of the Virgin, dating to the 17th century, and the older Church of the Assumption), massive defensive walls with towers, and a fortified citadel. While the current structures date primarily to the late medieval period (16th-17th centuries), the site has been fortified since antiquity. Ananuri controlled the strategically vital Darial Pass route and served as the seat of the eristavi (dukes) of Aragvi. Its dramatic setting and well-preserved architecture make it one of Georgia's most photographed monuments.

### Zarzma Monastery

The Zarzma Monastery, located in the Samtskhe region of southwestern Georgia, was an important religious center during the Mongol and post-Mongol periods. Founded in the 8th century but substantially rebuilt in the 14th century under the patronage of the Jakeli princes of Samtskhe, Zarzma became a symbol of Georgian cultural resilience in a time of foreign domination. The main church, dedicated to the Virgin, is a large domed building with rich exterior stone carving, including portraits of the Jakeli donors. The monastery survived the Mongol and Timurid invasions and continued to serve as an active religious community, a center of manuscript production, and a symbol of Georgian Christian identity in a contested border region.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### Adaptation to Mongol Warfare

The Georgians were forced to adapt their military methods in response to the Mongol threat. The heavy cavalry that had been so effective against the Seljuks was less effective against the highly mobile Mongol horse archers. Georgian commanders learned to avoid open-field battles against the Mongols and instead relied on fortress defense, mountain warfare, and guerrilla tactics. Some Georgian warriors adopted Mongol weapons and tactics, including the composite bow and the use of horse archers in support roles. Georgian troops who served in Mongol military campaigns gained valuable experience in large-scale warfare that would benefit later Georgian armies.

### Mountain Fortifications

The constant threat of invasion during this era led to a dramatic expansion of mountain fortifications throughout Georgia. In addition to the famous Svan towers, the Georgians built and maintained a network of hilltop fortresses, watchtowers, and fortified monasteries throughout the mountainous regions. These fortifications were designed to provide refuge for the civilian population during raids and to serve as bases for guerrilla resistance. Many were built in locations that were extremely difficult to attack, taking advantage of natural cliffs, ravines, and narrow mountain passes. The system of mountain fortifications allowed Georgian society to survive even when the lowlands were under foreign control.

### Chemical and Incendiary Weapons

Georgian sources from this period make reference to the use of chemical and incendiary weapons in the defense of fortifications, including "Greek fire" (a flammable liquid mixture) and various poisons. While the details are sketchy, it is clear that Georgian defenders used a variety of incendiary devices — including fire arrows, burning pitch, and potentially early gunpowder weapons — to defend their fortresses against besiegers. The use of such weapons reflects the desperation of the defensive warfare of this era and the willingness of Georgian defenders to use every available means of resistance.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

The constant warfare and economic disruption of this era led to a simplification of Georgian dress. The elaborate court costumes of the Golden Age gave way to more practical, functional clothing. Elite men wore simpler caftans and cloaks, often of locally produced wool rather than imported silk. Armor and weapons became a more prominent part of daily dress for the warrior class. Women's clothing also became simpler, though the wealthy still valued jewelry — particularly religious jewelry like crosses and icons — as both adornment and spiritual protection. The disruptions of trade routes meant that imported luxuries were harder to obtain, and local crafts became more important.

### Food and Subsistence

The economic devastation of the Mongol and Timurid periods severely disrupted Georgian agriculture. Many fertile lowland areas were depopulated and reverted to wilderness, and agricultural production declined dramatically. People relied more heavily on subsistence farming, hunting, and gathering. The mountain regions, less affected by the invasions, became more important for food production. The distinctive mountain cuisines of Svaneti, Tusheti, and Khevsureti — based on grains, dairy products, and preserved meats — reflect the adaptations made during this period. Despite the hardships, the Georgian feasting and toasting tradition survived as a vital expression of social cohesion and cultural identity.

### Social Structure

The Mongol period disrupted Georgian social structures profoundly. The old aristocracy was decimated — many nobles were killed in battle or executed by the Mongols, and others fled to the mountains or abroad. The Mongols elevated some Georgian nobles who cooperated with them and destroyed others who resisted, creating a new elite based on collaboration rather than traditional status. The Church, while weakened, remained the most stable institution and continued to exercise significant social authority. The destruction of cities led to a decline in the merchant and artisan classes, and Georgian society became more rural and more fragmented. The post-Mongol period saw the emergence of powerful regional princely families — the Jakeli in Samtskhe, the Dadiani in Samegrelo, the Gurieli in Guria, the Sharvashidze in Abkhazia — who would dominate Georgian political life for centuries.

### Religion

The Georgian Orthodox Church was both a victim of the Mongol and Timurid invasions and a primary vehicle for cultural survival. Timur specifically targeted churches and monasteries, destroying many and killing clergy. The physical infrastructure of the Church — churches, monasteries, manuscripts, icons — suffered enormous losses. Yet the Church endured, maintaining its institutional structure, continuing to produce religious literature and art (albeit at a reduced level), and providing the spiritual framework that enabled Georgian society to survive. The cult of saints — particularly martyrs like Demetre the Self-Sacrificer — provided powerful models of resistance and faith in the face of persecution. The Church's role as the guardian of Georgian national identity became even more important during this era of foreign domination.

## GLOSSARY

**Koshki** — A Svan defensive tower. These tall, narrow stone towers, built without mortar, are among the most distinctive structures in the Caucasus.

**Mongoloba** — The Georgian term for the period of Mongol domination (13th-15th centuries). One of the most traumatic periods in Georgian historical memory.

**Tavdadebuli** — "Self-sacrificing." The epithet of King Demetre II, who accepted martyrdom rather than betray his faith or bring retribution upon his people.

**Gmertveli** — "Magnificent" or "brilliant." The epithet of King George V, who briefly restored Georgian independence during a weakening of Mongol power.

**Imereti** — The western Georgian kingdom that emerged as a separate political entity during the Mongol period. Ruled from Kutaisi.

**Samtskhe** — A historic region in southwestern Georgia that became a powerful principality under the Jakeli family during the Mongol period.

**Jakeli** — A powerful Georgian princely family that ruled Samtskhe as an independent principality during and after the Mongol period.

**Kartli** — The eastern Georgian kingdom (also called "Kartli" or "Georgia" in the narrow sense) that was more directly controlled by the Mongols than western Georgia.

**Khevsureti** — A mountainous region of northeastern Georgia known for its distinctive tower architecture and preservation of ancient traditions.

**Ushguli** — A village community in Upper Svaneti, one of the highest permanently inhabited settlements in Europe, famous for its medieval towers.
