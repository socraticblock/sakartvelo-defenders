# Era 2: Age of Invasions (~630 - ~1089 AD)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The period from roughly 630 to 1089 AD was the most turbulent and testing era in Georgian history. If the Kingdom of Iberia had been forged between Rome and Persia, the medieval Georgian kingdoms were now squeezed between an even more daunting array of threats: the Arab Caliphate from the south, the Khazars and other steppe peoples from the north, the Byzantine Empire from the west, and various Turkish dynasties from the east. Georgia survived this era not through military strength alone — though fierce resistance was certainly part of the story — but through extraordinary resilience, diplomatic cunning, and the deep cultural cohesion provided by the Georgian Orthodox Church.

The era opened with the explosive expansion of the Arab Caliphate following the death of the Prophet Muhammad in 632 AD. Arab armies conquered the Sasanian Persian Empire by 651 and quickly turned their attention to the Caucasus. Tbilisi fell to the Arabs in 736, and for the next three centuries, the city was an Arab emirate under the nominal authority of the Caliph. The Arab period brought profound changes to Georgia: new agricultural techniques, new artistic influences, and the arrival of Islam in the region. But it also brought devastation, heavy taxation, and the forced migration of thousands of Georgians into the mountain fastnesses of the Caucasus.

Yet Georgia was never fully conquered. While the lowlands and major cities were occupied or dominated by foreign powers, the Georgian kingdoms and principalities — Tao-Klarjeti in the southwest, Abkhazia/Egrisi in the northwest, Kakheti and Hereti in the east, and Kartli in the center — maintained a stubborn, often precarious independence. From these fragmented polities, a slow process of cultural revival and political consolidation began in the 9th and 10th centuries. The monastic movement, centered on the great monasteries of Tao-Klarjeti (in what is now northeastern Turkey) and the Black Sea monastic communities, was a driving force in this revival, preserving and developing Georgian religious literature, art, and architecture even in times of political weakness.

The latter part of this era saw the emergence of powerful Georgian rulers who began to reclaim lost territory and rebuild a unified Georgian state. The Bagrationi dynasty, which would rule Georgia for a thousand years, rose to prominence in this period, initially as rulers of small principalities in the south and west but eventually claiming the kingship of all Georgia. King Bagrat III achieved the formal unification of the Georgian kingdoms in 1008, creating a united Georgian realm for the first time in centuries. His successors, particularly Bagrat IV and his energetic daughter Mariam, continued the work of consolidation and defense against external enemies.

This era connects to the Golden Age that follows because it was precisely the trials and struggles of these centuries that forged the military traditions, political institutions, and cultural confidence that made the Golden Age possible. The monasteries built during the darkest years of Arab domination produced the scholars, artists, and architects whose masterpieces would adorn the Golden Age. The military techniques developed fighting Arab and Turkish raiders would be perfected against the Seljuks. And the political unity painstakingly achieved through the 10th and 11th centuries provided the foundation upon which David the Builder would construct his empire.

## KEY FIGURES

### Ashot I the Great (c. 813-826/830 AD)

Ashot I was the prince of Iberia (Kartli) who laid the foundations for the revival of Georgian royal power in the early 9th century. A member of the Bagrationi family, Ashot was initially a vassal of the Arab emirs who controlled Tbilisi and the surrounding region. But as the power of the Abbasid Caliphate began to wane in the late 8th and early 9th centuries, Ashot skillfully expanded his authority over the Georgian territories, gaining recognition from both the Abbasid caliph in Baghdad and the Byzantine emperor in Constantinople.

Ashot's achievement was remarkable: he restored Georgian royal authority in a region that had been under foreign domination for over a century. He received the title of "kuropalates" (a senior Byzantine court title) from the emperor, and was recognized by the Abbasid caliph as the prince of Iberia. Although his actual power was limited to western Kartli and parts of the southern Georgian territories, Ashot's reign marked the beginning of the Bagrationi dynasty's ascent to supreme power in Georgia. He established a pattern of playing Byzantine and Arab powers against each other while quietly building Georgian strength — a diplomatic strategy that his successors would refine to great effect.

### King David III Kuropalates (c. 958-1001 AD)

David III was the ruler of the southern Georgian principality of Tao-Klarjeti (in what is now northeastern Turkey) and one of the most powerful Georgian princes of the 10th century. Known as "the Great" or "Kuropalates" (from his Byzantine court title), David was a brilliant military commander and a generous patron of Georgian culture. Under his rule, Tao-Klarjeti became the most powerful and culturally vibrant Georgian principality, and David himself was one of the wealthiest and most influential rulers in the Caucasus.

David is perhaps best known for his lavish patronage of monasteries and churches. He founded or supported numerous monastic communities in Tao-Klarjeti, including the great monasteries of Oshki, Parkhali, and Khakhuli, which became centers of Georgian religious and artistic life. The famous Khakhuli Triptych, one of the finest examples of medieval Georgian enamelwork, was created during David's reign. David's death in 1001 without direct heirs led to a succession crisis — he had bequeathed his territories to the Byzantine emperor Basil II, but the Georgian king Bagrat III contested this bequest, leading to decades of conflict between Georgia and Byzantium over the Tao-Klarjeti territories.

### King Bagrat III (c. 975-1014 AD)

Bagrat III was the first king of a unified Georgia, and his achievement of political unification in 1008 was one of the most important events in medieval Georgian history. Through a combination of inheritance, diplomacy, and military force, Bagrat united the western Georgian kingdom of Abkhazia (which his mother, Gurandukht, had ruled as regent) with the eastern Georgian territories of Kartli and Kakheti, creating a single Georgian kingdom stretching from the Black Sea to the Caspian.

Bagrat's reign was not without challenges. He faced opposition from powerful nobles who resented the centralization of power, and he had to contend with both Byzantine and Muslim neighbors who viewed a strong, unified Georgia with suspicion. His greatest architectural legacy is the Bagrati Cathedral near Kutaisi, a magnificent church built in the early 11th century that symbolized the unity and ambition of the new Georgian kingdom. Although the cathedral was later damaged by Ottoman artillery (and partially reconstructed in modern times), it remains one of Georgia's most iconic monuments. Bagrat III's achievement of unification set the stage for the even greater accomplishments of the Golden Age.

### Giorgi II (c. 1072-1089 AD)

Giorgi II, son of Bagrat IV, ruled Georgia during one of its most desperate periods. The Seljuk Turks, having conquered much of the Near East and Anatolia, launched devastating raids into Georgia in the 1070s and 1080s. Giorgi's armies were repeatedly defeated, major cities were sacked and burned, and the Georgian countryside was laid waste. The king himself was captured at least once by the Seljuks. The situation became so dire that Giorgi was forced to pay tribute to the Seljuk sultan and accept a humiliating peace.

Giorgi's reign is often judged harshly by Georgian historians, but it is important to recognize the overwhelming military superiority of the Seljuks at this time. The same Turkish armies that devastated Georgia had also destroyed the mighty Byzantine army at Manzikert in 1071, ending Byzantine power in Anatolia. Against such an opponent, no Georgian king could have prevailed without the kind of sweeping reforms that Giorgi's son and successor, David the Builder, would implement. Giorgi's greatest contribution to Georgian history was his decision to abdicate in favor of his young son David in 1089, recognizing that the crisis required a new approach and a new generation of leadership.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Arab Conquest and the Emirate of Tbilisi (736-1122)

The Arab conquest of the Caucasus was a prolonged and bloody process. Arab forces first raided Georgia in the 640s, and by 736, the Arab general Marwan ibn Muhammad (later the Umayyad caliph Marwan II) had conquered Tbilisi and established it as the capital of an Arab emirate. The Emirate of Tbilisi would endure for nearly four centuries, a Muslim-ruled enclave in the heart of the Georgian lands. The emirs of Tbilisi often exercised considerable independence from Baghdad and frequently warred with the surrounding Georgian kingdoms.

The Arab period was devastating for lowland Georgia but also brought important cultural influences. Arab administration introduced new agricultural practices (particularly irrigation) and new crops (cotton, rice, citrus fruits). Arabic became a language of scholarship and administration alongside Georgian. The cities of Tbilisi and Dmanisi became important centers of trade connecting the Caucasus with the broader Islamic world. However, the Arab emirs' demands for tribute and their periodic raids into Georgian territory were a constant source of suffering and resistance. The eventual recapture of Tbilisi by King David the Builder in 1122 would be one of the great triumphs of the Georgian Golden Age.

### The Battle of Sasireti (1042)

The Battle of Sasireti was a major civil conflict fought between the forces of King Bagrat IV of Georgia and a powerful rebel coalition led by the Liparitid clan, one of the most powerful noble families in the kingdom. The battle took place in 1042 in the region of Inner Kartli and resulted in a decisive defeat for the royal forces. King Bagrat was forced to flee, and the Liparitid faction briefly dominated Georgian politics.

The battle was significant because it demonstrated the fragility of Georgian royal authority even after the achievement of political unification under Bagrat III. The powerful noble families (dideli aznauri, or "great nobles") retained enormous military and economic power and could challenge the king when they felt their interests were threatened. The recurring conflict between royal centralization and aristocratic particularism would be a major theme throughout medieval Georgian history. Although Bagrat IV eventually regained his throne and subdued the Liparitids, the tension between crown and nobility would persist into the Golden Age and beyond.

### The Seljuk Invasions (1060s-1080s)

The arrival of the Seljuk Turks in the Caucasus in the 1060s was a catastrophe for Georgia. The Seljuks, a Turkic nomadic people who had converted to Islam and rapidly built a vast empire stretching from Central Asia to Anatolia, brought a style of warfare — fast-moving horse archer armies — that the Georgians were ill-equipped to counter. Beginning in the 1060s, Seljuk raiding parties poured through the Caucasus passes, devastating the Georgian countryside, sacking cities, and carrying off thousands of prisoners into slavery.

The Seljuk invasions reached their peak in the 1080s, when large-scale Turkish campaigns under Sultan Malik-Shah systematically conquered the major Georgian towns and fortresses of the southern and eastern regions. Tbilisi remained under its Arab emir, and the Georgian kingdom was reduced to a fraction of its former territory. The economic and demographic impact was devastating: agricultural land was abandoned, trade routes were disrupted, and entire regions were depopulated. This crisis created the conditions for the radical reforms of David the Builder, who inherited a kingdom on the brink of extinction and transformed it into the most powerful state in the Caucasus.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### The Tao-Klarjeti Renaissance

The monastic communities of Tao-Klarjeti (in what is now northeastern Turkey) produced an extraordinary flowering of Georgian religious culture in the 9th and 10th centuries. Monasteries like Oshki, Parkhali, Khakhuli, and Ishkhani became centers of manuscript illumination, icon painting, stone carving, and theological scholarship. The distinctive architectural style developed in Tao-Klarjeti — characterized by large domed churches with elaborate exterior stone carving, richly decorated portals, and sophisticated spatial planning — influenced Georgian architecture for centuries. The illuminated manuscripts produced in these monasteries, with their vivid colors and intricate geometric and figural designs, are among the finest achievements of medieval Georgian art.

### The Georgian Hagiographical Tradition

This era saw the composition of numerous lives of saints (martyrologies and hagiographies) that became classics of Georgian literature. Works like the "Life of Saint Nino," the "Martyrdom of Saint Shushanik," the "Life of Abo Tbileli" (the Arab perfumer who converted to Christianity and was martyred in Tbilisi), and the "Life of Gregory of Khandzta" combined religious devotion with rich historical detail, providing invaluable information about Georgian society, politics, and culture. These works were written in a distinctive Georgian literary language that blended classical influences with native rhetorical traditions, and they established a literary tradition that would continue to flourish in the Golden Age.

### The Development of Georgian Chant

Georgian liturgical chant (ghmerti's ghmertminda saghmomoebi — "songs to the glory of God") developed into a sophisticated polyphonic musical tradition during this period. Georgian Orthodox chant is unusual in the Christian world for its use of three-part polyphony — a practice that may reflect pre-Christian Georgian musical traditions. The chant tradition was preserved and transmitted orally in monastic communities for centuries before being notated, and it survives today as one of the most distinctive and beautiful aspects of Georgian worship. UNESCO has recognized Georgian polyphonic singing as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### Bagrati Cathedral, Kutaisi

Built by King Bagrat III in the early 11th century (consecrated in 1003), the Bagrati Cathedral was one of the most ambitious building projects of medieval Georgia. Located on a hilltop overlooking Kutaisi, the ancient capital of western Georgia, the cathedral was a massive domed church built of dressed stone, with elegant proportions and rich decorative carving on its exterior walls. The inscription on the north wall records that it was built "in the year 223 of the Georgian era" (1003 AD) during the reign of Bagrat III. The cathedral was severely damaged by Ottoman artillery in 1692, which destroyed the dome and parts of the walls, but it has been partially reconstructed in modern times and remains a powerful symbol of Georgian unity and royal ambition.

### Gelati Monastery

Founded by King David the Builder in 1106 (just at the end of this era, on the cusp of the Golden Age), Gelati Monastery near Kutaisi was conceived as a center of learning and culture that would rival the great monasteries of Byzantium. The monastery complex includes the main Cathedral of the Virgin (with its remarkable mosaic of the Virgin and Child, one of the finest medieval mosaics in the Caucasus), the Church of Saint George, and the Church of Saint Nicholas. David the Builder himself was buried at Gelati, and his tomb became a place of pilgrimage. The Gelati Academy, established within the monastery, was a major center of scholarly activity, producing translations, original theological and philosophical works, and scientific treatises. Gelati is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

### Oshki Monastery

Located in the Tao-Klarjeti region (now in northeastern Turkey), Oshki Monastery was one of the great centers of the Georgian monastic renaissance of the 10th century. Built between 963 and 973 under the patronage of the Georgian princes of Tao, Oshki is a large domed basilica with some of the finest exterior stone carving in all of Georgian architecture. The elaborate portal on the south facade, with its intricate interlace patterns, animal and human figures, and inscriptions in the Georgian Asomtavruli script, is a masterpiece of medieval stone carving. The monastery was also an important scriptorium, producing illuminated manuscripts that are now preserved in museums and libraries in Georgia and abroad.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### Cavalry Evolution

Georgian cavalry evolved significantly during this era in response to the changing nature of threats. Against the Arabs, Georgian heavy cavalry — armed with lances, swords, and protected by mail or lamellar armor — proved effective in set-piece battles. The Seljuk Turks, however, introduced a different challenge: highly mobile horse archer armies that avoided direct engagement and wore down opponents with constant harassing fire. The Georgians adapted by developing their own mounted archer capabilities and by learning to fight in more flexible formations. The experience of fighting the Seljuks would directly inform the military reforms of David the Builder in the following era.

### Fortification Development

This era saw significant advances in Georgian military architecture. The constant threat of invasion led to the construction of extensive fortification networks, including citadels, watchtowers, fortified monasteries, and walled towns. Georgian fortifications of this period typically featured massive stone walls, projecting rectangular or semicircular towers, fortified gateways with multiple barriers, and carefully designed killing zones. Many fortresses were built on commanding hilltops or at strategic points along roads and mountain passes. The Khertvisi Fortress, the Rabati Castle at Akhaltsikhe, and the numerous tower-fortresses of the Caucasus foothills all date from this period or were substantially rebuilt during it.

### Guerrilla Warfare in the Mountains

Perhaps the most distinctive Georgian military adaptation of this era was the development of guerrilla warfare techniques in the mountainous terrain. When conventional armies were defeated by superior enemy forces, Georgian fighters would retreat to the mountains and wage a war of ambush, raid, and harassment. Mountain passes were blocked with stone walls and abatis, and small bands of fighters would emerge to attack enemy supply lines and isolated detachments. This strategy of "strategic withdrawal to the mountains" became a recurring pattern in Georgian military history and allowed the Georgian kingdoms to survive periods when the lowlands were under foreign occupation.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

Georgian clothing during this period evolved under the influence of both Byzantine and Islamic fashion. Elite men wore long tunics with embroidered borders, wide trousers, and cloaks or mantles fastened at the shoulder. Headgear included various styles of caps and, for the nobility, ornate crowns or tiaras. Women's dress featured long, richly decorated robes with wide sleeves and elaborate headdresses. Jewelry continued to be important, with gold and silver rings, earrings, necklaces, and belt buckles displaying both local Georgian craftsmanship and imported styles. The introduction of Islam to the region brought new textile patterns and clothing styles, particularly in areas under Arab or Turkish influence.

### Food and Agriculture

Despite the political instability of this era, Georgian agriculture continued to develop, incorporating new crops and techniques introduced from the Arab world. Rice, cotton, citrus fruits, and various spices entered Georgian agriculture during the Arab period. Viticulture and winemaking remained central to the Georgian economy and culture, with the qvevri tradition continuing to produce distinctive wines. Bread was the staple food, accompanied by beans, lentils, cheese, and vegetables. Meat (primarily beef, mutton, and poultry) was eaten more frequently by the wealthy. Walnuts, hazelnuts, pomegranates, and grapes were important dietary staples. The feasting and toasting traditions that would become so famous in Georgian culture continued to develop during this period.

### Social Structure

Georgian society in this era was organized around a hierarchy of kings, great nobles (dideli aznauri), lesser nobles (aznauri), free commoners, and dependent peasants or slaves. The great noble families — the Bagrationi, the Liparitid-Orbelian, the Abulazre, and others — wielded enormous power, controlling large estates with their own military forces. The Church was another major landowner and social institution, with monasteries and churches owning extensive properties worked by peasant labor. The constant warfare of this era often disrupted the social order, as displaced populations fled to fortified towns or monasteries for protection. The monastic movement provided an important social safety net and a path of upward mobility for talented individuals of humble origin.

### Religion

The Georgian Orthodox Church was the central institution of Georgian cultural life during this era, providing continuity and identity through centuries of political fragmentation and foreign domination. The Church's monastic communities were centers of learning, manuscript production, and artistic creation. The Church also played an important diplomatic role, as Georgian monks and clergy maintained contacts with the Byzantine and Armenian churches. The arrival of Islam in the region added a new religious dimension: while the Arab emirs of Tbilisi and their followers were Muslim, the majority of the Georgian population remained firmly Orthodox. The coexistence (and occasional conflict) between Christianity and Islam in Georgia during this period was an important formative experience for Georgian religious culture.

## GLOSSARY

**Tao-Klarjeti** — A historical Georgian region in what is now northeastern Turkey. In the 9th-10th centuries, it was the center of a Georgian cultural and religious renaissance under the Bagrationi dynasty.

**Kuropalates** — A senior Byzantine court title, often granted to Georgian princes as a mark of honor and political alliance. Literally "keeper of the palace."

**Dideli Aznauri** — "Great nobles." The most powerful aristocratic families in medieval Georgia, who often rivaled the king in wealth and military power.

**Eristavi** — A Georgian territorial governor or duke, typically ruling a province (saeristavo) on behalf of the king.

**Sakdrisi** — Georgian term for gold mining or gold panning. The Colchian tradition of gold extraction continued in the western Georgian regions during this period.

**Mtsignobart Khutsesi** — "Literary man" or "scribe." The class of educated clergy and monks who preserved and developed Georgian literature during this era.

**Tbilisis Emirate** — The Arab-ruled emirate centered on Tbilisi, which existed from roughly 736 to 1122 AD. A Muslim polity surrounded by Christian Georgian kingdoms.

**Kldis Tskhali** — "Stone fortress." A term used for the numerous fortifications built throughout Georgia during this era of invasion and resistance.

**Saghmos** — "Psalm" or "chant." Georgian liturgical chant developed into a sophisticated polyphonic tradition during this period.

**Mokalake** — A free peasant or commoner in medieval Georgian society. Mokalake formed the backbone of the agricultural economy.
