11. Vision
Sakartvelo Defenders is a historically accurate tower defense game set across the full sweep  
of Georgian history, from the ancient Kingdom of Colchis to the modern Republic of  
Georgia. The game is built on three foundational pillars: education through gameplay,  
invisible blockchain integration, and cultural preservation. Players defend Georgian lands  
against historically authentic waves of invaders across ten distinct eras, each meticulously  
researched and visually distinct.
The core philosophy is that players should never feel like they are interacting with a  
blockchain product. All Web3 elements, including the SAKART token economy, NFT-based  
mastery scrolls, and tradable cosmetic skins, operate beneath the surface. The gameplay  
experience is indistinguishable from a premium Web2 tower defense title, with blockchain  
providing true asset ownership and player-driven economies as invisible infrastructure rather  
than visible friction.
Georgia's history is one of the most dramatic and underrepresented narratives in gaming.  
Positioned at the crossroads of Europe and Asia, Georgia has faced invasions from virtually  
every regional power: Persians, Romans, Arabs, Mongols, Ottomans, Persians again, and  
Russians. Each era produced unique military technologies, heroic figures, and cultural  
achievements that translate naturally into compelling tower defense mechanics. The game's  
ten-era  structure  ensures  players  experience  this  entire  arc,  learning  Georgian  history  
organically through hundreds of levels of strategic gameplay.
2. Game Overview
2.1 Core Gameplay Loop
The fundamental gameplay loop of Sakartvelo Defenders follows the classic tower defense  
formula, refined with historical authenticity and strategic depth. Players progress through  
levels by placing defensive towers along enemy approach paths, utilizing heroes with unique  
abilities, and activating consumable scrolls at critical moments. Each level presents a map  
inspired by real Georgian landscapes and fortifications, with enemy waves that reflect the  
actual historical invaders of each era.
The loop begins with level selection within an era. Players assess the map layout, choose  
their tower composition from their available roster, and place towers strategically during a  
build phase. Enemy waves then advance along predetermined paths, and players must  
manage their economy by earning resources from defeated enemies to build additional

2towers  or  upgrade  existing  ones.  Heroes  provide  active  abilities  that  players  trigger  
manually, adding a layer of real-time decision-making. Scrolls offer powerful one-time  
effects that can turn the tide of difficult engagements.
Completion is measured through a three-star system. Each star represents a progressively  
harder challenge tier within the same level: surviving all waves (1 star), completing with a  
life threshold (2 stars), and completing under a strict time or resource constraint (3 stars).  
Each star earns a one-time SAKART token reward, establishing a natural economy where  
progression drives token distribution without allowing replay farming.
2.2 Genre and Platform
Sakartvelo Defenders is a 2D isometric tower defense game. The isometric perspective  
provides clear visibility of enemy paths, tower ranges, and strategic positioning while  
maintaining the aesthetic warmth of illustrated historical environments. The 2D approach  
enables efficient asset production using AI-generated art with strict style bible consistency  
controls, making the project feasible for a solo developer.
The game is developed in Godot 4.x and deployed as a Progressive Web App (PWA). This  
architectural choice eliminates server hosting costs entirely, as the game runs client-side in  
any modern browser. The PWA wrapper enables offline gameplay for the full campaign,  
with  blockchain  transactions  queued  locally  during  chain  outages  and  submitted  
automatically when connectivity resumes. This ensures the player experience is never  
interrupted by infrastructure issues.
2.3 Target Audience
The primary audience encompasses tower defense enthusiasts, Web3 gaming early adopters,  
and players interested in history and cultural education. Tower defense fans will appreciate  
the strategic depth of synergy combinations, hero ability management, and the challenge of  
three-star completion across hundreds of levels. Web3 players will find genuine utility in the  
SAKART token economy without the usual crypto-game friction of mandatory wallet  
connections or pay-to-win mechanics.
A secondary but equally important audience is the Georgian diaspora community and  
cultural preservation advocates. Georgia has a rich cultural heritage that is underrepresented  
in mainstream gaming. By presenting this history through an accessible, engaging format,  
the game serves as both entertainment and educational tool. Historical pop-ups appear  
between levels, offering context about real events, figures, and locations depicted in the  
gameplay.

33. Eras
The game spans ten chronological eras, each representing a major period in Georgian history.  
Every era contains 20 levels, totaling 200 levels across the full campaign. The era structure  
follows a strict chronological progression, ensuring historical accuracy and allowing players  
to experience the complete narrative arc of Georgian civilization. Each era introduces new  
tower  types,  enemy  variants,  hero  characters,  and  synergy  combinations,  providing  
continuous mechanical evolution alongside historical progression.
The chronological approach is non-negotiable. Georgian history is a continuous story where  
events in one era directly shape the next. The Arab invasions of the 7th century created the  
conditions for David IV's reforms in the 11th century. The Mongol devastation of the 13th  
century led directly to the fragmentation that made Georgia vulnerable to Ottoman and  
Persian domination. By maintaining strict chronological order, the game preserves historical  
cause-and-effect relationships that would be lost with a thematic or arbitrary ordering.

3.1 Era 0: Ancient Colchis (~1500 BC – ~300 BC)
The opening era of Sakartvelo Defenders transports players to the ancient Kingdom of  
Colchis, the land of the Golden Fleece. This era draws from Greek mythological sources and  
archaeological evidence to recreate the earliest period of Georgian statehood. Colchis was a  
wealthy kingdom located on the eastern shore of the Black Sea, known for its gold mining,  
metalworking, and advanced agricultural practices. The Greek myth of Jason and the  
Argonauts reflects the historical reality of Greek interest in Colchian gold and trade routes.
Gameplay in this era introduces fundamental tower defense mechanics with a primitive,  
bronze-age aesthetic. Towers are stylized as wooden watchtowers, stone fortifications, and  
archer platforms. Enemy waves represent tribal raiders, early Greek expeditionary forces,  
and mythological creatures drawn from Georgian folklore such as the devi (giant demons).  
The environment features lush Colchian forests, river valleys, and the ancient city of Vani,  
one of the most important archaeological sites of the Colchian civilization.
The hero for this era is Medea, the Colchian princess from Greek mythology. In Georgian  
tradition, Medea is reclaimed as a powerful local figure rather than a Greek villain. Her  
abilities reflect her mythological association with magic and herbalism, providing healing  
buffs for nearby towers and poison damage to enemies. The educational pop-ups for this era  
cover the archaeological evidence for Colchian civilization, the reality behind the Golden  
Fleece legend (possibly a reference to gold-panning techniques using sheep fleeces), and the  
cultural exchange between Colchis and the ancient Greek world.
3.2 Era 1: Kingdom of Iberia (~300 BC – ~630 AD)
The Kingdom of Iberia (Kartli in Georgian) represents the formation of the Georgian state as  
a major regional power. This era spans over nine centuries, from the establishment of the  
Iberian kingdom under King Pharnavaz to the Arab conquest of Tbilisi in the 7th century. It  
encompasses the Roman-Parthian rivalry for control of the Caucasus, the Christianization of  
Georgia by Saint Nino in the 4th century, and the reign of King Vakhtang Gorgasali, who  
founded Tbilisi as the capital.
Towers in this era evolve from simple wooden structures to stone fortifications and early  
siege equipment. New enemy types include Roman legionaries, Parthian cavalry, Sassanid  
Persian forces, and early Arab raiders. The introduction of Christianity as the state religion

3.3 Era 2: Age of Invasions (~630 – ~1089 AD)
The  Age of Invasions covers  nearly five centuries  of continuous foreign pressure  on  
Georgian lands. Following the Arab conquest of Tbilisi in 645 AD, Georgia entered one of  
the most challenging periods in its history. The Tbilisi Emirate became a center of Islamic  
power in the Caucasus, while Byzantine and later Seljuk Turkish forces competed for  
influence over the fragmented Georgian principalities. This era is defined by the slow,  
determined consolidation of power by the Bagrationi dynasty, who would eventually unify  
Georgia and launch its golden age.
Mechanically, this era introduces the concept of fortified positions and defensive sieges.  
Maps feature crumbling Iberian fortresses being held against numerically superior Arab and  
Seljuk forces. New tower types include fortified gatehouses and mounted archer platforms.  
Enemy waves are larger and more aggressive, reflecting the overwhelming military pressure  
of the period. The difficulty curve increases significantly, as players must defend against  
sustained multi-wave assaults from enemies with greater variety and strength.
The heroes of this era represent the resistance and resilience of the Georgian people during  
centuries of foreign domination. Bagrationi princes and kings who led the slow reconquest  
feature as playable heroes, alongside religious figures who maintained Georgian cultural  
identity during the occupation. The educational content covers the Tbilisi Emirate, the Battle  
of Shirimni, the Battle of Sasireti, and the broader geopolitical context of Arab-Byzantine-
Seljuk rivalry in the Caucasus.
3.4 Era 3: The Georgian Golden Age (~1089 – ~1225 AD)
The Georgian Golden Age is the centerpiece of the game, representing the period when  
Georgia  reached  its  greatest  territorial  extent,  cultural  flowering,  and  military  power.  
Initiated by King David IV (David the Builder), who reformed the military, reunited the  
Georgian territories, and defeated the Seljuk Turks at the Battle of Didgori in 1121, this era

6culminated under Queen Tamar (r. 1184-1213), during whose reign Georgia controlled  
territories from the Black Sea to the Caspian and from the Caucasus Mountains to northern  
Iran.
This era features the most diverse tower roster in the game. Advanced fortifications,  
including  the  massive  stone  castles  that  dot  the  Georgian  countryside,  are  joined  by

challenged Georgian hegemony: remnants of Seljuk forces, Turkmen tribes, and northern

Both David IV and Queen Tamar are playable heroes in this era, as the user has confirmed  
that more heroes is better and both figures are essential to the period. David IV provides  
military leadership buffs that increase tower damage and reduce upgrade costs, reflecting his  
historical reputation as the great reformer. Queen Tamar offers diplomatic and governance  
abilities that generate bonus resources and provide temporary invulnerability to structures,  
reflecting her reign of prosperity. Additional heroes from this era may include the poet Shota  
Rustaveli and the military commander Zakaria Mkhargrdzeli.
3.5 Era 4: The Mongol Catastrophe (~1225 – ~1500 AD)
The Mongol Catastrophe marks the abrupt end of the Georgian Golden Age. Beginning with  
the first Mongol expeditions into the Caucasus in the 1220s and culminating in the complete  
subjugation of Georgia by 1243, this era represents the most devastating period in Georgian  
history. The Mongol conquest destroyed the centralized Georgian state, decimated the  
population, and ushered in nearly three centuries of fragmentation and decline. Georgia was  
divided into competing kingdoms and principalities that paid tribute to the Mongol Golden  
Horde and later the Ilkhanate.
Gameplay in this era shifts dramatically in tone. Players are no longer defending a unified,  
prosperous  kingdom  but  rather struggling to survive  overwhelming odds  with limited  
resources. Tower placement is constrained by crumbling infrastructure, and enemy waves  
are  massive  Mongol  armies  with  superior  mobility  and  firepower.  New  mechanics  
introduced in this era include retreat and evacuation objectives, where players must protect  
civilian populations as they withdraw to mountain fortresses. The environment art reflects  
the devastation: burned cities, abandoned villages, and fortified mountain redoubts.
The hero for this era represents the last generation of unified Georgian resistance, figures  
who  fought  desperately  against  the  Mongol  advance  before  the  kingdom  fractured.  
Educational pop-ups cover the Mongol administrative system in Georgia, the economic  
exploitation that drained the kingdom's wealth, and the cultural resilience that kept Georgian

3.6 Era 5: Between Empires (~1500 – ~1801 AD)
Between Empires covers the period of Georgian history when the fragmented Georgian  
kingdoms found themselves caught between the Ottoman Empire and Safavid (later Afsharid  
and Qajar) Persia. This era saw both devastating wars and remarkable cultural achievements.  
King Erekle II of Kartli-Kakheti stands as the towering figure of this period, attempting to  
unite the eastern Georgian kingdoms and navigate the impossible diplomatic situation  
between rival empires. Despite his efforts, including a disastrous alliance with Catherine the  
Great of Russia, Georgia was ultimately unable to maintain its independence.
Towers in this era reflect the evolving military technology of the early modern period.  
Gunpowder weapons appear for the first time, alongside traditional Georgian defensive  
structures. Maps are set in the three major Georgian kingdoms of the period: Kartli, Kakheti,  
and Imereti, each with its own architectural style and strategic challenges. Enemy waves  
alternate between Ottoman and Persian forces, each with distinct unit compositions and  
attack patterns that require different defensive strategies.
King Erekle II is the primary hero of this era, providing abilities that combine military  
leadership  with  diplomatic  finesse.  His  special  ability  temporarily  reduces  all  enemy  
damage, reflecting his historical skill at negotiation even in wartime. The educational content  
covers the Battle of Krtsanisi (1795), one of the most tragic events in Georgian history,  
where the Persian army of Agha Mohammad Khan devastated Tbilisi despite Erekle's  
desperate defense. This event directly led to Georgia's incorporation into the Russian  
Empire.
3.7 Era 6: Russian Empire (~1801 – ~1918 AD)
The Russian Empire era covers the period from Georgia's annexation by Russia in 1801 to  
the collapse of the empire in 1918. Despite the loss of political independence, this era was  
marked by a remarkable cultural renaissance. Georgian literature flourished with the works  
of Ilia Chavchavadze and Akaki Tsereteli, theater and music experienced a golden age, and  
Georgian national identity was consciously preserved and strengthened. The era also saw  
major infrastructure development, including the construction of the Transcaucasian Railway  
connecting the Black and Caspian Seas.
Gameplay in this era introduces industrial-age military technology. Towers include fortified  
machine gun positions, artillery emplacements, and barricaded urban streets. Enemy waves  
represent the various conflicts of the period: the Caucasian War against Imam Shamil's

Maps are set in rapidly modernizing Tbilisi, the mountain villages of Svaneti, and the Black

The heroes of this era are primarily cultural and intellectual figures rather than military  
commanders. Ilia Chavchavadze provides abilities that boost tower efficiency and resource  
generation, reflecting his role as a national leader and modernizer. Other potential heroes

3.8 Era 7: First Democratic Republic (1918 – 1921 AD)
The First Democratic Republic of Georgia (1918-1921) was the first modern democratic  
state in the Caucasus and one of the first democratic republics to emerge from the ruins of the  
Russian Empire. Led by the Social Democratic (Menshevik) government of Noe Jordania,  
Georgia declared independence on May 26, 1918, and spent three chaotic years building a  
multi-ethnic, multi-party democratic state while fighting wars against Armenia, Azerbaijan,  
White Russian forces, and the Bolshevik Red Army.
Despite its brief existence, the First Republic achieved remarkable accomplishments: a  
constitution  guaranteeing  universal  suffrage  and  minority  rights,  land  reform,  judicial  
independence, and recognition by major international powers including the League of  
Nations. The era ended when the Bolshevik Red Army invaded in February 1921, forcing the  
democratic government into exile. This brief democratic experiment remains a powerful  
symbol in modern Georgian politics and national identity.
Gameplay in this era features early 20th-century military technology: rifles, machine guns,  
and  artillery.  Enemy  waves  represent  the  multiple  threats  faced  by  the  republic  
simultaneously. Maps are set in Tbilisi, the Black Sea coast, and the border regions where the  
republic fought its defensive wars. The hero is Noe Jordania, whose abilities focus on  
diplomatic coordination that temporarily strengthens all towers and generates emergency  
resources.  Educational  pop-ups  cover  the  constitution,  the  international  recognition  
campaign, and the tragic circumstances of the Bolshevik invasion.
3.9 Era 8: The Soviet Century (1921 – ~1991 AD)
The Soviet Century covers the seventy years of Georgian history within the Soviet Union.  
This era is presented with historical honesty, neither glorifying the Soviet period nor erasing  
its complexities. Georgia experienced both severe repression and significant modernization  
during this time. The most controversial figure is Joseph Stalin (born Ioseb Jughashvili in

Towers in this era reflect Soviet military technology: reinforced concrete bunkers, anti-
aircraft positions, and fortified checkpoints. Enemy waves represent the various conflicts

during  the  Soviet  period.  The  educational  content  covers  the  Great  Purge  (which  
disproportionately affected Georgia), World War II, the 1978 language protests, and the  
growing independence movement of the late 1980s.
3.10 Era 9: Modern Georgia (~1991 – Present)
The final era brings players to the modern Republic of Georgia, from the chaotic first years of  
independence through the Rose Revolution of 2003 to the present day. This era covers the  
1991-1993 civil war and war in Abkhazia, the economic collapse of the 1990s, the peaceful  
Rose Revolution that ousted the Shevardnadze government, the 2008 war with Russia, and  
the ongoing process of democratic consolidation and Euro-Atlantic integration.
Towers in this era represent modern military technology: precision-guided weapon systems,  
drone surveillance platforms, and fortified military outposts. Enemy waves reflect the  
conflicts of modern Georgia: separatist forces, irregular militias, and conventional military  
threats. Maps are set in modern Tbilisi, the conflict zones of South Ossetia and Abkhazia, and  
the strategic Georgian Military Highway connecting Tbilisi to Russia through the Caucasus  
Mountains.
This era's heroes are drawn from modern Georgian history, figures who shaped the country's  
transition from Soviet republic to independent democracy. The educational content covers  
the challenges of post-Soviet state-building, the achievements and controversies of the  
Saakashvili era, and Georgia's current strategic position as a Western-oriented democracy in  
a region of renewed Russian assertiveness. The final levels of this era serve as the game's

4. Tower System
The tower system is the mechanical core of Sakartvelo Defenders. Ten distinct tower types  
provide the player's defensive toolkit, each filling a specific strategic role. Towers are  
introduced gradually across the ten eras, with each era introducing one new tower type that  
reflects the military technology of that historical period. Every tower receives era-specific  
visual  skins  to  maintain  historical  authenticity  while  preserving  the  underlying  game  
mechanics that players have mastered.
The tower progression follows a clear design principle: newer towers add strategic options  
rather than rendering older ones obsolete. An Archer Tower from Era 0 remains viable in Era  
9 because it occupies a unique niche (fast single-target damage at low cost) that no later  
tower perfectly replicates. This ensures that all ten tower types remain relevant throughout  
the  full  200-level  campaign,  rewarding  players  who  have  mastered  diverse  tower  
compositions.

4.1 Tower Upgrade System
Each tower can be upgraded through three levels, with each upgrade increasing damage,  
range,  attack  speed,  or  adding  special  effects.  Upgrade  costs  scale  exponentially,  
encouraging players to invest in a focused defense rather than spreading resources thinly  
across many weak towers. At maximum upgrade level (Level 3), towers gain a unique special  
ability specific to their type: Archer Towers gain a chance for critical hits, Catapults gain  
increased splash radius, and so on.
Tower upgrades are purchased with in-game resources earned from defeating enemies. This  
resource economy is entirely separate from the SAKART token economy and functions as  
the core gameplay loop driver. Players must make strategic decisions about when to invest in  
upgrades versus placing additional towers, creating meaningful choices every level. The  
upgrade  system  is  designed  to  feel  impactful:  a  Level  3  Archer  Tower  should  feel  
dramatically more powerful than a Level 1, both visually and mechanically.
4.2 Tower Synergy System
Synergies  are  activated  when  specific  tower  combinations  are  placed  within  a  3-tile  
adjacency radius of each other. Synergies provide powerful bonuses that encourage diverse  
tower compositions and strategic map planning. Each synergy combination is introduced in  
the  era  where  its  component  towers  are  most  thematically  appropriate,  with  new  
combinations becoming available as players unlock more tower types.
The synergy system creates emergent strategic depth. A player who places an Archer Tower  
near a Cavalry Outpost might discover a synergy that increases both units' attack speed,  
creating a rapid-response kill zone. Another player might combine a Magic/Shrine Tower  
with a Wall/Barrier to create a slowing zone that funnels enemies into a devastating Catapult  
kill zone. The system rewards experimentation and map-specific planning, ensuring that no  
single strategy dominates across all levels.
A comprehensive list of synergy combinations is documented in the Synergy Reference  
appendix (see Section 24). With ten tower types, there are 45 possible two-tower pairs and  
120 possible three-tower triples, though only a curated subset of the most strategically  
interesting  and  thematically  appropriate  combinations  will  be  implemented.  Each  era

12introduces approximately 2-3 new synergy combinations, building up to a rich strategic  
toolkit by the final era.
5. Enemies
Enemy design in Sakartvelo Defenders follows two core principles: historical authenticity  
and mechanical clarity. Every enemy type is based on a real historical military unit or cultural  
archetype from Georgian history, modified as needed for gameplay balance. Enemies are  
visually designed to be instantly distinguishable, with clear silhouettes, color coding by  
threat level, and animation that communicates their behavior (fast enemies move erratically,  
armored enemies have deliberate, heavy animations).
Enemy HP and damage scale progressively across the 200 levels, but the scaling is carefully  
calibrated to maintain challenge without requiring numerical inflation that makes earlier  
towers irrelevant. Instead of simply increasing HP values, later eras introduce enemy types  
with new mechanics: shields that block frontal damage, healing abilities that restore HP to  
nearby enemies, flying units that bypass ground-based tower ranges, and boss enemies with  
unique phase-based attack patterns.
5.1 Enemy Categories
Enemies are divided into five broad categories that define their mechanical behavior.  
Infantry units follow standard path movement at moderate speed and HP, forming the  
backbone of most waves. Cavalry units move faster and have higher HP but follow the same  
paths as infantry. Siege units are slow but devastating, capable of damaging player towers  
directly. Flying units ignore path constraints and can only be targeted by towers with anti-air  
capabilities. Boss enemies appear at the end of specific levels as challenging multi-phase  
encounters.
Each era introduces era-specific variants within these categories. Era 0 enemies are tribal  
warriors and mythological creatures with low HP but fast movement. Era 2 enemies include  
heavily armored Arab cavalry and siege engines. Era 4 Mongol enemies feature devastating  
horse archers with hit-and-run tactics. Era 9 enemies include mechanized infantry and drone  
swarms that require modern tower capabilities to counter. The visual evolution of enemies  
across eras serves as both a gameplay progression mechanic and a visual history lesson.
5.2 Boss Encounters
Boss encounters appear at the end of every 5th level within each era (levels 5, 10, 15, and 20).  
Each boss is a named historical or mythological figure with a multi-phase fight that requires  
specific strategies to defeat. Bosses have dramatically higher HP than standard enemies and

--- Page 18 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
13feature  unique  abilities  that  challenge  the  player's  tower  placement  and  resource  
management.
Examples of planned bosses include the Colchian Dragon (Era 0), a mythical beast requiring  
coordinated multi-tower attacks; the Arab Emir of Tbilisi (Era 2), who summons reinforcing  
waves; the Mongol General Subutai's proxy (Era 4), who speeds up all enemy movement  
across the map; and Agha Mohammad Khan (Era 5), who deals area damage to nearby  
towers. Each boss fight is designed to be a memorable set-piece moment that rewards  
strategic thinking and diverse tower compositions.
6. Heroes
Heroes are special units that players deploy alongside their tower defenses. Each hero has a  
unique set of active abilities that the player triggers manually during combat, adding a layer  
of real-time decision-making to the tower defense formula. Heroes are era-specific, meaning  
they can only be used in levels belonging to their historical period. This design encourages  
players to engage with each era's historical context and ensures that hero abilities feel  
thematically appropriate.
The user has confirmed that more heroes is better. The design philosophy is to provide at  
least one hero per era, with major eras featuring two to three heroes to reflect the abundance  
of notable historical figures. As confirmed, both David IV and Queen Tamar are playable  
heroes in Era 3, representing the two most important figures of the Georgian Golden Age.  
Stalin is handled historically in Era 8, presented factually in educational content but not as a  
playable hero character.

6.1 Hero Abilities and Cooldowns
Each hero has three abilities: one basic ability on a short cooldown (15-20 seconds), one  
advanced ability on a medium cooldown (45-60 seconds), and one ultimate ability on a long  
cooldown (120-180 seconds). Basic abilities provide tactical benefits like minor damage  
buffs or healing. Advanced abilities can shift the flow of an individual wave. Ultimate  
abilities are game-changing effects that, when timed correctly, can save a failing defense or  
enable a perfect three-star run.
When a hero is defeated (all HP depleted), they enter a cooldown period before they can be  
redeployed. During this cooldown, the player must rely solely on their tower defenses. The  
cooldown duration scales with the hero's power level, ensuring that losing a powerful hero  
like Queen Tamar is more punishing than losing a support-oriented hero. This mechanic  
encourages players to position heroes strategically rather than exposing them to unnecessary  
damage.
6.2 Hero Progression
Heroes level up through gameplay by participating in battles. Each battle a hero participates  
in grants experience points based on the enemies defeated. Leveling up improves hero base  
stats (HP, damage) and reduces ability cooldowns. Hero levels are stored on-chain as part of  
the player's account data, ensuring that progression is persistent and portable. The maximum

--- Page 20 ---

15hero level is capped to prevent power inflation, with the cap set to ensure that a max-level  
hero is powerful but not capable of solo-carrying a level without tower support.
7. Maps
Each of the 200 levels is set on a unique map inspired by real Georgian landscapes,  
fortifications, and cities. Maps are designed with the isometric 2D perspective in mind,  
ensuring clear visibility of enemy paths, tower placement zones, and strategic elevation  
points. The art style for each map reflects the architectural and environmental characteristics  
of the era, creating a visual journey through Georgian history that parallels the mechanical  
progression.
Map design follows several key principles. First, every map must have at least three viable  
tower placement strategies to encourage replay and experimentation. Second, maps must  
feature at least one elevation mechanic, where towers placed on higher ground receive  
increased range, adding a spatial dimension to tower placement decisions. Third, each map  
must include at least one chokepoint where players can concentrate defensive fire, balanced  
by at least one wide-open section that requires spread defenses. These principles ensure  
strategic variety across all 200 levels.
7.1 Map Themes by Era
Era 0 maps feature prehistoric Colchian landscapes: dense forests, river crossings, and the  
ancient gold-panning sites of the Rioni River. Era 1 maps showcase the early urban centers of  
Mtskheta and Uplistsikhe, with cave-city layouts providing unique pathing challenges. Era 2  
maps depict fortified monasteries and mountain passes under siege from Arab and Seljuk  
forces. Era 3 maps feature the grand castles and cathedrals of the golden age, including Gelati  
Monastery and the cave city of Vardzia.
Era 4 maps show the devastation of the Mongol period: burning cities, abandoned fortresses,  
and desperate mountain retreats. Era 5 maps are set in the three kingdoms of Kartli, Kakheti,  
and  Imereti,  with  distinct  architectural  styles  for  each.  Era  6  maps  feature  rapidly  
modernizing Tbilisi with its mix of old and new architecture. Era 7 maps depict the war-torn  
landscapes of the independence period. Era 8 maps showcase Soviet-era industrial zones and  
mountain  villages.  Era  9  maps  present  modern  Georgia  with  contemporary  military  
installations and urban environments.
7.2 Elevation Mechanic
The elevation mechanic is a core map feature that rewards strategic tower placement. Maps  
are divided into ground-level and elevated positions, with higher positions providing a range

--- Page 21 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
16bonus to any tower placed there. The range bonus is approximately 15-20%, giving elevated  
towers a meaningful but not overwhelming advantage. Maps are designed so that the best  
elevated positions are also the most exposed or hardest to defend, creating risk-reward  
decisions for players.
Additionally, arrow-type and projectile towers benefit disproportionately from elevation,  
gaining both increased range and a damage bonus due to the improved angle of attack. This  
means an Archer Tower on a hill is dramatically more effective than one on flat ground,  
encouraging players to prioritize elevated positions for their projectile-based defenses while  
using melee-adjacent towers like Wall/Barrier units to control ground-level approaches.
8. Tutorial
The tutorial is integrated into the first five levels of Era 0, teaching core mechanics  
organically through gameplay rather than separate tutorial screens. The philosophy is that  
players learn by doing, with contextual hints appearing only when the game detects that a  
player is struggling. The tutorial levels are designed to be completable by any player  
regardless of experience with tower defense games, while still being engaging enough to  
retain experienced players.
Level 1 introduces tower placement: the player is given a single Archer Tower and must  
place it to defend against a slow, weak enemy wave. Level 2 introduces the economy system,  
requiring players to earn resources from kills and invest them in a second tower. Level 3  
introduces tower upgrades by presenting a wave that cannot be defeated with Level 1 towers  
alone. Level 4 introduces the hero system with Medea's basic ability. Level 5 introduces  
scrolls as a consumable resource and features the first mini-boss encounter. By the end of  
these five levels, players have mastered all core systems and are ready for the full campaign  
experience.
Each tutorial level includes a brief historical pop-up between levels that introduces the world  
of Ancient Colchis. These pop-ups are concise (2-3 sentences) and focus on creating intrigue  
rather than delivering encyclopedic information. The deeper educational content is reserved  
for the main campaign, where players who are already invested in the gameplay will be more  
receptive to historical context.
9. Progression
Progression in Sakartvelo Defenders operates on two parallel tracks: gameplay progression  
and economic progression. Gameplay progression is the journey through 200 levels across  
10 eras, with each level unlocked by completing the previous one. Economic progression is

--- Page 22 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
17the accumulation of SAKART tokens earned from star completions, which can be used to  
purchase cosmetic items, mastery scrolls, and access to competitive features.
The progression system is designed to be generous with gameplay content while strictly  
controlling economic rewards. All 200 levels are accessible to every player without any  
payment requirements. The SAKART economy is driven entirely by in-game achievement:  
each star earned on a level grants a one-time token reward, with higher stars granting larger  
rewards. Because each star can only be earned once per level, there is zero incentive for  
replay farming, and the total token supply is mathematically bounded by the number of  
players and levels.
9.1 Era Unlock Requirements
Eras are unlocked sequentially. To unlock the next era, a player must earn at least 1 star (the  
basic completion star) on a minimum number of levels in the current era. The requirement is  
set at 15 out of 20 levels, allowing players to skip the most difficult levels while still  
demonstrating sufficient mastery. This ensures that players experience most of each era's  
content before moving forward, maintaining the chronological educational narrative without  
creating frustrating progression blockers.
The era unlock requirement is intentionally permissive rather than restrictive. The game's  
philosophy is that players should be encouraged to explore, not punished for difficulty. A  
player who struggles with specific levels can move forward after completing the majority,  
then return later with better towers, higher-level heroes, and more experience to tackle the  
challenges they skipped. The three-star system provides the difficulty ceiling for dedicated  
players, while the one-star unlock provides the accessible floor for casual players.
9.2 Star System
Each level offers three stars, representing three tiers of completion challenge. The first star is  
earned by surviving all enemy waves without losing all lives. The second star requires  
completing the level while retaining a minimum percentage of lives (typically 50% or more).  
The third star requires completing the level under an additional constraint, which varies by  
level  and  may  include  time  limits,  resource  caps,  tower  count  limits,  or  no-upgrade  
restrictions.
Each star grants a one-time SAKART reward. The reward amounts scale with the difficulty  
of the era: early era stars grant fewer tokens, while later era stars grant progressively more.  
This scaling reflects both the increased difficulty of later content and the economic design  
goal of distributing tokens over the long term rather than front-loading rewards in early

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
18content. Once a star is earned, replaying the level generates zero additional SAKART,  
eliminating replay farming as a concern entirely.
10. Scrolls and Mastery
Scrolls are consumable items that provide powerful temporary effects during gameplay.  
They are the primary tactical resource that players manage during a level, alongside tower  
placement and hero ability usage. Scrolls are single-use: activating a scroll consumes it  
permanently, and players must acquire new scrolls to replenish their inventory. Each player  
account can hold a maximum of 50 scrolls at any time, preventing hoarding and encouraging  
strategic use.
Scrolls are divided into two categories: standard scrolls and mastery scrolls. Standard scrolls  
provide gameplay effects during levels (damage boosts, resource generation, crowd control,  
healing, etc.) and are earned through gameplay progression or purchased with SAKART  
tokens. Mastery scrolls provide permanent passive bonuses to specific tower types or heroes  
and are represented as ERC-721 NFTs on the blockchain, making them tradeable on the  
marketplace.
10.1 Standard Scrolls
Standard scrolls come in several types, each providing a different tactical effect. Damage  
scrolls boost tower damage output for a limited duration. Economy scrolls generate bonus in-
game resources. Crowd control scrolls freeze or slow all enemies on the map. Healing scrolls  
restore HP to damaged towers. Shield scrolls grant temporary invulnerability to a targeted  
tower or hero. Each scroll type has multiple tiers of potency, with higher tiers providing  
stronger effects.
Standard scrolls are consumed when activated during a level. They are not blockchain assets  
and cannot be traded between players. This design keeps the tactical resource economy  
simple and predictable. Standard scrolls can be earned as rewards for completing levels with  
3 stars, purchased from an in-game shop using in-game currency (not SAKART), or  
occasionally received as daily login bonuses. The scroll inventory cap of 50 ensures that  
players must make meaningful choices about which scrolls to bring into each level.
10.2 Mastery Scrolls (ERC-721)
Mastery scrolls are NFT-based permanent passive bonuses. Unlike standard scrolls, mastery  
scrolls are not consumed during gameplay. Instead, they are activated in the player's loadout  
before a level begins and provide their bonus for the entire level duration. Mastery scrolls are  
type-based, not unique: there is no limit on how many copies of a specific mastery scroll can

--- Page 24 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
19exist, but each player can only activate each type of mastery scroll once per level, with a  
maximum stack of 3 copies of the same type providing increasing bonuses.
The stack system works as follows: activating 1 copy of a mastery scroll provides a 3% bonus  
to the relevant stat, 2 copies provide 6%, and 3 copies provide the maximum of 9%. This cap  
ensures that mastery scrolls enhance gameplay without breaking balance. For example, a  
player with 3 copies of the 'Archer Tower Mastery' scroll would have their Archer Towers  
deal 9% more damage for the duration of any level where the scroll is activated. Mastery  
scrolls  are  tradeable  on  the  marketplace,  creating  a  player-driven  economy  for  these  
permanent upgrades.
10.3 SAKART Boost Scroll
As confirmed during design review, there is NO SAKART Boost scroll. The token economy  
is driven entirely by star-based earning, and no consumable item should exist that accelerates  
or modifies SAKART rewards. This prevents economic exploits and keeps the token  
distribution system clean and predictable.
11. Skins and Binding
Cosmetic skins are the primary visual customization system and the main use case for the  
SAKART token economy. Skins change the visual appearance of towers and heroes without  
affecting  gameplay  mechanics.  They  are  implemented  as  ERC-1155  tokens  on  the  
Immutable zkEVM blockchain, enabling frictionless trading on the marketplace. Skins are  
purely cosmetic: a tower with a skin has identical stats and abilities to the same tower without  
a skin.
The skin system is designed around the era structure. Each tower type has one default skin  
per era (automatically applied when playing levels in that era) and multiple premium skins  
that players can acquire through gameplay rewards or marketplace purchases. Premium skins  
feature more detailed art, special effects, and animation variations that make them visually  
distinct from the default era skins.
11.1 Skin Binding
When a player equips a skin to a tower, the skin becomes bound to that specific tower  
instance. Bound skins cannot be traded on the marketplace until they are unbound, which  
costs a small amount of SAKART. This binding mechanism serves two purposes: it creates a  
SAKART  token  sink  (burn  mechanism)  through  the  bind/unbind  fee,  and  it  prevents  
marketplace speculation on skins that are actively being used in gameplay.

--- Page 25 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
20The bind/unbind fee is set low enough to not discourage players from experimenting with  
different skins, but high enough to create meaningful economic friction against rapid  
marketplace trading. The exact fee amount is subject to balance testing and may vary based  
on the skin's rarity tier. Common skins have minimal bind/unbind fees, while rare and  
legendary skins have higher fees, reflecting their greater marketplace value.
11.2 Marketplace Tooltip
As confirmed during the design review with Hermes, every item on the marketplace displays  
a clear tooltip showing: what the item does, which era/level it applies to, its current bind  
status, and the cost to unbind if applicable. This transparency ensures that players can make  
informed purchasing decisions without needing to consult external resources. The tooltip  
system is especially important for mastery scrolls, where the stack bonus progression (3%,  
6%, 9%) must be clearly communicated.
12. Player vs. Player (PvP)
PvP in Sakartvelo Defenders is an asynchronous attack-then-defend format designed for  
fairness and accessibility. Unlike synchronous PvP tower defense, which requires both  
players to be online simultaneously, the asynchronous format allows players to attack an  
opponent's preset defense at their convenience, and defend against incoming attacks with AI-
managed towers. This design respects the player's time while maintaining competitive depth.
12.1 Attack-Then-Defend Format
Each PvP engagement consists of two phases. In the Attack phase, the player selects a  
league-level map and sends enemy waves against the opponent's pre-built defense. In the  
Defend phase, the player's own defense is tested against waves sent by their opponent. The  
winner is determined by total score across both phases, where score is based on lives  
remaining, time survived, and enemies defeated.
The turn order for who attacks first is determined by a Rock-Paper-Scissors (RPS) mechanic  
at the start of each engagement. Both players secretly choose their throw, and the winner  
attacks first. Attacking first provides a slight psychological advantage (knowing the defense  
layout) but also reveals information that the defender can use in the second phase. This  
simple RPS mechanic adds a layer of pre-game strategy without introducing complexity that  
would alienate casual players.

--- Page 26 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
2112.2 Loadout System
PvP uses identical loadouts for both players to ensure absolute fairness. When a PvP  
engagement begins, both players are assigned the same tower roster, hero, and scroll  
selection. This eliminates pay-to-win concerns, as no player can gain an advantage through  
superior NFTs or token spending. The skill expression in PvP comes entirely from tower  
placement strategy, timing of hero abilities, and scroll usage.
There are no re-rolls on scrolls in PvP. As confirmed during design review, allowing re-rolls  
would create imbalance by letting players gamble for optimal scroll draws. Each player  
receives a fixed scroll selection at the start of the engagement and must make the best use of  
what they are given. This constraint encourages strategic planning and rewards players who  
can adapt their tactics to available resources rather than relying on lucky draws.
12.3 Defense Setup
Players build their defense on a standardized PvP map layout before entering matchmaking.  
The defense setup uses pre-built tower configurations that are executed by AI when the  
player is being attacked. The AI uses a predefined priority system: towers target the enemy  
closest to the exit, use abilities when enough enemies are in range, and do not make  
placement changes during the defense. This ensures that the defense performs consistently  
regardless of whether the player is online.
The PvP league system is organized by skill tiers rather than eras. As confirmed during  
design  review,  PvP  should  not  have  era  restrictions;  only  league  levels  should  gate  
matchmaking. This means a player who has completed Era 5 can compete in PvP against  
players at the same league level regardless of their campaign progress. The league system  
uses a standard ELO-based rating with promotion and demotion between tiers.
12.4 Hero and Scroll Selection
For the launch version, PvP uses a fixed rotating roster of heroes and scrolls that changes  
weekly. This design ensures balance without requiring continuous meta adjustments, and it  
keeps the PvP experience fresh by regularly changing the available strategic options. The  
rotating  roster  is  selected  by  the  development  team  based  on  balance  testing,  with  
community feedback incorporated through regular surveys. Future versions may introduce a  
draft-pick system where players alternately select heroes and scrolls from a shared pool.

2213. Survival Mode
Survival Mode is an endless wave defense challenge separate from the main campaign.  
Players select a map and defend against increasingly difficult waves that continue until all  
lives are lost. There is no win condition in Survival Mode; the goal is to survive as many  
waves as possible and achieve the highest score on the global leaderboard. Survival Mode is  
the primary competitive format for players who have completed the campaign and seek  
ongoing challenges.
Survival Mode uses the player's full tower roster, hero collection, and scroll inventory from  
the campaign. Unlike PvP, there are no loadout restrictions, allowing players to use their best  
available assets. However, the scaling difficulty ensures that even the most powerful loadout  
will eventually be overwhelmed, typically around wave 50-100 depending on player skill.  
The global leaderboard tracks the highest wave reached, total enemies defeated, and score,  
with seasonal resets that award SAKART tokens to top performers.
14. Seasons
Seasons are quarterly content updates that introduce new gameplay features, limited-time  
events, and exclusive cosmetic rewards. Each season lasts approximately three months and is  
themed around a specific aspect of Georgian culture or history that may not fit into the main  
chronological  campaign.  For  example,  a  season  might  focus  on  Georgian  mythology  
(featuring  folklore-based  enemies  and  towers),  Georgian  cuisine  (with  food-themed  
cosmetic skins), or specific historical events like the Battle of Didgori.
Seasons serve several design purposes. They provide ongoing content for players who have  
completed the campaign, creating reasons to return regularly. They introduce variety into the  
gameplay experience by temporarily modifying core rules (such as double damage for a  
specific tower type, or altered enemy compositions). They generate marketplace activity  
through limited-edition cosmetic skins that are only available during the season. And they  
provide additional SAKART burn mechanisms through season pass purchases and exclusive  
item acquisition.
Each season includes a free track with basic rewards available to all players, and a premium  
track purchased with SAKART that offers enhanced rewards including exclusive skins,  
scroll packs, and mastery scroll fragments. The season pass price is set to create a meaningful  
but not punitive SAKART sink, contributing to the target annual deflation rate.

2315. Endgame
The endgame begins when a player completes all 200 campaign levels and has access to the  
full tower roster, hero collection, and competitive modes. The endgame is designed to  
provide  long-term  engagement  through  three  pillars:  mastery  (perfecting  three-star  
completions and Survival Mode performance), collection (acquiring rare skins and mastery  
scrolls through the marketplace), and competition (climbing PvP and Survival leaderboards).
Mastery challenges include achieving three stars on all 200 levels, completing levels under  
self-imposed restrictions (no upgrades, single tower type only, hero-only runs), and setting  
high scores in Survival Mode. These challenges provide bragging rights and are tracked on  
the player's public profile. Collection goals revolve around acquiring the full set of cosmetic  
skins for each tower type and hero, including rare limited-edition skins from past seasons.  
Competition is maintained through seasonal leaderboard resets and regular PvP tournaments  
with SAKART prize pools.
The endgame economy is critical for long-term token health. With campaign star rewards  
largely earned by this point, the endgame introduces additional SAKART sinks through  
cosmetic  purchases,  season  passes,  and  tournament  entry  fees.  The  balance  between  
endgame sinks and remaining campaign earning ensures that the token economy remains  
healthy without requiring players to spend real money to continue enjoying the game.
16. Educational System
The educational system is one of the three foundational pillars of Sakartvelo Defenders. The  
game is designed to teach Georgian history organically through gameplay, with educational  
content woven into the fabric of the experience rather than presented as separate lessons. The  
educational system operates on three levels: passive learning through visual design and  
environmental storytelling, active learning through historical pop-ups between levels, and  
deep learning through an optional in-game encyclopedia.
Passive learning happens naturally as players progress through the campaign. The era-
specific art styles, tower designs, enemy types, and map environments all communicate  
historical information visually. A player who spends 20 levels defending against Mongol  
armies in Era 4 will develop an intuitive understanding of the Mongol period's impact on  
Georgia, even if they never read a single educational pop-up. The visual design language is  
the most powerful educational tool in the game.
Historical pop-ups appear between every level and provide 2-4 sentences of context about  
the events, figures, or locations featured in the upcoming level. These pop-ups are skippable  
for players who want to focus on gameplay, but they are designed to be engaging enough that

--- Page 29 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
24most players will read them. As confirmed during design review, historical pop-ups do NOT  
cost SAKART to read. Making education paywalled would undermine the game's core  
philosophy of cultural preservation and accessibility.
16.1 Cultural Footer
As confirmed during the design review, the game includes a cultural footer on the main menu  
and loading screens that displays rotating Georgian cultural facts, proverbs, and historical  
quotes. This passive educational element adds flavor without interrupting gameplay and  
reinforces the game's identity as a cultural product. The cultural footer content is curated to  
be interesting and diverse, covering topics ranging from Georgian wine-making traditions  
(the  world's  oldest)  to  the  unique  Georgian  alphabet,  a  UNESCO  Intangible  Cultural  
Heritage.
16.2 Historical Accuracy Policy
The game is committed to historical accuracy wherever possible. All historical events,  
figures,  and  cultural  elements  depicted  in  the  game  are  based  on  accepted  historical  
scholarship and Georgian historical sources. Where the historical record is ambiguous or  
contested, the game presents the most widely accepted interpretation and notes the existence  
of alternative views. The Stalin era (Era 8) is handled with particular care, presenting factual  
information about his actions and their consequences without glorification or erasure.
The historical accuracy policy extends to visual design. Tower skins, enemy designs, map  
environments, and hero appearances are all based on historical research and archaeological  
evidence. The Art Style Guide (see companion document) provides detailed reference  
material for each era's visual language, ensuring that the AI-generated art assets maintain  
consistency with historical reality. Anachronistic elements are avoided unless they serve a  
specific gameplay purpose and are clearly identified as non-historical.
17. Mythology Integration
Georgian  mythology  is  deeply  intertwined  with  the  country's  historical  identity,  and  
Sakartvelo  Defenders  incorporates  mythological  elements  as  complementary  to  the  
historical narrative rather than as a separate system. Mythological creatures and heroes  
appear primarily in Era 0 (Ancient Colchis, where the boundary between myth and history is  
naturally blurred) and in special seasonal events that explore specific aspects of Georgian  
folklore.
Key mythological elements include the devi (giant demons from Georgian folklore who  
serve as boss enemies), the kaji (magical blacksmiths who serve as tower upgrade mechanics

The mythology system is designed to enhance rather than replace the historical content.

18. SAKART Token Economy
SAKART is the ERC-20 utility token that powers the Sakartvelo Defenders economy. The  
token has a fixed hard cap of 1 billion (1,000,000,000) SAKART, with zero pre-mine and  
zero team allocation. All tokens enter circulation exclusively through player gameplay: stars  
earned on campaign levels are the sole distribution mechanism. This design ensures that the  
token economy is entirely player-driven, with no inflation from team unlocks, investor  
allocations, or algorithmic minting.
18.1 Token Distribution
Token distribution follows a star-based system. Each of the 200 campaign levels offers 3  
stars (600 total stars). Each star, when earned for the first time, grants a one-time SAKART  
reward. The reward per star scales with the era, with early eras granting smaller rewards and  
later eras granting larger ones. The total token supply allocated to campaign rewards is  
approximately 700 million SAKART (70% of the hard cap), with the remaining 300 million  
reserved for seasonal rewards, competitive prizes, and marketplace liquidity incentives.
Because each star can only be earned once per player, the total number of SAKART tokens in  
circulation is directly proportional to the number of active players and their campaign  
progress. A player who earns all 600 stars across the full campaign will have accumulated a  
substantial but finite amount of SAKART. There is no mechanism for earning SAKART  
through  replay,  grinding,  or  any  activity  other  than  first-time  star  completions.  This  
structural design makes replay farming mathematically impossible.

2618.2 Burn Mechanisms
The SAKART economy includes multiple burn mechanisms designed to create sustained  
deflationary pressure. The primary burn mechanisms are: skin purchases on the marketplace  
(marketplace fees are partially burned), skin bind/unbind fees, season pass purchases, and  
cosmetic effect unlocks. Additional burn mechanisms under consideration include scroll  
reroll fees (allowing players to reroll their scroll selection for a level at a SAKART cost),  
cosmetic tower effects (visual effects applied to towers that cost SAKART per activation),  
and leaderboard entry fees for competitive tournaments.
The  target  annual  deflation  rate  is  2-5%  of  circulating  supply,  achieved  through  the  
combination of all burn mechanisms. This deflation is not aggressive enough to create  
hoarding behavior (which would harm the gameplay economy) but sufficient to offset the  
slow trickle of new tokens entering circulation as new players join and earn stars. The fee  
structure is designed to be invisible to casual players while providing meaningful economic  
activity for engaged players.
18.3 Anti-Inflation Design
The SAKART economy is structurally anti-inflationary due to several design choices. First,  
the star-based earning system caps total possible earnings per player. Second, the absence of  
replay farming eliminates the most common Web3 game inflation vector. Third, the fixed  
hard cap of 1 billion tokens provides an absolute ceiling on supply. Fourth, the multiple burn  
mechanisms create continuous deflationary pressure. Fifth, the gameplay economy (in-game  
resources  for  tower  placement  and  upgrades)  is  entirely  separate  from  the  SAKART  
economy, preventing token inflation from affecting gameplay balance.
19. Fees and Revenue Model
The revenue model for Sakartvelo Defenders is built around a transparent transaction fee  
applied to all marketplace trades. The total fee is 1.8% per transaction, distributed as follows:  
1.3% to the development treasury for ongoing game development, server costs (minimal due  
to PWA architecture), and team compensation; 0.5% automatically compounded into the  
marketplace liquidity pool to ensure healthy trading conditions. No additional fees are  
charged for gameplay, account creation, or any other activity.
The 1.8% fee structure is designed to be low enough to encourage active marketplace trading  
while generating sufficient revenue to sustain development. As a solo developer with a  
$100/month budget, the minimal server costs (PWA has zero server hosting) mean that  
marketplace fees are almost entirely profit. The automatic LP compounding (0.5%) ensures

27that  the  marketplace  remains  liquid  even  during  low-activity  periods,  preventing  the  
common Web3 problem of illiquid markets that discourage trading.
19.1 Treasury and Developer Withdrawal
The  development  treasury  accumulates  the  1.3%  developer  fee  from  all  marketplace  
transactions. Withdrawals from the treasury are subject to a monthly cap to prevent sudden  
large withdrawals that could harm community trust. The monthly cap is set to cover  
development costs plus a reasonable developer salary, with any excess remaining in the  
treasury for future development needs. Treasury activity is fully transparent and auditable  
on-chain.
The withdrawal mechanism requires a timelock: withdrawal requests are queued for 48 hours  
before  execution,  during  which  they  are  publicly  visible  on  the  blockchain.  This  
transparency mechanism ensures that the community can verify that treasury funds are being  
used for legitimate development purposes. As a solo developer, this level of transparency is  
both  a  business  practice  and  a  competitive  advantage,  building  trust  with  the  player  
community that the game will be maintained and improved over the long term.
20. Smart Contracts
Sakartvelo Defenders deploys on the Immutable zkEVM Layer 2 network, which provides  
zero gas fee transactions for players while maintaining the security guarantees of Ethereum.  
The smart contract architecture consists of three primary contracts: the SAKART token  
contract  (ERC-20),  the  Skin  contract  (ERC-1155),  and  the  Mastery  Scroll  contract  
(ERC-721). All contracts follow established security best practices and will be audited  
before mainnet deployment.
20.1 SAKART Token Contract (ERC-20)
The SAKART token contract implements the standard ERC-20 interface with custom  
modifications for the game's requirements. The total supply is fixed at 1 billion tokens with  
no  minting  function.  Token  transfers  to  and  from  the  game's  designated  contracts  
(marketplace,  season  pass,  bind/unbind)  are  gasless  through  Immutable's  account  
abstraction. The contract includes a blacklist function for fraud prevention and multi-account  
detection, governed by a multisig wallet controlled by the developer.

2820.2 Skin Contract (ERC-1155)
The ERC-1155 standard is used for cosmetic skins because it supports both fungible and  
non-fungible tokens within a single contract. Common skins are semi-fungible (multiple  
identical copies exist), while rare and legendary skins are non-fungible (unique or limited  
edition). The contract includes metadata URI pointers to decentralized storage (IPFS) for  
skin artwork and description data, ensuring that skin assets remain accessible even if the  
game's servers go offline.
20.3 Mastery Scroll Contract (ERC-721)
Mastery scrolls use the ERC-721 standard because each scroll is a distinct non-fungible  
asset, even when multiple scrolls of the same type exist. The contract tracks scroll type, stack  
count, and activation status. When a player activates a mastery scroll for a level, the contract  
verifies ownership and stack count before applying the bonus. The contract also manages the  
bind/unbind mechanism, requiring a SAKART payment for unbinding that is automatically  
burned.
21. Blockchain Integration
The blockchain integration follows the 'invisible blockchain' design philosophy: players  
should never feel like they are interacting with a blockchain product unless they choose to  
engage with the marketplace or token features. All core gameplay, including the full 200-
level campaign, PvP, Survival Mode, and hero progression, functions identically whether or  
not the player has connected a wallet. Blockchain features enhance the experience for players  
who want true asset ownership and tradable items, but they are never required for gameplay.
21.1 Wallet Integration
Wallet  connection is handled through Immutable's Passport  wallet abstraction system.  
Passport provides a seamless onboarding experience that allows players to create a wallet  
using  social  login  (email,  Google,  Apple)  without  needing  to  understand  blockchain  
concepts. For players who already have Web3 wallets, Passport supports direct connection  
through  MetaMask,  WalletConnect,  and  other  popular  wallet  providers.  The  wallet  
connection is optional and can be deferred indefinitely without affecting gameplay access.
21.2 Chain Outage Fallback
As confirmed during design review with Hermes, the game implements a chain outage  
fallback  system.  If  the  Immutable  zkEVM  network  experiences  downtime,  gameplay

--- Page 34 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
29continues  uninterrupted.  All  gameplay  state  (level  progress,  star  completions,  hero  
experience) is tracked locally and synced to the blockchain when connectivity resumes.  
Blockchain-specific transactions (marketplace trades, token transfers, NFT operations) are  
queued locally and submitted automatically in order when the chain comes back online. This  
ensures that players never lose progress due to infrastructure issues.
21.3 Invisible Blockchain Philosophy
The invisible blockchain philosophy extends to every aspect of the game's design. There is  
no blockchain tutorial, no crypto jargon in the UI, and no requirement to understand gas,  
wallets, or smart contracts to enjoy the full gameplay experience. The UI uses standard  
gaming terminology ('Shop' instead of 'Marketplace,' 'Items' instead of 'NFTs,' 'Coins'  
instead of 'Tokens') for all core gameplay interactions. Blockchain-specific terminology is  
only used in the dedicated marketplace section, which players only see if they choose to  
explore trading features.
22. Multi-Account Prevention
Multi-account prevention is critical for the SAKART economy because each account can  
earn SAKART through star completions. Without prevention, a single person could create  
multiple accounts, complete the early (easier) levels on each account to farm SAKART, and  
then consolidate the tokens through marketplace trades. This would undermine the structural  
anti-inflation design and devalue the token for legitimate players.
The prevention system uses multiple layers. At the wallet level, Passport's identity system  
ties each account to a verified identity (email, phone, or social login), making it difficult to  
create large numbers of accounts. At the gameplay level, progression requirements (15/20  
stars to unlock the next era) ensure that farming accounts must invest significant time before  
earning  meaningful  SAKART.  At  the  transaction  level,  the  marketplace  monitors  for  
patterns consistent with multi-account farming (multiple accounts sending tokens to a single  
address within a short timeframe) and flags suspicious activity for investigation.
The  blacklist  function in  the  SAKART  token contract  allows  flagged accounts  to  be  
suspended from token transactions while preserving their gameplay progress. This ensures  
that legitimate players who happen to share IP addresses (family members, university  
networks)  are  not  unfairly  penalized.  The  threshold  for  blacklisting  is  intentionally  
conservative, requiring multiple indicators of farming behavior before action is taken.

Web3 tower defense games are a nascent category with few established titles. Most Web3

Defenders takes the opposite approach: the gameplay is designed to be competitive with  
Web2 titles, and blockchain is an invisible infrastructure layer that enhances the experience

and Web3 audiences is the game's most significant market opportunity.
24. Art Pipeline
The art pipeline is designed around the constraints of solo development with AI-generated  
art. All visual assets are created using AI image generation tools, guided by a comprehensive  
Art Style Guide (see companion document) that ensures consistency across all 10 eras, 10  
tower types, 200 maps, and hundreds of enemy and hero designs. The Art Style Guide  
provides era-specific color palettes, architectural reference keywords, character proportion  
standards, and prompt templates that enable reproducible, consistent output from AI tools.
24.1 Style Bible System
The Art Style Guide v2.0 (to be updated with the new 10-era structure) provides the  
authoritative reference for all visual assets. The guide defines a 2D isometric art style with  
cel-shaded lighting, a consistent character proportion system (10-unit body scale), era-
specific color palettes (8 colors per era), and tower silhouette guidelines that ensure each  
tower type is visually distinct at small sizes. Every AI generation prompt includes era-
appropriate keywords drawn from the style guide, ensuring that assets from different eras are  
visually cohesive within their period while being clearly distinct across periods.

3224.2 AI Generation Workflow
The workflow begins with a prompt template that incorporates the style guide parameters for  
the target era and asset type. The prompt includes era-specific color palette references,  
architectural keywords (e.g., 'Svan medieval stone tower' for Era 4, 'Soviet brutalist concrete'  
for Era 8), and composition guidelines (isometric perspective, cel-shaded lighting, no text  
overlays). Generated images are reviewed against the style guide consistency checklist,  
which verifies color accuracy, proportion correctness, silhouette clarity, and era-appropriate  
architectural details.
Assets that pass the consistency check are composited into the game at the appropriate  
resolution.  The  isometric  perspective  and  2D  art  style  make  asset  compositing  
straightforward, as all elements share the same perspective and lighting model. The style  
guide's standardization enables efficient batch generation: a single prompt template with era-
specific parameter substitutions can produce an entire era's worth of tower skins, map  
backgrounds, and character art in a single generation session.

25.1 Godot 4.x and PWA Architecture
Godot 4.x was selected as the game engine for several reasons: it is free and open-source, it  
has excellent 2D rendering capabilities, it supports PWA export natively, and it has a small  
but active community. The PWA deployment model means the game runs entirely in the  
browser with no server-side game logic. All gameplay computation happens on the player's  
device, eliminating the need for game servers. The only server interactions are blockchain  
transactions  (handled  by  Immutable's  infrastructure)  and  website  hosting  (handled  by  
Vercel's free tier).
The PWA architecture also provides offline gameplay capabilities. The full campaign, all  
tower and hero data, and all map assets are cached locally through the PWA service worker.  
Players can continue playing the campaign even without internet access, with blockchain  
transactions queued for later submission. This offline capability is a significant competitive  
advantage over cloud-dependent games and aligns with the design philosophy of removing  
all friction from the player experience.
25.2 Blockchain Infrastructure
Immutable zkEVM provides the blockchain infrastructure with two critical advantages: zero  
gas fees for players and EVM compatibility. Zero gas is essential for the invisible blockchain  
philosophy, as any visible transaction cost would immediately break the illusion of a  
traditional game. EVM compatibility ensures that the smart contracts can use established  
standards (ERC-20, ERC-721, ERC-1155) and benefit from the extensive Ethereum tooling  
ecosystem.
Passport, Immutable's wallet abstraction layer, handles wallet creation and management.  
Players who have never used blockchain before can create a wallet through a simple email  
signup, while experienced Web3 users can connect their existing wallets. The Passport

26. Design Principles
The following principles are the non-negotiable rules that govern all design decisions in  
Sakartvelo  Defenders.  When  any  design  question  arises,  these  principles  provide  the  
definitive answer. They are not suggestions or guidelines; they are hard constraints that  
cannot be overridden without a deliberate design review.
26.1 No-Nerf Rule
Once a tower, hero, scroll, or ability is released, its core functionality will never be nerfed  
(reduced in power). If something is too powerful, the response is to buff everything else to  
match, not to weaken the overperforming element. This principle protects player investment  
and builds trust. A player who spends time and resources mastering a specific strategy should  
never wake up to find that strategy has been intentionally weakened. The no-nerf rule is the  
most important design principle in the game.
26.2 Invisible Blockchain
No player should ever feel like they are interacting with a blockchain product unless they  
choose to engage with marketplace or token features. All UI language uses standard gaming  
terminology. No blockchain tutorial is required. No gas fees are visible to players. Wallet  
creation uses familiar social login methods. The blockchain exists as infrastructure, not as a  
feature that players must understand or engage with.
26.3 Historical Accuracy
The game is chronologically accurate and committed to truthful representation of Georgian  
history. Eras follow the correct historical sequence. Towers, enemies, and heroes are based  
on real historical references. The Stalin era is presented factually, with neither glorification  
nor  erasure.  When  the  historical  record  is  ambiguous,  the  game  acknowledges  the  
uncertainty rather than presenting speculation as fact.
26.4 Solo Developer Feasibility
Every feature must be implementable by a solo developer on a $100/month budget. This  
constraint drives technology choices (PWA over dedicated servers, AI-generated art over  
hand-drawn assets, Immutable zkEVM over custom blockchain), content scope (10 eras with

--- Page 40 ---

Sakartvelo Defenders | Game Design Bible v3.0
3520 levels each rather than open-ended content), and feature prioritization. Features that  
cannot be implemented within these constraints are deferred to post-launch community  
contributions or removed from the design entirely.
26.5 Accessibility
The game must be accessible to players with no prior experience with tower defense games,  
blockchain technology, or Georgian history. The tutorial system teaches core mechanics  
organically. Difficulty is calibrated to allow casual players to progress while providing  
meaningful challenges for dedicated players. Educational content is available but never  
forced. The blockchain is invisible to players who do not seek it out. Cultural references are  
explained in context rather than assumed as prior knowledge.

generated assets that do not meet the style guide standards are regenerated.

focus  on  tower  defense  communities,  Web3  gaming  forums,  the  Georgian  diaspora

operational.  Post-launch  development  continues  with  seasonal  content  updates  and

This section documents all changes made to the Game Design Bible between version 2.0 and  
version 3.0. The changes are driven by comprehensive Georgian history research that  
revealed significant historical inaccuracies in the previous era structure, user feedback on  
specific game systems, and design review feedback from Hermes. The v3.0 update is the  
most substantial revision to the Bible since the initial creation.
