# Era 9: Modern Georgia (~1991-Present)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The restoration of Georgian independence in April 1991 marked the beginning of a new and extraordinarily challenging era. After seventy years of Soviet rule, Georgia emerged as an independent state facing a daunting array of problems: economic collapse, civil war, secessionist conflicts, political instability, and the immense task of building democratic institutions and a market economy virtually from scratch. The first three decades of independence were marked by extraordinary hardship, remarkable resilience, and — gradually — the emergence of a more stable, prosperous, and democratic Georgia, though significant challenges remain.

The early years of independence (1991-1993) were catastrophic. The presidency of Zviad Gamsakhurdia, Georgia's first popularly elected leader, was characterized by authoritarian tendencies, political polarization, and a deteriorating security situation. Gamsakhurdia was overthrown in a violent coup in January 1992, triggering a civil war between his supporters and the new government led by former Soviet foreign minister Eduard Shevardnadze. Simultaneously, secessionist movements in Abkhazia and South Ossetia — supported by Russia — erupted into armed conflicts that the Georgian government was unable to control. By 1993, Georgia had lost control of Abkhazia and large parts of South Ossetia, and the country's economy was in ruins.

The Shevardnadze era (1992-2003) brought a measure of stability but was marked by corruption, economic stagnation, and the failure to resolve the secessionist conflicts. Shevardnadze's government survived the challenges of the 1990s but lost public support due to widespread perceptions of corruption and ineffectiveness. The Rose Revolution of November 2003 — a peaceful mass protest movement led by Mikheil Saakashvili — toppled Shevardnadze's government and ushered in a period of ambitious reform.

The Saakashvili era (2004-2013) was characterized by dramatic reforms in governance, law enforcement, and economic policy, as well as a strong Western orientation. Georgia pursued NATO and EU membership, implemented anti-corruption measures, and experienced rapid economic growth. However, Saakashvili's increasingly authoritarian tendencies, the brief but devastating war with Russia in August 2008, and persistent economic inequality ultimately undermined his government's popularity. The 2008 war resulted in Russian recognition of Abkhazia and South Ossetia as independent states and the further entrenchment of Russian military forces on Georgian territory.

The post-Saakashvili period (2013-present) has seen a more cautious but generally positive trajectory. The Georgian Dream party, led by Bidzina Ivanishvili, came to power in 2012 and has governed since, pursuing a more pragmatic relationship with Russia while maintaining Georgia's Western orientation. Georgia has achieved significant progress in economic development, tourism, and institutional reform, and it is currently a candidate for EU membership — a remarkable achievement for a country that, thirty years ago, was in the grip of civil war and economic collapse. Yet challenges remain: the unresolved conflicts with Abkhazia and South Ossetia, Russian occupation of Georgian territory, political polarization, poverty, and the ongoing struggle to build genuinely democratic institutions.

## KEY FIGURES

### Zviad Gamsakhurdia (1939-1993)

Zviad Gamsakhurdia was the first president of independent Georgia, elected in May 1991. A dissident writer and human rights activist during the Soviet period, Gamsakhurdia was a charismatic but increasingly authoritarian leader whose presidency was marked by political confrontation with opposition groups, ethnic conflicts, and a deteriorating security situation. His embrace of exclusive Georgian nationalism alienated ethnic minorities and contributed to the escalation of conflicts in South Ossetia and Abkhazia.

Gamsakhurdia was overthrown in a violent coup in January 1992 and driven into exile. He returned to western Georgia in 1993 to lead an armed insurgency against Shevardnadze's government but was defeated and died in circumstances that remain disputed (officially by suicide, but his supporters claim he was murdered). Gamsakhurdia's legacy is deeply contested: he is revered by some as the founder of Georgian independence and a martyr to the cause, while others view him as a dangerous nationalist whose authoritarian tendencies helped bring Georgia to the brink of collapse.

### Eduard Shevardnadze (1928-2014)

Eduard Shevardnadze served as the de facto leader of Georgia from 1992 to 2003, first as chairman of the State Council and later as elected president. His appointment as leader of Georgia was initially welcomed as a stabilizing force after the chaos of the Gamsakhurdia period. Shevardnadze's international reputation — built on his role as Soviet foreign minister — helped Georgia gain recognition and assistance from the international community. He negotiated ceasefires in the Abkhaz and South Ossetian conflicts and brought Georgia into the Council of Europe and the World Trade Organization.

However, Shevardnadze's government became increasingly associated with corruption, economic stagnation, and political stagnation. His inability to address Georgia's fundamental problems — corruption, poverty, the secessionist conflicts, and the erosion of democratic institutions — led to growing public disillusionment. The Rose Revolution of 2003, in which peaceful protesters forced him to resign, was a response to the accumulated frustrations of the Shevardnadze era. Despite his failures, Shevardnadze's role in ending the civil wars of the early 1990s and preventing further territorial losses was significant.

### Mikheil Saakashvili (born 1967)

Mikheil Saakashvili was the driving force behind the Rose Revolution of 2003 and served as president of Georgia from 2004 to 2013. Educated in the United States (Columbia Law School), Saakashvili came to power promising to fight corruption, modernize the state, and integrate Georgia into Euro-Atlantic institutions. His government implemented sweeping reforms: a radical anti-corruption campaign that dramatically reduced petty bribery, police reform that replaced a notoriously corrupt force with a professional service, economic liberalization that attracted foreign investment, and a public works program that modernized infrastructure.

Saakashvili's achievements were substantial. Georgia rose in international rankings for governance and ease of doing business, and the country experienced rapid economic growth in the mid-2000s. However, his government was also criticized for increasing authoritarianism — the suppression of opposition media, the use of force against protesters in 2007, and the concentration of power in the presidency. The 2008 war with Russia — which Georgia lost decisively — was a major setback, and Saakashvili's party lost the 2012 elections, ending his presidency. He subsequently left Georgia and was convicted in absentia on abuse-of-power charges, a verdict that many international observers viewed as politically motivated.

### Bidzina Ivanishvili (born 1956)

Bidzina Ivanishvili is a Georgian billionaire businessman and politician who founded the Georgian Dream coalition and served as prime minister from 2012 to 2013. Ivanishvili made his fortune in Russia during the 1990s and returned to Georgia in the early 2000s, becoming the country's wealthiest person. His entry into politics in 2011-2012 transformed the Georgian political landscape, as his vast personal wealth and political organization enabled the Georgian Dream coalition to defeat Saakashvili's party in the 2012 parliamentary elections.

Ivanishvili's government pursued a more pragmatic and less confrontational approach than Saakashvili's, seeking to improve relations with Russia while maintaining Georgia's Western orientation. His tenure saw the continuation of economic reforms and the development of Georgia's tourism industry, which became a major engine of economic growth. However, his government has also been criticized for politicizing the judiciary, tolerating oligarchic influence, and being insufficiently committed to democratic reforms. Ivanishvili remains the most powerful figure in Georgian politics despite his formal retirement from active government roles.

### Salome Zourabichvili (born 1952)

Salome Zourabichvili became the first woman to serve as president of Georgia in 2018. Born in Paris to Georgian refugee parents, Zourabichvili had a career in French diplomacy before entering Georgian politics. Her presidency has been marked by efforts to balance Georgia's EU aspirations with the complex realities of regional geopolitics. She has been a strong advocate for Georgia's EU candidacy, which was granted in December 2023 — a historic milestone for the country. Her presidency represents the continuing evolution of Georgian democracy and the growing role of women in Georgian public life.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Rose Revolution (November 2003)

The Rose Revolution was a series of peaceful mass protests in November 2003 that forced President Eduard Shevardnadze to resign and brought Mikheil Saakashvili to power. The protests were triggered by parliamentary elections widely perceived as rigged, in which the government manipulated results to maintain its hold on power. Thousands of demonstrators, carrying roses (the symbol of the revolution), gathered outside the parliament building in Tbilisi and refused to leave until Shevardnadze stepped down.

The Rose Revolution was one of the first of the "color revolutions" that swept through the post-Soviet world in the early 2000s, and it was widely celebrated in the West as a triumph of peaceful democratic change. The revolution was remarkable for its nonviolence — there were no deaths and relatively few injuries — and for the speed with which it achieved its objectives. The revolution inspired similar movements in Ukraine (the Orange Revolution) and Kyrgyzstan (the Tulip Revolution). However, the long-term legacy of the Rose Revolution is more complex, as the democratic gains of 2003 were partially reversed by Saakashvili's increasingly authoritarian governance.

### The August 2008 War

The August 2008 War was a brief but devastating conflict between Georgia and Russia over the secessionist territories of South Ossetia and Abkhazia. The immediate trigger was a Georgian military operation to retake the South Ossetian capital of Tskhinvali on August 7-8, 2008, which prompted a massive Russian military response. Russian forces defeated the Georgian army in five days of fighting, advancing deep into Georgian territory before a French-brokered ceasefire ended the active hostilities.

The war's consequences were severe and lasting. Russia recognized Abkhazia and South Ossetia as independent states (a recognition that only a handful of other countries have followed), and established permanent military bases in both territories. Georgia lost effective control of approximately 20 percent of its internationally recognized territory. The war also caused hundreds of military and civilian casualties, displaced approximately 192,000 people, and caused significant economic damage. The war fundamentally altered the geopolitical dynamics of the South Caucasus and demonstrated the limits of Western support for Georgia in the face of Russian military power.

### EU Candidate Status (December 2023)

On December 14, 2023, the European Union granted Georgia candidate status for EU membership — a historic milestone in the country's post-Soviet trajectory. The decision came after years of reform efforts and was seen as a validation of Georgia's Euro-Atlantic orientation. Georgia had signed an Association Agreement with the EU in 2014 and a Deep and Comprehensive Free Trade Area agreement, but the granting of candidate status represented a significant step toward full membership.

The EU candidacy decision was also accompanied by conditions requiring further reforms, particularly in the areas of judicial independence, media freedom, and democratic governance. The road to actual EU membership remains long, and Georgia faces significant challenges in meeting the required standards. Nevertheless, the candidacy represents a remarkable achievement for a country that, just thirty years earlier, was emerging from the ruins of the Soviet Union. For many Georgians, EU membership represents the ultimate expression of their country's European identity and the fulfillment of a centuries-long aspiration for integration with the Western world.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### Post-Soviet Cultural Revival

The independence period has seen a remarkable revival of Georgian culture after the constraints of the Soviet era. Georgian literature has flourished, with writers like Aka Morchiladze, Dato Turashvili, and Lasha Bugadze producing works that explore Georgia's post-Soviet identity and the complexities of national memory. Georgian cinema has experienced a renaissance, with films like "Tangerines" (directed by Zaza Urushadze, nominated for an Academy Award in 2014) and "And Then We Danced" (directed by Levan Akin) gaining international acclaim. The restoration of historic buildings and neighborhoods in Tbilisi and other cities has revitalized Georgia's architectural heritage.

### The Georgian Wine Renaissance

Georgian wine has undergone a remarkable international renaissance in the 21st century. The unique qvevri winemaking tradition — in which wine is fermented and aged in large clay vessels buried underground — has been recognized by UNESCO as part of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity (2013). Georgian wines, particularly those made from indigenous grape varieties like Saperavi, Rkatsiteli, and Kisi, have gained enthusiastic followings in international markets. The development of wine tourism has become a major economic driver, with visitors from around the world coming to Georgia to experience its ancient wine culture.

### Digital Innovation and Technology

Georgia has emerged as a significant player in the Caucasus digital economy. The country has invested in digital infrastructure, e-government services, and a growing technology startup ecosystem. Tbilisi has become a regional hub for IT professionals and entrepreneurs, attracted by the city's quality of life, relatively low costs, and favorable visa policies. While Georgia's technology sector is still small by global standards, its growth represents an important diversification of the Georgian economy beyond traditional sectors like agriculture and tourism.

### Tourism and Cultural Heritage

Tourism has become one of Georgia's most important economic sectors, with the country attracting millions of visitors annually. Georgia's appeal lies in its extraordinary cultural heritage (ancient churches and monasteries, medieval fortresses, historic cities), its natural beauty (the Caucasus Mountains, the Black Sea coast, diverse landscapes), its renowned cuisine and wine culture, and the legendary hospitality of its people. The development of tourism has created economic opportunities throughout the country and has helped drive the restoration and preservation of Georgia's historic monuments and traditions.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### The Bridge of Peace, Tbilisi

The Bridge of Peace, a striking pedestrian bridge over the Mtkvari River in central Tbilisi, was completed in 2010 and has become one of the most recognizable symbols of modern Georgia. Designed by Italian architect Michele De Lucchi, the bridge is a steel and glass structure with a flowing, organic form that contrasts dramatically with the historic buildings on both banks of the river. The bridge connects the old city with the new district of Rike Park and is illuminated at night by thousands of LED lights. It represents the aspirations of modern Georgia — dynamic, forward-looking, and connected to both its heritage and the wider world.

### The Rike Park Concert Hall and Theater

Located adjacent to the Bridge of Peace, the Rike Park Theater (also known as the Rike Concert Hall) was completed in 2015 and is one of the most architecturally ambitious buildings in the Caucasus. Designed by Italian architect Massimiliano Fuksas, the building features a striking form of interlocking steel tubes that house a concert hall and a musical theater. The building has been controversial — some critics find its design incongruous with the surrounding historic fabric — but it represents the ambition and confidence of modern Georgian architecture.

### The Restoration of Batumi

The Black Sea city of Batumi has undergone one of the most dramatic urban transformations in the post-Soviet world. From a somewhat rundown Soviet-era port city, Batumi has been redeveloped into a vibrant, modern resort destination with striking contemporary architecture, a renovated historic center, a beautiful seaside boulevard, and a growing reputation as the "Las Vegas of the Black Sea." Landmark buildings like the Alphabet Tower (a 130-meter structure featuring the 33 letters of the Georgian alphabet), the Radisson Blu Hotel (shaped like a sail), and the futuristic Piazza Square represent the new Batumi. The city's transformation reflects Georgia's broader post-Soviet economic development and its embrace of international tourism and investment.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### The Georgian Armed Forces

Since independence, Georgia has built a small but capable military force, the Georgian Armed Forces (GAF), consisting of the Land Forces, Air Force, and National Guard. The GAF has been significantly modernized with Western assistance, particularly from the United States and NATO member states. Georgian soldiers have served alongside American and NATO forces in Iraq and Afghanistan, gaining valuable combat experience. The GAF is equipped with a mixture of Soviet-era and Western weapons systems, including small arms, armored vehicles, artillery, and limited air defense capabilities.

### NATO Partnership and Military Training

Georgia has been one of the most active partners of NATO among non-member states. The NATO-Georgia Individual Partnership Action Plan (IPAP) and the Substantial NATO-Georgia Package (SNGP) have provided significant military assistance, including training, equipment, and advisory support. The joint NATO-Georgia Training Center in Krtsanisi (near Tbilisi) has trained Georgian soldiers using NATO standards and procedures. Georgia's contribution of troops to the NATO-led missions in Iraq (2003-2008) and Afghanistan (2009-2014) was one of the largest per-capita contributions of any non-NATO country and earned Georgia considerable goodwill in the alliance.

### Hybrid Warfare and Cybersecurity

The 2008 war with Russia demonstrated the challenges of modern hybrid warfare, in which conventional military operations are combined with cyber attacks, information warfare, and political subversion. Georgia has since invested in cybersecurity capabilities, working with Western partners to develop defenses against cyber attacks and information operations. The Georgian experience of hybrid warfare — particularly the large-scale Russian cyber attacks that accompanied the 2008 military operations — has made Georgia a recognized expert in this field, and Georgian cybersecurity specialists have contributed to international understanding of hybrid threats.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Fashion

Modern Georgian fashion reflects the country's position at the crossroads of Europe and Asia. Urban Georgians dress in contemporary Western styles, with Tbilisi developing a reputation as a fashion-forward city. The Tbilisi Fashion Week, established in 2009, has become an important event in the regional fashion calendar, and Georgian designers like Demna Gvasalia (creative director of Balenciaga and co-founder of Vetements) have achieved international prominence. Traditional Georgian dress — the chokha for men, elaborate national costumes for women — is worn at weddings, national holidays, and cultural events, and has experienced a revival as a symbol of national identity.

### Food and the Modern Georgian Table

Georgian cuisine has become one of the country's most important cultural exports. The traditional Georgian supra (feast) remains central to social life, and the tamada (toastmaster) tradition continues to be taken seriously. Georgia's diverse regional cuisines — the spicy dishes of Samegrelo, the herb-filled specialties of Imereti, the meat-heavy fare of Kakheti, the mountain cuisine of Svaneti — are celebrated both domestically and internationally. The qvevri wine tradition has been rediscovered by international wine enthusiasts, and natural wine producers from around the world travel to Georgia to study the ancient technique. Georgia's restaurant scene in Tbilisi and other cities is vibrant and innovative, blending traditional recipes with contemporary culinary trends.

### Social Structure and Economy

Modern Georgian society is in transition from the post-Soviet economic collapse to a more developed market economy. The middle class has grown, particularly in Tbilisi, but significant poverty remains, especially in rural areas and among internally displaced persons (IDPs) from the Abkhaz and South Ossetian conflicts. The economy is driven by services (particularly tourism), agriculture (wine, nuts, fruit, and vegetables), and a growing technology sector. Remittances from Georgians working abroad (particularly in Russia and Europe) remain an important source of income for many families. The social safety net is limited, and many Georgians rely on extended family networks for support.

### Religion and Society

The Georgian Orthodox Church has experienced a dramatic revival since the end of the Soviet period. Church attendance has increased dramatically, new churches have been built throughout the country, and the Church exercises significant social and political influence. The Church's relationship with the Georgian state is complex: the constitution recognizes the Church's "special role" in Georgian history, and the Church receives state funding, but Georgia is officially a secular state. The Church's conservative positions on social issues — particularly LGBTQ+ rights — have been a source of tension with more liberal segments of Georgian society and with Georgia's Western partners. The Church remains the most trusted institution in Georgian society, and its influence on public policy is substantial.

## GLOSSARY

**Vardebis Revolutsia** — "Rose Revolution." The peaceful 2003 protest movement that toppled the Shevardnadze government and brought Mikheil Saakashvili to power.

**Qvevri** — Large clay vessels used for fermenting and storing wine, buried underground. The Georgian qvevri winemaking tradition is recognized by UNESCO.

**Saqartvelos Shekhedva** — "Georgia's Defense." The Georgian Armed Forces, established after independence and modernized with Western assistance.

**Abkhazeti da Samachablo** — The Georgian names for Abkhazia and South Ossetia (Samachablo being the Georgian name for the Tskhinvali region). Both are breakaway territories under Russian military occupation.

**Damoukideblobis Gantskhadeba** — "Integration into Europe." Georgia's policy of seeking membership in the European Union and NATO.

**Ishvebi** — "Internally displaced persons." The hundreds of thousands of Georgians displaced by the conflicts in Abkhazia and South Ossetia.

**Didi Lilebi** — "Great Bridge." A term sometimes used for the Bridge of Peace and other modern infrastructure projects.

**Supra** — A traditional Georgian feast, central to Georgian social life. The tradition has been both preserved and commercialized in the modern era.

**Chokha** — The traditional Georgian men's coat, which has experienced a revival as a symbol of national identity in the modern period.

**Mshvenieria** — "Representation" or "democracy." The ongoing project of building genuinely democratic institutions in post-Soviet Georgia.
