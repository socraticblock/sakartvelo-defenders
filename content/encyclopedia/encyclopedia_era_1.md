# Era 1: Kingdom of Iberia (~300 BC - ~630 AD)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The Kingdom of Iberia (known as Kartli in Georgian sources) represents the crucible in which Georgian national identity was truly forged. Emerging from the fragmentation of earlier Colchian and tribal societies, Iberia occupied the eastern Georgian lands — the fertile valleys of the Mtkvari (Kura) River and its tributaries, bounded by the Caucasus Mountains to the north and the Lesser Caucasus to the south. While Colchis in the west had been shaped by Greek contact, Iberia found itself at the crossroads of Iranian, Roman, and indigenous Caucasian worlds, absorbing influences from all three while maintaining a distinct cultural core that would ultimately define what it meant to be Georgian.

The period begins with the emergence of Iberia as a recognizable polity in the 4th century BC, possibly under the influence of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, which had controlled much of the South Caucasus until the conquests of Alexander the Great. The early kings of Iberia — figures like Azo and Pharnavaz who hover between history and legend — unified the eastern Georgian tribes into a single kingdom centered on the fortified capital of Mtskheta, where the Aragvi and Mtkvari rivers converge. This unification was a transformative event: for the first time, the peoples of eastern Georgia shared a common political framework, and the idea of a united Kartvelian realm was born.

The first centuries AD brought one of the most consequential events in all of Georgian history: the adoption of Christianity. According to tradition, Saint Nino, a woman from Cappadocia, converted King Mirian III of Iberia to Christianity around 327 AD, making Georgia one of the first nations in the world to embrace Christianity as a state religion. This decision had far-reaching consequences. It oriented Georgia culturally toward the Roman/Byzantine world rather than the Iranian Sasanian Empire that dominated the region, established a powerful institutional church that would become a pillar of Georgian identity, and inspired the creation of a unique Georgian script, one of only about twenty alphabets ever independently invented in human history.

For most of this era, Iberia existed in a precarious position between two great rival empires: Rome (and later Byzantium) to the west and the Iranian Sasanian Empire to the east. Both sought to control the strategic passes of the Caucasus, which guarded the frontier between the steppe and the civilized world. Iberian kings were forced to navigate this impossible diplomatic landscape, sometimes allying with Rome, sometimes with Persia, and sometimes attempting to maintain a precarious independence. The Iberian nobility itself was divided into pro-Roman and pro-Persian factions, creating internal tensions that weakened the kingdom.

By the end of this era, around 630 AD, Iberia as an independent kingdom had effectively ceased to exist, replaced by competing principalities and direct foreign control. But the legacy of Iberia was enormous. The Georgian Orthodox Church, the Georgian alphabet, the concept of a united Georgian realm, and the core of Georgian literary and artistic tradition all had their roots in this period. The challenges of survival between empires would set a pattern that would recur throughout Georgian history, and the resilience developed in this era would serve Georgia well in the centuries to come.

## KEY FIGURES

### King Pharnavaz I (c. 302-237 BC)

Pharnavaz I is traditionally regarded as the founder of the Kingdom of Iberia and the first king of a unified Kartvelian state. According to the medieval Georgian chronicles, Pharnavaz was a young nobleman from Mtskheta who, after the death of his father, was raised in obscurity but eventually rose to overthrow the Macedonian garrison that controlled his homeland. With the help of the Kuji (a tribal leader from western Georgia), Pharnavaz defeated his rivals, unified the eastern Georgian tribes, and established his capital at Mtskheta, which would remain the political and religious center of Georgia for centuries.

Pharnavaz is credited with a number of foundational reforms: the creation of a formal administrative structure (dividing the kingdom into eight administrative districts called saeristavo), the establishment of a standing army, and the promotion of a new script for the Georgian language. The historical Pharnavaz is difficult to separate from the legendary accretions that surrounded his memory over the centuries, but the core of the tradition — the unification of Iberia under a single ruler and the establishment of institutional structures — is consistent with the archaeological and historical evidence for state formation in eastern Georgia during the early Hellenistic period. His reign represents the birth of Georgia as a political entity.

### King Mirian III (c. 284-361 AD)

King Mirian III of Iberia is best known as the king who converted to Christianity, making Georgia one of the world's earliest Christian nations. According to tradition, Mirian was originally a worshipper of the sun and moon, but after the miraculous healing of his illness by Saint Nino — a Christian woman from Cappadocia who had come to Georgia as a missionary — he declared Christianity the state religion of Iberia around 327 AD. The conversion story, preserved in Georgian hagiographical texts, describes Mirian being struck blind while hunting in the forest and regaining his sight only after praying to the Christian God.

Mirian's conversion was not merely a personal religious choice but a geopolitical decision of immense significance. By embracing Christianity, Iberia aligned itself with the Roman Empire rather than the Sasanian Persian Empire, which was Zoroastrian. This orientation toward Rome/Byzantium would shape Georgian culture for the rest of its history. Mirian's reign also saw the construction of some of Georgia's earliest churches, including the Svetitskhoveli Cathedral at Mtskheta, which would become the most sacred site in Georgian Christianity. His wife, Queen Nana, is also celebrated as an early convert and supporter of the new faith.

### Saint Nino (c. 280-335 AD)

Saint Nino, known in Georgia as "Nino the Enlightener" (C'minda Nino), is one of the most important figures in Georgian religious and cultural history. According to tradition, she was born in Cappadocia (in modern Turkey), was the daughter of a Roman general, and was related to Saint George. She came to Georgia around 320 AD, reportedly carrying a cross made of grapevines bound with her own hair. She lived as a holy woman in Mtskheta, performing miracles and gaining converts, until she eventually won over King Mirian III and the royal court.

Nino's impact on Georgian history cannot be overstated. Her missionary work led directly to the Christianization of Iberia, which in turn triggered the development of a unique Georgian literary and artistic tradition within the Christian framework. The Georgian Orthodox Church venerates her as equal to the apostles, and her grapevine cross remains one of the most important symbols of Georgian Christianity. The church she is said to have founded at Mtskheta, the Samtavro Monastery, is still an active place of pilgrimage. Nino's story also highlights the important role of women in early Georgian Christianity — a pattern that would continue throughout Georgian history.

### King Vakhtang Gorgasali (c. 439-502 AD)

Vakhtang Gorgasali — "Vakhtang the Wolf Head" — is one of the most celebrated warrior-kings in Georgian history and a central figure of the Georgian national pantheon. His byname "Gorgasali" comes from the Persian "gurgaslan," meaning "wolf-headed" or "having the head of a wolf," a reference to his ferocity in battle. Vakhtang ruled Iberia during one of its most difficult periods, when the kingdom was caught between the warring Byzantine and Sasanian Persian empires.

Vakhtang is best known for two major accomplishments: his military campaigns against the Sasanian Persians and his founding (or refounding) of the city of Tbilisi. According to tradition, the king discovered the warm sulfur springs of Tbilisi while hunting and was so impressed that he ordered a city built there. The name "Tbilisi" comes from the Georgian word "tbili" (warm), referring to the thermal springs. Vakhtang also fought numerous campaigns against the Persians, the mountain tribes of the North Caucasus, and other enemies. He sought to strengthen Iberia's ties with Byzantium while maintaining independence from both great powers. Although he was ultimately killed in battle against the Persians, Vakhtang's legacy as a warrior-king and national hero endured for centuries, and he was canonized as a saint by the Georgian Orthodox Church.

### Peter the Iberian (c. 417-491 AD)

Peter the Iberian (Petre Iberieli) was a Georgian prince, monk, and theologian who played a significant role in the religious controversies of the 5th century. Born as Nabarnugios, son of King Bosmarios of Iberia, he was sent as a hostage to the Byzantine court in Constantinople as a child, where he received a Greek education. Rather than pursue a political career, Peter renounced his royal inheritance and became a monk, eventually settling near Gaza in Palestine.

Peter became a leading figure in the anti-Chalcedonian (Miaphysite) movement, which opposed the Christological definitions of the Council of Chalcedon (451 AD). His theological writings and monastic foundations were influential throughout the Near East, and he was venerated as a saint in the Syriac, Coptic, and Armenian churches as well as in Georgia. Peter the Iberian represents the broader reach of Georgian culture and learning beyond the Caucasus — a reminder that Georgia was not merely a passive recipient of foreign influences but an active participant in the intellectual and religious life of the ancient and medieval world.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Christianization of Iberia (c. 327 AD)

The conversion of King Mirian III and the establishment of Christianity as the state religion of Iberia was a watershed moment not only in Georgian history but in the history of the entire Caucasus region. According to the Georgian tradition preserved in the "Conversion of Kartli," Saint Nino's preaching and miracles convinced first the queen, then the king, and finally the entire population to embrace Christianity. The conversion was formalized through the destruction of pagan idols and the construction of churches on their sites, most notably at Mtskheta, where a wooden church was built on the site of a pagan shrine, later replaced by the great Svetitskhoveli Cathedral.

The Christianization of Georgia had consequences that extended far beyond religion. It brought Georgia into the cultural orbit of the Roman/Byzantine world, introduced literacy (as the Bible and liturgical texts needed to be translated), and provided the institutional framework — the Church — that would preserve Georgian identity through centuries of foreign domination. The decision to adopt Christianity rather than Zoroastrianism (the religion of the dominant Sasanian Persian Empire) also had geopolitical implications, aligning Georgia with Rome against Persia and setting the pattern of East-West rivalry that would characterize Georgian politics for centuries.

### The Invention of the Georgian Alphabet (c. 5th century AD)

The creation of a unique Georgian alphabet is one of the most remarkable achievements of this era and one of the great intellectual accomplishments of late antiquity. The Georgian script consists of three distinct writing systems — Asomtavruli (capital letters), Nuskhuri (minuscule), and Mkhedruli (the modern secular script) — making it unique among the world's alphabets in having three historically successive but distinct forms. According to medieval tradition, the alphabet was invented by King Pharnavaz I in the 3rd century BC, but modern scholarship dates the earliest inscriptions to the 5th century AD, during the early Christian period.

The invention of the Georgian alphabet was almost certainly motivated by the need to translate Christian texts from Greek and Armenian into the Georgian language. The creation of a unique script — rather than simply adopting the Greek or Armenian alphabets — reflects a strong sense of cultural identity and a desire for linguistic independence. The earliest surviving Georgian inscriptions, found at the Bolnisi Sioni church (c. 490 AD) and the Davati Gorge, demonstrate a fully developed writing system from its earliest appearance. The Georgian alphabet remains one of only about twenty writing systems ever independently invented, and it is still used today, making it one of the world's oldest continuously used alphabets.

### The Battle of Dara and Iberian-Persian Wars (5th-6th centuries)

The Iberian kingdom spent much of this era caught in the struggle between the Byzantine and Sasanian Persian empires. The so-called "Lazic War" (541-562 AD) was particularly significant for Georgian history. Fought primarily in the western Georgian kingdom of Lazica (Egrisi), this conflict between Byzantium and Sasanian Persia saw Georgian forces fighting on both sides. The Byzantines ultimately prevailed, but at enormous cost, and the Georgian kingdoms were devastated by years of warfare.

The relentless pressure of the Byzantine-Sasanian rivalry eventually proved too much for Iberia. In 580 AD, the Byzantine emperor Maurice abolished the Iberian monarchy, and the kingdom was incorporated into the Byzantine administrative system. This marked the end of Iberia as an independent state, though Georgian princes continued to exercise local power. The abolition of the monarchy was a devastating blow, but it also freed Georgian political energy to develop in new directions, leading eventually to the emergence of the powerful medieval Georgian principalities that would reunite the country.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### The Georgian Alphabet and Early Literature

The invention of the Georgian alphabet enabled the development of a rich literary tradition. The earliest Georgian texts were translations of Christian scripture and liturgical works from Greek and Armenian, but Georgian authors soon began producing original works of theology, hagiography, and history. The 5th-century "Martyrdom of Saint Shushanik" by Iakob Tsurtaveli is considered the oldest surviving work of Georgian literature and is a remarkable document of early Georgian Christian culture. The development of Georgian literacy made possible the preservation and transmission of Georgian cultural identity across the centuries.

### Ecclesiastical Architecture

The early Christian period saw the development of a distinctive Georgian architectural tradition. Early Georgian churches adopted and adapted the basilica plan from the Roman world, but over time developed unique features including the tetraconch plan (a church with four apses arranged in a cross pattern), elaborate stone carving, and distinctive decorative motifs drawn from both Christian symbolism and pre-Christian Georgian artistic traditions. The churches of this period — such as the Bolnisi Sioni (c. 490 AD) and the early structures at Mtskheta — established the architectural vocabulary that would reach its full flowering in the great cathedral-building programs of the Golden Age.

### Ecclesiastical Autocephaly

In the 5th century, the Georgian Church achieved autocephaly (ecclesiastical independence), meaning it was self-governing rather than subordinate to another patriarchate. This was a significant achievement for a relatively young church in a small kingdom, and it reflected the importance of Christianity in Georgian national identity. The Catholicos of Mtskheta, the head of the Georgian Church, became one of the most powerful figures in the kingdom, and the Church served as a repository of learning, culture, and political legitimacy throughout Georgia's history.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### Svetitskhoveli Cathedral, Mtskheta

Svetitskhoveli (literally "the Life-Giving Pillar") is the most sacred cathedral in Georgia and one of the most important religious sites in the Christian Caucasus. According to tradition, the original church was built in the 4th century on the site where a cedar pillar — which contained Christ's robe — had performed miracles. The current structure, though much rebuilt over the centuries, retains elements dating to the 11th century. Located at the confluence of the Mtkvari and Aragvi rivers in the old capital of Mtskheta, Svetitskhoveli has been the coronation and burial church of Georgian kings for centuries. Its massive stone walls, elegant proportions, and rich decorative carving make it a masterpiece of Georgian ecclesiastical architecture.

### Jvari Monastery

Perched dramatically on a mountaintop at the confluence of the Mtkvari and Aragvi rivers, the Jvari Monastery is one of Georgia's most iconic landmarks. Built in the late 6th century (c. 586-605 AD) by King Erismtavari Stepanoz I, Jvari is a tetraconch church — a design with four apses arranged in the form of a cross — and represents one of the finest examples of early medieval Georgian ecclesiastical architecture. The church contains important early inscriptions in the Asomtavruli (capital) Georgian script. Its dramatic hilltop setting, overlooking the ancient capital of Mtskheta, has made it a symbol of Georgian Christianity and one of the country's most photographed and beloved monuments. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

### Bolnisi Sioni

Built in the late 5th century (c. 478-493 AD), the Bolnisi Sioni basilica is one of the oldest surviving church buildings in Georgia and contains some of the earliest known Georgian inscriptions. Located in the Kvemo Kartli region, about 80 km southwest of Tbilisi, Bolnisi Sioni is a three-aisled basilica built of dressed stone. The Georgian inscriptions on the church walls, written in the early Asomtavruli script, are among the most important documents for the study of the Georgian language and early Georgian Christianity. The church also features distinctive carved stone reliefs, including stylized animal and geometric motifs that show the blending of pre-Christian Georgian artistic traditions with new Christian iconography.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### Cavalry Warfare

The Iberian military was heavily influenced by both Roman and Iranian military traditions. Iberian cavalry was particularly esteemed in the ancient world — the Greek geographer Strabo noted that the Iberians provided cavalry forces to both the Roman and Persian empires. Iberian horsemen were typically equipped with spears, swords, and bows, and wore scale or mail armor for protection. The combination of heavy cavalry (cataphracts, modeled on Persian and later Byzantine heavy cavalry) and light horse archers (influenced by steppe warfare traditions) gave the Iberians a versatile and effective mounted force well suited to the varied terrain of the South Caucasus.

### Infantry and Siege Warfare

Iberian infantry fought with spears, swords, axes, and bows, and were organized into units that reflected both indigenous traditions and Roman influence. The Iberians were known as skilled fortress builders, and their hilltop fortifications — built of stone with massive walls, towers, and carefully positioned gateways — were formidable obstacles. Siege warfare, including the use of siege towers, battering rams, and other engines, was practiced by both the Iberians and their enemies. The rugged terrain of the Caucasus made siege warfare particularly important, as control of fortified positions was often the key to controlling the strategic mountain passes.

### Mountain Warfare

One of the distinctive features of Georgian military tradition in this period (and throughout Georgian history) was expertise in mountain warfare. The Caucasus Mountains provided both a defensive barrier and a strategic challenge, and control of the passes — particularly the Darial Gorge in the north and various routes through the Lesser Caucasus in the south — was essential for the defense of the kingdom. Georgian warriors developed specialized techniques for fighting in mountainous terrain, including the use of ambushes, rockfalls, and the construction of watchtowers and fortified outposts along key routes.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

Iberian clothing reflected the kingdom's position between Roman and Iranian cultural spheres. Elite men wore tunics, trousers (in the Iranian style), and cloaks fastened with elaborate brooches or fibulae. Boots and shoes were made of leather, often decorated. Women wore long, layered dresses with richly embroidered borders. Both sexes valued jewelry — gold rings, earrings, necklaces, and belt buckles — often decorated with techniques inherited from the Colchian goldworking tradition. Textiles included wool and linen, with silk becoming increasingly available through trade with the East. The aristocracy also adopted elements of Persian court dress, including elaborate headdresses.

### Food and Agriculture

The Iberian economy was fundamentally agricultural. The fertile valleys of the Mtkvari basin produced wheat, barley, millet, and legumes. Viticulture was enormously important — Georgia has one of the world's oldest and most distinguished winemaking traditions, and grape cultivation and wine production were central to Iberian economy and culture. The unique Georgian method of winemaking in large clay vessels called qvevri, buried underground, likely developed during this period or even earlier. Livestock — cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, and poultry — provided meat, dairy products, and wool. Orchards produced apples, pears, plums, cherries, and various nuts. The cuisine was enriched by herbs and spices traded from the East.

### Social Structure

Iberian society was organized hierarchically, with the king (mep'e) at the apex, supported by a powerful aristocracy (aznauri) who held land and commanded military retinues. Below the nobility were free commoners — farmers, craftsmen, and merchants — and at the bottom of the social scale were slaves and other unfree laborers. The Georgian Church added another layer of authority, with bishops and monks wielding significant social and political influence. The erosion of royal power in the later part of this period led to the rise of powerful noble families (the eristavi, or dukes) who increasingly dominated political life.

### Religion

The religious landscape of Iberia was transformed during this era, from the pagan polytheism of the early period to the triumphant Christianity of the later centuries. Pre-Christian Iberian religion involved the worship of deities associated with natural forces — the sun god Armazi, the moon god, and various local gods and spirits. Sacred groves, hilltop shrines, and the worship of ancestral spirits were important features. The conversion to Christianity in the 4th century did not entirely eradicate these earlier beliefs, which often persisted in modified forms. The Georgian Orthodox Church, with its distinctive liturgy, monastic tradition, and rich cycle of saints' days and festivals, became the central institution of Iberian cultural life and remains so to this day.

## GLOSSARY

**Kartli** — The Georgian name for the eastern Georgian kingdom known to classical sources as Iberia. Also the name of the central region of Georgia surrounding Tbilisi.

**Mtskheta** — The ancient capital of the Kingdom of Iberia, located at the confluence of the Mtkvari and Aragvi rivers. A UNESCO World Heritage Site and the spiritual center of Georgian Christianity.

**Mep'e** — Georgian word for "king." The ruler of Iberia held this title.

**Aznauri** — The Georgian aristocratic/noble class. Aznauri held land, commanded military forces, and exercised political power at both the royal and local levels.

**Eristavi** — A Georgian duke or governor. During the later Iberian period, eristavi became increasingly powerful as royal authority declined.

**Qvevri** — Large clay vessels used for fermenting and storing wine, buried underground. The qvevri winemaking tradition is unique to Georgia and is recognized by UNESCO as part of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.

**Mtkvari** — The largest river in the South Caucasus, flowing through Georgia from northeast to southwest. Known as the Kura in Western languages.

**Svetitskhoveli** — "The Life-Giving Pillar." The principal cathedral of the Georgian Orthodox Church, located in Mtskheta.

**Tbilisi** — The capital of Georgia, founded (according to tradition) by King Vakhtang Gorgasali in the 5th century. The name means "warm place," referring to the thermal springs.

**Samtskhe** — A historic region in southern Georgia that played an important role in the medieval period as a semi-independent principality.
