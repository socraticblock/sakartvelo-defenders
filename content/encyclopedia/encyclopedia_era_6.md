# Era 6: Russian Empire (~1801 - ~1918 AD)

## ERA OVERVIEW

The annexation of the Georgian kingdoms by the Russian Empire in 1801 marked the beginning of a new and profoundly transformative chapter in Georgian history. For the first time in centuries, the Georgian lands were united under a single authority — but that authority was foreign, imperial, and increasingly Russifying. The Russian period brought modernization, infrastructure development, and integration into the broader European economy and culture, but it also brought the suppression of Georgian political autonomy, the erosion of the Georgian Church's independence, and the gradual displacement of Georgian language and culture from positions of influence.

The annexation of the Kingdom of Kartli-Kakheti in 1801 (followed by the incorporation of the Kingdom of Imereti in 1810 and the principalities of Samegrelo, Guria, and Svaneti in subsequent decades) was justified by the Russians as a response to Georgian requests for protection, but it was in fact a straightforward imperial conquest. The Georgian royal family — the Bagrationi dynasty, which had ruled Georgia for a thousand years — was deposed and exiled to Russia. The Georgian Church, which had been autocephalous (self-governing) since the 5th century, was abolished as an independent institution and subordinated to the Russian Orthodox Church's synodal system in 1811. Georgian nobles were gradually integrated into the Russian aristocracy, expected to serve the empire and adopt Russian language and customs.

Despite these humiliations, the Russian period also brought significant benefits. The Russian military presence ended the devastating cycle of Persian and Ottoman invasions that had plagued Georgia for centuries. The Russians built roads (most famously the Georgian Military Highway connecting Tbilisi with Russia), railways, and telegraph lines that integrated Georgia into modern transportation and communication networks. New industries — oil, manganese, wine — were developed, and Georgia's economy grew substantially. Tbilisi was transformed into a cosmopolitan city, one of the most multiethnic and multicultural in the Russian Empire, with substantial Armenian, Russian, Azerbaijani, Persian, and European communities living alongside the Georgian majority.

The later decades of the 19th century saw the emergence of a Georgian national movement that challenged Russian domination and sought to restore Georgian cultural and political rights. The movement was led by a remarkable generation of Georgian intellectuals — writers, journalists, scholars, and political activists — who promoted the Georgian language, studied Georgian history, and organized political opposition to Russian policies. The movement gained momentum in the early 20th century, particularly after the Russian Revolution of 1905, which saw mass protests and strikes throughout Georgia. By the time the Russian Empire collapsed in 1917, Georgia was ready to reclaim its independence.

This era is complex and deeply contested in Georgian historical memory. Some Georgians view the Russian period as a time of modernization and progress that prepared Georgia for the modern world. Others see it as a colonial occupation that suppressed Georgian identity and exploited Georgian resources for the benefit of the empire. The truth contains elements of both perspectives, and understanding this era is essential to understanding modern Georgia's complicated relationship with Russia.

## KEY FIGURES

### General Alexi Ermolov (1777-1861)

Alexi Petrovich Ermolov was the Russian commander-in-chief in the Caucasus from 1816 to 1827, and his policies toward Georgia and the Caucasian peoples had a profound and lasting impact. Ermolov was an able but brutal military administrator who pursued a strategy of relentless expansion into the North Caucasus while maintaining tight Russian control over the Georgian territories. His "scorched earth" policies against the mountain peoples of the North Caucasus — particularly the Circassians and Chechens — were devastating, and his methods were characterized by a ruthlessness that even some of his contemporaries found excessive.

Ermolov's impact on Georgia itself was significant. He improved Tbilisi's infrastructure, established schools, and promoted economic development, but he also suppressed Georgian political aspirations and treated the Georgian nobility with suspicion. His approach to governance — centralized, military, and authoritarian — set the pattern for Russian administration in the Caucasus for decades to come. Ermolov's name remains controversial in Georgia: he is remembered both as a modernizer and as a symbol of Russian imperial oppression.

### Ilia Chavchavadze (1837-1907)

Ilia Chavchavadze is the most important figure in the modern Georgian national movement and is widely regarded as the father of modern Georgia. A nobleman, writer, journalist, and political leader, Ilia dedicated his life to the revival of Georgian language, culture, and national consciousness. He founded newspapers and literary journals, established the Society for the Spreading of Literacy Among Georgians, promoted the study of Georgian history, and organized political campaigns against Russian policies that threatened Georgian cultural rights.

Ilia's literary works — poems, short stories, essays, and his great novel "Is a Man a Man?" — explored themes of national identity, social justice, and individual dignity. His journalism and political activism challenged both Russian imperial policies and the traditional social order, advocating for education, women's rights, and economic development. His assassination in 1907 — likely ordered by the Russian secret police — was a national tragedy that galvanized the Georgian national movement. Ilia is canonized as a saint by the Georgian Orthodox Church, and his portrait appears on the Georgian lari currency. He is the central figure of modern Georgian cultural and political identity.

### Niko Nikoladze (1843-1928)

Niko Nikoladze was a Georgian writer, journalist, and political activist who played a major role in the development of modern Georgian political thought. Educated in Russia and Western Europe, Nikoladze was a progressive thinker who advocated for liberal democracy, economic modernization, and social reform. He served as mayor of Poti and later as a member of the Russian State Duma (parliament), where he was a vocal advocate for Georgian rights.

Nikoladze's significance lies in his efforts to connect the Georgian national movement with broader European liberal and democratic traditions. He argued that Georgia's future lay in the development of democratic institutions, a modern economy, and a society based on the rule of law. His vision of Georgia as a modern, European-oriented democratic state was profoundly influential and helped shape the political culture of the First Democratic Republic that would emerge after World War I. Nikoladze was also a pioneer of Georgian urban planning — the coastal town of Anaklia was designed by him.

### Saint Nino (Nino Chavchavadze) (1812-1857)

Nino Chavchavadze (often called "the Georgian Nino" to distinguish her from the ancient saint) was a Georgian aristocrat and philanthropist whose life story became a symbol of Georgian national feeling. She married the famous Russian diplomat and writer Alexander Griboyedov in 1828, but he was killed in a riot in Tehran just months after their wedding. Nino spent the rest of her life in Georgia, devoting herself to philanthropy and caring for her husband's memory. She was widely admired for her beauty, intelligence, and devotion to Georgian culture.

Nino's grave at the Mtatsminda Pantheon in Tbilisi, marked by a simple cross and the inscription "Cease your tears, my soul, I am immortal," became a place of pilgrimage and national veneration. She was the mother of the great writer Grigol Orbeliani and was related to many of the leading figures of the Georgian national movement. Her life embodied the intersection of Georgian and Russian culture that characterized this era, and her image as a tragic but noble figure resonated deeply with the Georgian national consciousness.

### Ekaterine (Catherine) Dadiani (1816-1882)

Princess Ekaterine Dadiani was the last ruling princess of the Principality of Samegrelo in western Georgia and one of the most remarkable women in 19th-century Georgian history. Following the death of her husband, Prince David Dadiani, in 1853, Ekaterine ruled Samegrelo as regent for her young son. During the Crimean War (1853-1856), she skillfully managed the complex diplomacy between Russia, the Ottoman Empire, and the local Caucasian powers, maintaining Samegrelo's position despite enormous pressure.

Ekaterine was known for her intelligence, political acumen, and cultural patronage. She supported the development of education and infrastructure in Samegrelo and maintained one of the finest courts in the Caucasus. After the formal abolition of the Samegrelo principality by Russia in 1857 (during which she was briefly exiled), she lived in Russia and Europe but remained an important figure in Georgian society. Her palace in Zugdidi, with its remarkable botanical garden and collection of art and manuscripts, is now a museum. Ekaterine's life illustrates the complex position of Georgian elites in the Russian imperial system — powerful and privileged within the empire but ultimately subordinate to Russian authority.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Annexation of Kartli-Kakheti (1801)

On January 18, 1801, Tsar Paul I of Russia issued a manifesto formally abolishing the Kingdom of Kartli-Kakheti and incorporating it into the Russian Empire. The manifesto was justified as a response to a request from the Georgian nobility for Russian protection, but the request had been obtained under duress, and the deposition of the Georgian royal family violated the terms of the Treaty of Georgievsk (1783). The Georgian royal family — the Bagrationi dynasty — was exiled to Russia, and Georgia was placed under direct Russian military administration.

The annexation was a seismic event in Georgian history. It ended over a thousand years of Bagrationi royal rule and reduced Georgia from an independent (if weakened) kingdom to a province of a foreign empire. The Georgian nobility was divided in its response — some accepted Russian rule as a necessary evil, others actively resisted. Rebellions in 1804, 1812, and 1832 sought to restore Georgian independence but were brutally suppressed. The annexation set the pattern for the Russian period: modernization and development coexisting with political subordination and cultural suppression.

### The Abolition of the Georgian Church's Autocephaly (1811)

In 1811, the Russian authorities abolished the autocephaly (self-governing status) of the Georgian Orthodox Church, which had been independent since the 5th century. The Catholicos-Patriarch of Georgia was removed from office and not replaced. The Georgian Church was subordinated to the Russian Orthodox Church's Holy Synod and placed under the authority of a Russian-appointed exarch. Georgian liturgical practices were gradually Russified, and the use of the Georgian language in church services was restricted in some contexts.

The abolition of church autocephaly was one of the most deeply resented actions of the Russian period. The Georgian Orthodox Church had been the central institution of Georgian national identity for over 1,500 years, and its subordination to a foreign church was experienced as an attack on Georgian identity itself. The struggle for the restoration of church autocephaly would become a central demand of the Georgian national movement and would not be achieved until 1917.

### The 1832 Conspiracy

The 1832 Conspiracy was a secret plot by a group of Georgian nobles and intellectuals to overthrow Russian rule and restore Georgian independence. The conspirators, who included members of the Orbeliani, Eristavi, and other prominent families, planned to assassinate the Russian governor-general and his senior officers during a ball in Tbilisi and then declare Georgian independence. The plot was betrayed by one of the conspirators, and the leaders were arrested before the plan could be executed.

The failure of the 1832 Conspiracy marked the end of the armed resistance phase of Georgian opposition to Russian rule. The severity of the Russian response — arrests, exiles, and confiscations — convinced most Georgian nobles that military resistance was futile. Opposition increasingly shifted to cultural and political channels — the promotion of Georgian language and literature, the study of Georgian history, and the gradual development of a modern national movement. The conspirators' ideal of an independent Georgia would not die, but it would take different forms in the decades to come.

### The 1905 Revolution in Georgia

The Russian Revolution of 1905 had a particularly strong impact in Georgia, where mass protests, strikes, and armed uprisings challenged Russian authority. Georgian workers, peasants, and intellectuals participated actively in the revolutionary movement, and the country saw widespread unrest including strikes in the oil fields of Batumi, peasant uprisings in Guria (the "Gurian Republic" of 1902-1906, a peasant self-government movement), and armed clashes in Tbilisi and other cities. The Russian government responded with brutal repression, including mass arrests, executions, and punitive expeditions against rebellious villages.

The 1905 Revolution was a watershed in Georgian political development. It demonstrated the strength of popular opposition to Russian rule and the depth of Georgian political consciousness. It also led to the emergence of organized political parties — the Mensheviks (social democrats) became particularly strong in Georgia, advocating for social democracy within a framework of national self-determination. The experience of 1905 prepared Georgian political leaders for the events of 1917-1918, when the collapse of the Russian Empire would finally create the opportunity for Georgian independence.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### The National Revival and the Georgian Language

The most significant cultural achievement of this era was the preservation and revitalization of the Georgian language against the pressures of Russification. The Society for the Spreading of Literacy Among Georgians, founded in 1869, established schools throughout Georgia and published textbooks in Georgian. Georgian writers like Ilia Chavchavadze, Akaki Tsereteli, Vazha-Pshavela, and Galaktion Tabidze created a modern literary tradition that was both deeply rooted in Georgian cultural heritage and engaged with contemporary European literary movements. The publication of newspapers and journals in Georgian provided a platform for public debate and the development of modern Georgian political thought.

### Theater and Opera

Georgia developed a vibrant theatrical tradition during this period. The Tbilisi Opera and Ballet Theatre, founded in 1851, became one of the premier cultural institutions in the Caucasus. Georgian composers like Zacharia Paliashvili (whose opera "Abesalom and Eteri" is a masterpiece of Georgian music) created a distinctive national musical tradition. The Georgian theater scene produced notable actors and directors who performed both Georgian and European works, and the theater became an important venue for the expression of national identity.

### The Tbilisi Renaissance

The late 19th and early 20th centuries saw a cultural flowering in Tbilisi that made the city one of the most dynamic cultural centers in the Russian Empire. The city's multiethnic character — with Georgian, Armenian, Russian, Azerbaijani, Persian, and European communities — created a rich, cosmopolitan cultural environment. Art nouveau architecture flourished, cafes and theaters multiplied, and the city attracted artists and intellectuals from across the empire. The painters Niko Pirosmani (a self-taught primitivist genius) and Lado Gudiashvili, the poet Galaktion Tabidze, and the composer Dimitri Arakishvili were among the many artists who contributed to this remarkable cultural moment.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### The Georgian Military Highway

The Georgian Military Highway, connecting Tbilisi with Vladikavkaz in Russia through the Darial Gorge, was built by the Russian military between 1799 and 1864. The road, which climbs to an altitude of 2,379 meters at the Darial Pass, was one of the greatest engineering achievements of its era in the Caucasus. It had enormous strategic and economic significance, connecting Georgia to the Russian heartland and facilitating trade, troop movements, and cultural exchange. The road passes through some of the most spectacular mountain scenery in the Caucasus, including the dramatic Darial Gorge and the confluence of the Terek and Sno rivers. It remains one of the most important roads in Georgia and a popular tourist route.

### Sioni Cathedral, Tbilisi

The Sioni Cathedral of the Dormition, located in the historic district of Tbilisi, is one of the most important churches in Georgia. Originally built in the 6th-7th centuries, the cathedral was destroyed and rebuilt multiple times over the centuries. The current structure dates primarily to the 13th century, with significant modifications in the 17th-19th centuries. The cathedral is notable as the repository of the sacred cross of Saint Nino, the most important relic of the Georgian Orthodox Church. During the Russian period, Sioni served as the seat of the Russian-appointed Exarch of Georgia. After the restoration of Georgian autocephaly in 1917, it became the temporary seat of the Catholicos-Patriarch until the reconsecration of Sameba Cathedral.

### Freedom Square (Goris Pani) and its Surroundings

The central square of Tbilisi, known as Freedom Square (formerly Erivan Square and Lenin Square), was developed during the Russian period into the political and commercial heart of the city. The square is dominated by the Town Hall (built in the neoclassical style in the 1830s) and framed by elegant 19th-century buildings in a variety of styles including neoclassical, art nouveau, and eclectic. The square has been the site of many of the most important events in modern Georgian history, including the declaration of independence in 1918, the 1991 independence referendum, and the Rose Revolution of 2003. The surrounding streets — Rustaveli Avenue, Leselidze Street — contain some of the finest 19th-century architecture in the Caucasus.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### Modernization of the Georgian Military

Under Russian rule, Georgian military forces were integrated into the Imperial Russian Army. Georgian soldiers served in regular Russian army units, and Georgian cavalry — particularly the Georgian Cossack-style mounted units — were valued for their skill and bravery. The Russian period introduced modern military technology to Georgia, including rifled muskets, artillery, and eventually machine guns. Georgian officers were trained in Russian military academies, and some rose to high rank in the imperial army. The military experience gained by Georgians in the Russian army would prove valuable when Georgia established its own national army after independence in 1918.

### The Caucasian War and Mountain Warfare

The long and brutal Caucasian War (1817-1864), in which Russian forces systematically conquered the mountain peoples of the North Caucasus, was largely fought from Georgian territory and involved many Georgian troops. The war was characterized by guerrilla warfare in extremely difficult mountain terrain, and the Russian military developed specialized techniques for mountain operations that drew on the experience of Georgian mountain warfare traditions. The war also brought Imam Shamil's forces into conflict with Georgian border communities, leading to raids and skirmishes along the Georgian-Caucasian frontier.

### Naval Development

The Russian period saw the development of naval facilities on Georgia's Black Sea coast. The port of Poti was developed as a commercial and military harbor, and the naval base at Batumi (after its acquisition from the Ottomans in 1878) became an important component of the Russian Black Sea Fleet. Georgian sailors served in the Russian navy, and the Black Sea coast's strategic importance was a significant factor in the region's economic development. The Batumi-Baku railway, connecting the Black Sea with the Caspian, was one of the most important infrastructure projects of the Russian period, facilitating the export of Azerbaijani oil through Georgian ports.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

Georgian dress during the Russian period underwent a gradual transformation from traditional to European styles, though traditional dress persisted in rural areas and on formal occasions. The chokha (cherkeska) remained the standard formal wear for Georgian men, particularly the aristocracy, and its distinctive silhouette — with the decorative cartridge holders across the chest — became an iconic symbol of Georgian identity. However, urban Georgians increasingly adopted European suits and dresses for everyday wear. The mixed cultural environment of Tbilisi and other cities produced eclectic fashions that blended Georgian, Russian, Persian, and European elements.

### Food and Urban Life

The Russian period saw significant changes in Georgian food culture. The cities, particularly Tbilisi, developed vibrant restaurant and cafe cultures that blended Georgian, Russian, Armenian, Persian, and European culinary traditions. The famous Tbilisi markets — the Dezerter Bazaar (so called because it was where deserters from the Russian army sold their goods) and other markets — offered a dazzling variety of local and imported foods. The supra (feast) tradition continued to be central to Georgian social life, and the tamada (toastmaster) tradition was maintained with great seriousness. Russian dishes like borscht and pelmeni entered the Georgian culinary repertoire, while Georgian dishes like khachapuri and khinkali became popular throughout the Russian Empire.

### Social Structure

Russian rule transformed Georgian social structure. The traditional aristocracy was gradually integrated into the Russian nobility, with Georgian princes and nobles receiving Russian titles and privileges. However, the abolition of the Georgian kingdoms and principalities meant that the old political structures disappeared, and Georgian nobles became dependent on the Russian imperial system for their status. A new middle class of merchants, professionals, and intellectuals emerged in the cities, particularly in Tbilisi. The peasantry, who made up the vast majority of the population, remained poor and often oppressed, leading to the peasant unrest that characterized the later decades of the Russian period. The multiethnic character of Georgian cities — with substantial Armenian, Russian, Azerbaijani, and other communities — created a complex social landscape.

### Education and Intellectual Life

The Russian period saw a dramatic expansion of education in Georgia, though this education was often conducted in Russian. Russian-language schools were established throughout the country, providing access to European scientific and literary knowledge. However, the Georgian national movement fought to preserve and expand Georgian-language education. The Society for the Spreading of Literacy Among Georgians established Georgian-language schools and published textbooks. The Tbilisi Theological Seminary (where a young Ioseb Jughashvili, later known as Joseph Stalin, studied) was an important educational institution. Georgian intellectuals increasingly studied at Russian and European universities, bringing back new ideas about literature, science, and politics.

## GLOSSARY

**Goris Pani** — "Freedom Square." The central square of Tbilisi, known by various names during the Russian period. Site of many key events in modern Georgian history.

**Tbilisi** — The capital of Georgia, which grew into a major cosmopolitan city during the Russian period, with diverse ethnic and cultural communities.

**Guria** — A western Georgian region that became famous for the "Gurian Republic," a peasant self-government movement (1902-1906) that was one of the earliest experiments in democratic self-rule in the Russian Empire.

**Sameba** — "Holy Trinity." The name of the main cathedral of the Georgian Orthodox Church in Tbilisi, built in the early 21st century.

**Mtatsminda** — "Holy Mountain." The mountain overlooking Tbilisi, topped by the church of Saint David and the Pantheon of Georgian writers and public figures.

**Dezerter Bazaar** — A major market in Tbilisi, named for the Russian army deserters who sold goods there in the 19th century.

**Zugdidi** — A city in western Georgia, the historical capital of the Principality of Samegrelo. The Dadiani Palace in Zugdidi houses an important museum.

**Manglisi** — A town in southern Georgia with an ancient cathedral. During the Russian period, it was an important center of Georgian ecclesiastical life.

**Pirveli Dasi** — "The First Group." A circle of young Georgian intellectuals in the 1860s who advocated for modernization while preserving Georgian national identity.

**Tergdaleulebi** — "Those who drank from the Terek." Georgian intellectuals who studied in Russia and returned with modern ideas. Named after the Terek River they crossed.
