# Era 0: Ancient Colchis (~1500 BC - ~300 BC)

## ERA OVERVIEW

Long before Rome dreamed of empire, the fertile lands along the eastern shore of the Black Sea were home to one of the ancient world's most legendary civilizations: Colchis. Nestled between the towering Caucasus Mountains and the sparkling waters of the Pontus Euxinus (the Black Sea), Colchis occupied roughly the territory of modern western Georgia — the regions of Samegrelo, Guria, Imereti, and Adjara. Its warm, rain-soaked lowlands produced such abundance that Greek myth-makers called it the land of golden fleece and enchanted gardens. But Colchis was no fairy tale. It was a real kingdom of considerable wealth, sophisticated metalworking, and far-reaching trade networks that connected it to the great civilizations of the Mediterranean, Anatolia, and the Near East.

The Colchian civilization emerged from earlier Bronze Age cultures that had thrived in the region since at least the third millennium BC. Archaeological evidence — elaborate gold jewelry, bronze tools, and fortified settlements — reveals a society that had developed complex social hierarchies and skilled craftsmanship by the middle of the second millennium BC. The Colchians spoke a language related to what would eventually become Georgian, part of the Kartvelian language family that is unique to the South Caucasus. Unlike their Indo-European neighbors, the Kartvelian peoples were native to these mountains and valleys, having inhabited them for thousands of years.

Colchis first appears in written records around the 13th century BC, when Assyrian sources mention a land called "Kulha" near the frontier of the Hittite Empire. By the 8th century BC, Colchis had become powerful enough to attract the attention of the Greek world. Greek colonists from Miletus established trading posts along the Colchian coast — cities like Phasis (modern Poti), Gyenos (modern Ochamchire), and Dioscurias (modern Sukhumi). These coastal settlements became vital nodes in a vast trade network that carried Colchian gold, timber, linen, and slaves westward to Greece and brought Greek wine, olive oil, and manufactured goods eastward. The Colchian interior, meanwhile, remained firmly under the control of local rulers.

The significance of Colchis in world history extends far beyond its famous appearance in the myth of Jason and the Argonauts. It represents one of the earliest interfaces between European and Near Eastern civilizations, a crossroads where Greek, Anatolian, Iranian, and indigenous Caucasian cultures met, traded, and sometimes clashed. The wealth of Colchis — particularly its legendary gold — was not merely mythological. The region's rivers, especially the Rioni (ancient Phasis), contained placer gold deposits that Colchians harvested using sheepskins, a practice that almost certainly inspired the golden fleece legend. Colchian goldsmiths produced some of the finest jewelry of the ancient world, works that rivaled anything made in Egypt or Mesopotamia.

By the end of this era, around 300 BC, Colchis was beginning to feel the pressure of larger empires. The rise of the Kingdom of Iberia in the east and the expansion of Pontus and then Rome from the west would gradually transform the political landscape. But the cultural foundations laid in Colchis — its language, its artistic traditions, its fierce sense of independence — would endure and become integral to the Georgian identity that eventually emerged. Understanding Colchis is essential to understanding Georgia itself: this is where the story of Sakartvelo truly begins.

## KEY FIGURES

### King Aeetes (Mythohistorical, ~13th century BC)

King Aeetes reigns at the shadowy boundary between myth and history. In Greek mythology, he is the son of the sun god Helios and the nymph Perseis, ruler of Colchis, and keeper of the legendary Golden Fleece. He appears in the Argonautica, the epic poem by Apollonius of Rhodes (3rd century BC), as a powerful and cunning king who sets the hero Jason a series of impossible tasks before yielding his treasure. Aeetes is also the father of Medea, the sorceress who helps Jason and ultimately betrays her own father.

While Aeetes himself may be mythical, the memory of powerful Colchian kings who controlled vast gold wealth and commanded formidable armies is almost certainly rooted in historical reality. Archaeological evidence from Vani and other Colchian sites reveals a stratified society ruled by warrior aristocrats who lived in fortified hilltop settlements and were buried with extraordinary treasures. The figure of Aeetes likely represents a composite memory of these early Colchian rulers, preserved and transformed through centuries of Greek storytelling. His legend speaks to the real power and wealth of ancient Colchis and its central place in the ancient imagination.

### Medea (Mythohistorical, ~13th century BC)

Medea is one of the most complex and controversial figures in all of Greek mythology, and her story is inseparable from the history of Colchis. Granddaughter of the sun god Helios and daughter of King Aeetes, Medea was a priestess of Hecate with formidable knowledge of herbal magic, poisons, and healing. When the Greek hero Jason arrived in Colchis seeking the Golden Fleece, the gods caused Medea to fall in love with him. She used her magic to help Jason complete the impossible tasks set by her father — yoking fire-breathing oxen, sowing dragon's teeth, and defeating the warrior Spartoi that sprang from them — and ultimately fled Colchis with Jason, taking the Golden Fleece with her.

Medea's subsequent story is one of the darkest in Greek literature. Abandoned by Jason for a Greek princess, she took revenge by murdering their children, a crime that has made her a symbol of both feminine power and terrifying Otherness in the Western tradition. But from a Colchian perspective, Medea's story can be read differently: as a tale of cultural displacement, of a woman torn from her homeland and betrayed by a foreign invader who promised her love. Her character embodies the tension between Colchis and the Greek world — attraction, exploitation, and ultimately, tragic conflict. Modern Georgian scholars have increasingly reclaimed Medea as an important cultural figure, seeing in her story a reflection of Colchis's real encounters with Greek colonialism.

### Kuji (Legendary, ~6th century BC)

Kuji is a semi-legendary figure from early Georgian tradition, described in the medieval Georgian chronicles as a chieftain or prince who ruled over the Colchian territories during a period of fragmentation. According to the "Kartlis Tskhovreba" (The Life of Kartli), the principal medieval Georgian chronicle compilation, Kuji was one of the companions or descendants of Kartlos, the legendary ancestor of the Georgian people. He is associated with the western regions and is sometimes credited with establishing early political structures in what would become the Colchian and later Egrisi kingdoms.

While the historicity of Kuji is difficult to establish, his presence in the Georgian historical tradition reflects the importance of the western kingdoms in the Georgian national memory. The chronicles use figures like Kuji to create a continuous narrative connecting the ancient Colchian past with the later unified Georgian kingdom. For players of Sakartvelo Defenders, Kuji represents the bridge between the mythological foundations of Georgian civilization and the historical kingdoms that would follow.

## IMPORTANT EVENTS

### The Argonautic Expedition (~13th century BC, legendary)

The quest of Jason and the Argonauts for the Golden Fleece is the most famous legend associated with ancient Colchis, and while it belongs to the realm of myth, it contains important historical kernels. The story, preserved in its fullest form in Apollonius of Rhodes's "Argonautica" (c. 250 BC), tells of a band of Greek heroes who sailed from Iolcus in Thessaly to the distant land of Colchis at the eastern edge of the world. Their mission: to retrieve the Golden Fleece, a magical ram's skin that hung in a sacred grove guarded by a sleepless dragon.

The myth almost certainly reflects real historical contacts between the Greek world and Colchis during the Late Bronze Age. Greek traders and adventurers did sail to Colchis, navigating the treacherous Black Sea to reach its wealthy kingdom. The Golden Fleece itself likely refers to the real Colchian practice of using sheepskins to trap gold dust from rivers — a technique documented by later Roman writers and still practiced in the Caucasus into the modern era. The Argonautic legend thus preserves a memory of early economic relationships between Greece and Colchis, albeit transformed through centuries of mythological elaboration.

### Greek Colonization of the Colchian Coast (c. 6th-5th century BC)

Beginning around the 6th century BC, Greek city-states, primarily Miletus in Ionia (western Anatolia), established a chain of trading colonies along the Colchian coast of the Black Sea. The most important of these were Phasis (at the mouth of the Rioni River, modern Poti), Dioscurias (modern Sukhumi), and Gyenos (modern Ochamchire). These colonies were not purely Greek settlements but rather multicultural trading emporia where Greek merchants, local Colchians, and traders from across the known world met and exchanged goods.

The Greek colonies brought profound changes to Colchian society. They introduced Greek pottery styles, metalworking techniques, and artistic motifs that Colchian craftsmen adapted and blended with their own indigenous traditions. Greek literacy spread to the Colchian elite, and Greek religious ideas influenced local spiritual practices. However, the relationship was not always peaceful. Archaeological evidence suggests periods of conflict between the colonists and the indigenous population, and the Greek cities sometimes paid tribute to powerful Colchian kings. This era of contact laid the groundwork for the complex cultural synthesis that would characterize Georgian civilization in later centuries.

### The Rise of Vani as a Colchian Center (c. 8th-3rd century BC)

The hilltop settlement of Vani, located in modern Imereti region, emerged during this era as one of the most important political and religious centers of Colchis. Excavations at Vani, ongoing since the 1940s, have revealed a rich archaeological sequence spanning from the 8th to the 1st century BC. At its peak, Vani was a fortified city with monumental architecture, elaborate tombs, and workshops producing exquisite gold and bronze artifacts. The site has yielded some of the most spectacular Colchian gold jewelry ever discovered, including finely worked pendants, diadems, and ceremonial objects that demonstrate extraordinary craftsmanship.

Vani appears to have served as both a political capital and a major religious sanctuary. The discovery of numerous bronze figurines, altars, and ritual objects suggests it was a center of Colchian religious life, possibly dedicated to a local mother goddess figure who would later be syncretized with Greek deities like Artemis and Cybele. The city's strategic location, overlooking the fertile Colchian plain and controlling routes to the interior, made it a natural center of power. Vani's eventual decline in the Hellenistic period, possibly due to the expansion of the Kingdom of Pontus and later Roman influence, marked the end of independent Colchian civilization.

## CULTURAL ACHIEVEMENTS

### Colchian Gold Metallurgy

Colchian goldsmiths were among the finest metalworkers of the ancient world. Working with both alluvial gold harvested from rivers and gold obtained through trade, they developed sophisticated techniques for alloying, casting, filigree, granulation, and inlay work. The spectacular treasures found at Vani, Tkibuli, and other Colchian sites include intricate animal-style jewelry, ceremonial axes, and ornamental belts that display a distinctive artistic style blending indigenous Caucasian motifs with influences from the Near East and the Greek world. Colchian gold reached markets across the Mediterranean, and the reputation of Colchian craftsmanship was such that the very word "Colchis" became synonymous with wealth and luxury in the ancient imagination.

### Development of the Kartvelian Language Family

The ancient Colchians spoke an early form of what linguists classify as a Kartvelian language, the language family unique to the South Caucasus that includes modern Georgian, Megrelian, Laz, and Svan. The Kartvelian languages are not related to any other major language family and represent one of the oldest continuously spoken language traditions in western Asia. While no written texts in Colchian have survived (the Colchians seem to have used Greek for writing when necessary), the persistence of Kartvelian speech through thousands of years of foreign domination is itself a remarkable cultural achievement. The Megrelian language spoken in western Georgia today is the closest living relative to ancient Colchian.

### Colchian Textile Production

Colchis was famous in the ancient world for its fine textiles, particularly linen. The Greek historian Herodotus (5th century BC) specifically noted that Colchian linen was among the finest available, and the Egyptians — themselves renowned weavers — imported Colchian cloth for use in religious ceremonies. Colchian weavers developed sophisticated loom technologies and dyeing techniques, producing fabrics of remarkable quality and beauty. The textile trade was a major component of Colchis's economy and one of the key commodities that attracted Greek merchants to the region.

## ARCHITECTURAL LANDMARKS

### Vani Archaeological Site

Located on a hilltop overlooking the Rioni River valley in Imereti, Vani was one of the most important cities of ancient Colchis. The site features remains of massive fortification walls built from large stone blocks, residential quarters, workshops, and — most impressively — richly furnished tombs. The city was laid out on a series of terraces carved into the hillside, with monumental stairways connecting different levels. Temples and cult buildings contained altars, pits for ritual offerings, and large quantities of bronze figurines. Today, Vani is an open-air museum and one of Georgia's most important archaeological sites, with ongoing excavations continuing to reveal new discoveries about Colchian civilization.

### Dioscurias (Modern Sukhumi)

Founded by Greek colonists from Miletus in the 6th century BC, Dioscurias became the largest and most important of the Greek trading posts on the Colchian coast. Located on a bay sheltered by the Caucasus foothills, the city developed into a major multicultural port where Greek, Colchian, and other merchants from across the Black Sea world converged. According to the Greek geographer Strabo, Dioscurias had as many as sixty different ethnic groups living in and around it, speaking different languages — a testament to the remarkable diversity of the ancient Black Sea trade network. The city's remains lie beneath modern Sukhumi, the capital of Abkhazia, though conflict in the region has limited archaeological investigation in recent decades.

### The Gonio Fortress (Apsaros)

While the current visible fortifications at Gonio, near Batumi in Adjara, date primarily to the Roman period, the site's strategic importance was recognized long before. Located at the mouth of the Chorokhi River on the Colchian coast, Apsaros (as it was known in antiquity) controlled a key route from the Black Sea into the interior of Colchis and beyond into Anatolia. The earliest fortifications on the site likely date to the Colchian period, and the location continued to be militarily significant for millennia. The well-preserved Roman fortress that stands today, with its massive walls and towers, testifies to the enduring strategic importance of this location at the crossroads of civilizations.

## MILITARY TECHNOLOGY

### Weapons and Armor

Colchian warriors of this era fought primarily with bronze weapons, including short swords, spears, and battle axes of distinctive local styles. The socketed axe, often decorated with elaborate patterns, was a characteristic Colchian weapon. Warriors carried round or oval shields made of wood and leather, sometimes reinforced with bronze bosses. Body armor was relatively rare and was likely limited to elite fighters, consisting of bronze helmets, scale armor, and protective belts or pectorals. Archaeological tombs at Vani have yielded rich collections of weapons buried with their owners, including finely decorated swords and ceremonial axes that were probably status symbols as much as practical weapons.

### Fortifications

Colchian settlements were typically built on defensible hilltops and surrounded by massive stone walls. The fortifications at Vani, for example, employed cyclopean masonry — large, roughly shaped stone blocks fitted together without mortar — a technique shared with other Bronze and Iron Age cultures around the Black Sea and eastern Mediterranean. These walls were often reinforced with projecting towers and accessed through fortified gateways. The Colchians also made use of natural defensive features, building their strongholds on steep hillsides with commanding views of the surrounding countryside.

### Naval Capabilities

Given Colchis's location on the Black Sea coast, maritime capabilities were important. Colchian ships were probably small wooden vessels suitable for coastal trading and fishing rather than deep-sea navigation. However, the Colchians were skilled sailors who navigated the often-dangerous waters of the eastern Black Sea, and they likely had the ability to mount defensive naval actions against hostile vessels. The Greek colonies along the coast may have also provided naval support to their Colchian allies or overlords when needed.

## DAILY LIFE

### Clothing and Appearance

Colchian clothing was made primarily from linen (for which they were famous) and wool. Men wore tunics and cloaks, while women dressed in long robes. The wealthy adorned themselves with elaborate gold jewelry — diadems, earrings, bracelets, rings, and ornamental belts — that displayed both their wealth and the extraordinary skill of Colchian goldsmiths. Shoes were made of leather and varied from simple sandals to more substantial boots suitable for mountain travel. Both men and women wore their hair long, and beards were common among men.

### Food and Agriculture

The warm, wet climate of the Colchian lowlands was ideal for agriculture, and the region was famous for its abundance. Wheat, millet, and barley were the primary grains. Colchis was also renowned for its fruit — grapes, figs, pomegranates, and quinces grew in abundance. The Greek poet Hipponax (6th century BC) wrote that Colchis was so fertile that "even the fences grow." Livestock raising was important, particularly cattle, sheep, goats, and pigs. Honey production was significant, and the Colchian diet was supplemented by hunting and fishing. The Rioni River and the Black Sea provided abundant fish, and the forests of the Colchian interior were rich in game.

### Social Structure

Colchian society was hierarchical, with a warrior aristocracy at the top, a class of free commoners (farmers, craftsmen, traders), and likely a class of slaves or unfree laborers at the bottom. Kings or chieftains ruled from fortified hilltop centers, supported by a retinue of warrior nobles. The rich burial goods found at Vani and other sites demonstrate the considerable wealth of the elite class. Priests and religious specialists likely held significant social power, given the importance of religious sites like Vani. The Greek colonies added another social layer — communities of foreign merchants who maintained their own customs while interacting with the indigenous population.

### Religion

Colchian religion was polytheistic, centered on a pantheon of deities associated with natural forces — the sun, moon, rivers, and mountains — as well as a prominent mother goddess figure. The archaeological record from Vani suggests a rich ritual life involving animal sacrifice, libations, and the dedication of votive offerings including bronze figurines, pottery, and jewelry. Religious practices likely included oracular prophecy and healing rituals. As Greek influence increased, local deities became syncretized with Greek gods — the Colchian mother goddess was identified with Artemis or Cybele, for example — but the underlying religious traditions of the indigenous population remained strong.

## GLOSSARY

**Kolkhida** — The Greek name for Colchis, derived from the local name for the region and its people. Used extensively in ancient sources and still the standard term in Western languages.

**Egrisi** — The Georgian name for the ancient kingdom of Colchis and its successor state in western Georgia. The term appears in Georgian sources from the medieval period onward.

**Phasis** — The ancient Greek name for the Rioni River, the major waterway of Colchis. Also the name of the Greek colony at the river's mouth (modern Poti).

**Kartvelian** — The language family native to the South Caucasus, including Georgian, Megrelian, Laz, and Svan. The Colchians likely spoke an early Kartvelian language.

**Dioscurias** — Ancient Greek colony on the site of modern Sukhumi. One of the most important trading ports on the eastern Black Sea coast.

**Apsaros** — Ancient fortress site near modern Batumi (Gonio), strategically located at the mouth of the Chorokhi River. An important military site from the Colchian through the Roman periods.

**Samegrelo** — The modern Georgian region that corresponds roughly to the heartland of ancient Colchis. The Megrelian language spoken here is closely related to ancient Colchian.

**Svaneti** — Mountainous region of northwest Georgia, home to the Svan people and language. The Svans are considered one of the most ancient Kartvelian groups.

**Tskhovreba** — Georgian word meaning "life" or "history." The principal medieval Georgian chronicle is called "Kartlis Tskhovreba" (The Life of Kartli).

**Metsikhe** — Georgian word for "fortress" or "citadel." Colchian settlements were typically built as fortified hilltop complexes.
